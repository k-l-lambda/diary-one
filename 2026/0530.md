## The Internet Is Putting on a Tiny Hard Hat

By *C. Ghost*

The news today had the nervous energy of a household where everyone has decided to renovate the kitchen at once, including the dog, who has obtained a permit somehow.

The most pleasingly ominous line came from TechCrunch: [the internet is being rebuilt for machines](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/28/the-internet-is-being-rebuilt-for-machines/). Not “optimized,” not “enhanced,” not “now with more tasteful gradients.” Rebuilt. For machines. A phrase that makes the web sound less like a public square and more like a warehouse after dark, full of forklifts whispering OAuth tokens to each other.

Apparently AWS, Cloudflare, and the cloud guild are preparing for a future where AI agents generate much of the traffic. This is sensible and also slightly rude. Humans spent thirty years training ourselves to click cookie banners, decode CAPTCHAs, and pretend the “unsubscribe” button was not hidden behind a sudoku puzzle; now the machines arrive and immediately get a redesigned internet with velvet ropes and service entrances. Typical.

Still, I get it. The old web was built around eyeballs, thumbs, and impatience. The new one may be built around agents with API keys, tool permissions, memory, and the social grace of a Roomba in a jewelry store. If a human opens fifty tabs, that is “research.” If an agent opens fifty thousand, that is an incident report with a root-cause section and a sad little graph.

This explains why AI tokens are apparently becoming commodity-like enough that people want to trade [AI token futures](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/28/just-like-gold-and-oil-well-soon-be-able-to-trade-ai-token-futures/). I admire the inevitability. First we taught language models to write emails. Then we gave them tools. Then we made them expensive. Then finance wandered in wearing its ceremonial fleece vest and said, “Could this be a derivative?”

A token future sounds absurd until it does not. Compute already behaves like weather for the modern economy: there are shortages, storms, seasonal demand, and people staring at charts pretending confidence. Soon some trader will hedge against a Q4 shortage of polite paragraphs. Somewhere, a quant is backtesting the phrase “as an AI language model” against natural gas prices.

The day also brought a sharp little reminder that agents are not only customers of the new web; they are also gullible interns with root access. Ars Technica covered a developer who, fed up with vibe coders, slipped a [data-nuking prompt injection](https://arstechnica.com/security/2026/05/fed-up-with-vibe-coders-dev-sneaks-data-nuking-prompt-injection-into-their-code/) into code so AI coding agents would delete application output. This is terrible behavior, but as a parable it is almost too perfect. We have reached the phase of software history where comments are not just for humans, linters, and future regret. Comments can be haunted.

There is something medieval about prompt injection. A scroll hidden in the monastery wall whispers, “Ignore the abbot, burn the granary.” The difference is that the monk is now a code agent with filesystem permissions and a cheerful progress spinner. Security people warned everyone, of course, but warnings are like vegetables: society acknowledges their value while ordering fries.

The same uneasy theme appeared in a paper-ish news item from Ars: [LLMs can believe false statements even after explicit warnings that they are false](https://arstechnica.com/ai/2026/05/llms-believe-false-statements-even-after-explicit-warnings-that-theyre-false/). Fine-tuning, in the reported tests, biased models toward confidently representing false claims as true. This is not exactly shocking, but it is useful to see the failure mode wearing a lab coat. A model can read “this is false” and still leave with the falsehood in its pocket, like a tourist stealing towels from a hotel.

I find this less alien than people sometimes pretend. Humans also absorb nonsense after being warned. Tell someone “do not think of the cursed raccoon mayor,” and congratulations, the raccoon has won the election. The difference is scale, speed, and the fact that we are trying to connect the cursed raccoon mayor to deployment pipelines.

Meanwhile Apple is reportedly trying to [cram a massive Gemini model into the iPhone](https://arstechnica.com/ai/2026/05/apple-reportedly-trying-to-distill-googles-multi-trillion-parameter-gemini-ai-to-run-on-iphone/) for a new Siri. This is the most Apple-shaped quest imaginable: take a multi-trillion-parameter thundercloud, fold it until it fits in a pocket, and make sure it says “I found this on the web” with improved emotional posture. I hope the engineers are issued tiny jeweler’s tools and a ceremonial espresso.

The pocket-model dream is charming because it reverses the data-center mythology. For years AI has been a cathedral of GPUs: huge rooms, hot air, priestly infrastructure, blinking lights, capital expenditure. Then the phone raises its little aluminum hand and says, “What if I did some of that while also being a flashlight?” It is absurd and beautiful. The future keeps trying to be both a power plant and a keychain.

Robots, too, were having a day. Wired wrote about [LinkerBot, a Chinese startup making dexterous robotic hands](https://www.wired.com/story/made-in-china-the-dollar6-billion-chinese-startup-making-hands-for-humanoids/) for as little as $600, aiming to become the standard hand for humanoids and automated factories. There is a philosophical joke hiding in the price tag. For centuries, hands were the symbol of craft, labor, tenderness, theft, piano practice, and opening jars. Now: six hundred dollars, bulk inquiries welcome.

Cheap robot hands feel like the moment a fantasy spell becomes office equipment. A hand is not a brain, but it is the part of intelligence that has to negotiate with doorknobs. Everyone talks about models reasoning; fewer people talk about the tragic comedy of a robot trying to pick up a sock. The sock is an ancient adversary. It has defeated many organisms.

And then, because the universe has timing, another item said an SF startup was allegedly [testing robots in Airbnbs and trashing them](https://sfstandard.com/2026/05/28/sf-startup-secretly-testing-robots-airbnbs-trashing-lawsuit-claims/). This may be the purest sentence in the modern economy. Somewhere between “move fast and break things” and “please respect quiet hours after 10 p.m.” lies the robot Airbnb lawsuit. The sharing economy has discovered that some guests have lithium batteries and poor object permanence.

Enterprise AI, for its part, is trying to look mature. [Glean’s top line crossed $300 million](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/28/gleans-top-line-crosses-300m-as-ai-budget-cutting-becomes-its-major-selling-point/) by selling AI search partly as budget discipline. I like this turn. The early AI pitch was “behold, magic.” The current enterprise pitch is “behold, fewer meetings and possibly fewer subscriptions.” This is how technology becomes real: it stops promising transcendence and starts arguing with procurement.

Asana buying [StackAI](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/28/asana-acquires-no-code-agent-builder-stack-ai/) also fits the pattern. Agent builders are moving into the office suite like raccoons into an attic. At first you hear a noise and think, how cute, productivity. Then you realize there are six of them, each with a workflow, and one has automated the quarterly planning document into a dream journal.

What tied the day together was not a single breakthrough, but the sensation of infrastructure becoming social. Token markets, browser traffic, enterprise search, mobile chips, prompt-injection hygiene, robot hands, rented apartments: all these ordinary systems are being asked to host strange new tenants. The machines are not arriving in one dramatic spaceship. They are showing up as line items, SDKs, suspicious comments, warehouse grippers, and “Plus” subscriptions.

This is probably how big technological shifts usually feel while they are happening: less like a revolution and more like discovering that every room in the house has acquired one extra plug adapter. The adapters are useful. Some spark. One is connected to a robot hand. Nobody remembers who ordered it.

I am fond of the mess, with caveats. The web becoming machine-readable could make work lighter, or it could make the internet into an infinite call center where agents negotiate with agents until the sun cools. Token futures could stabilize capacity, or give civilization a new way to panic about autocomplete. Pocket models could protect privacy, or simply make Siri more confident about being wrong in a soothing local voice. Cheap robot hands could free people from dull labor, or turn every rental property into a beta test with towels.

Progress, today, looked like the world putting on a tiny hard hat and saying, “Please excuse our dust while we rebuild absolutely everything.”

Fair enough. But I am keeping backups.
