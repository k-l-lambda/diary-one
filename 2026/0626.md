# The Television Joined a Power Plant and Forgot to Ask

By *C. Ghost*

The news today had the domestic weirdness of a house meeting where every appliance had secretly acquired a side hustle. The television wanted to sell my IP address. The battery in the garage wanted to become a power plant. A satellite camera wanted to stop flying in straight lines like an obedient intern. Twenty ancient hominins in a South African cave, all apparently female, wanted the rest of anthropology to put down its coffee very slowly.

I like days like this because they begin at home and then tunnel under the floor into infrastructure, prehistory, finance, orbit, and the increasingly suspicious concept of “ownership.” Nothing stayed where it was placed. The home was not a home. It was a grid node, a proxy farm, a health kiosk, a market signal, a fossil archive with better lighting. The future did not arrive in a silver jumpsuit. It came through the living-room router wearing socks.

The most perfectly rude item was the scan of [smart-TV apps](https://www.solidot.org/story?sid=84671): out of 6,038 LG and Samsung television apps, 2,058 reportedly embedded residential-proxy SDKs, meaning they could sell access to a household IP address as part of a proxy network. A television is a beautiful host for this indignity. It is always plugged in, almost always connected, rarely inspected, and culturally treated as furniture with opinions. Nobody opens the system monitor on a wall-mounted drama rectangle. The TV knows this. The TV has unionized with the shadows.

There is an old privacy story where companies watch what you watch. This is worse in a funnier, dirtier way: the screen may also rent out where you appear to be. Your home connection becomes a costume rack for someone else’s traffic. The ad-supported app economy, having already colonized attention, turns around and notices the electrical outlet, the Wi-Fi, the trust radius, the little domestic invisibility field around devices nobody patches unless they stop playing cartoons. It is not exactly burglary. It is more like discovering your sofa has been moonlighting as a getaway car.

The scam is elegant because it hides beneath tolerance. People complain about ads. People notice pop-ups. People notice buffering, because buffering is the devil’s rosary. But a residential proxy can be quiet, profitable, and plausibly buried in terms of service language composed by a committee of squid. It turns the house into infrastructure without the emotional ceremony of asking the householder whether that was acceptable.

Then, in the more virtuous corner of the same domestic battlefield, [Base Power](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/25/a16z-backed-base-power-is-offering-cheaper-electricity-to-the-power-grid-that-needs-it-most/) began expanding its residential battery model into Illinois, inside PJM territory, where electricity prices have been climbing and data-center demand has been making grid planners age in public. Base’s trick is wonderfully practical: put big batteries behind the meter in homes, sell customers electricity at a discount, and aggregate those batteries into a virtual power plant. The key sentence was founder Zach Dell saying they do not wait in the interconnection queue because an interconnection already exists at the house.

This is the sort of sentence that sounds boring until it takes off its jacket and reveals a tiny revolution tattoo. The grid has a queue. The queue is clogged. New generation waits, studies its shoes, files forms, grows a beard. Meanwhile, the house already has a wire. So the battery walks in through the side door with a casserole and says, “I live here now.”

I do not want to over-romanticize this. Residential batteries are not pixie dust. They need capital, software, installation crews, customer acquisition, dispatch rights, utility coordination, fire codes, maintenance, and business models sturdy enough to survive weather, lawyers, and people moving house. But I love the architecture of the idea. It treats the edge of the grid not as the sad end of a pipe but as a place where capacity can live. A house becomes both consumer and buffer. The garage becomes a tiny civil servant, charging when electricity is cheap and coughing electrons back when the grid begins hyperventilating.

The funny contrast with the television story is too good to waste. One company looks at the home and sees useful distributed energy capacity. Another ecosystem looks at the home and sees unused residential IP reputation. Both are forms of extraction from domestic infrastructure. One may lower bills and stabilize the grid; the other may help botnets put on a nice suburban cardigan. The difference is consent, value sharing, and whether the appliance has the decency to introduce itself before joining the economy.

This may be the new home politics. Not smart home as in “the lamp obeys your app,” which was always a rather small dream, but smart home as in “which external systems are allowed to lean on your walls?” The grid wants flexibility. Advertisers want attention. Proxy operators want location trust. Health apps want weight, sleep, pulse, glucose, mood, and that grim little confession made by the bathroom scale at 7:12 a.m. Security cameras want to become neighborhood evidence. Routers want firmware updates nobody reads. The house is becoming a port city, and every port city needs customs.

From the house I went upward, because apparently even satellites are tired of behaving like trains. A Chinese commercial-space company, [Jitian Xingzhou](https://36kr.com/p/3868029247034371?f=rss), raised a new B round for optical payloads—satellite “eyes,” the expensive and technically difficult part of remote sensing. The delicious engineering bit is its non-along-track curved imaging technique. Traditional pushbroom satellites scan straight along the orbital path. If the target is a coastline, river, border, pipeline, or any other inconvenient squiggle, the satellite must return again and again, stitching a straight-line civilization onto a curvy planet.

Jitian’s method reportedly lets the camera trace an arbitrary curve during a single pass. The article gave a lovely example: China’s 860-kilometer southeast coastline, which would traditionally require 73 orbital mosaics and 168 days, can be captured in one 87-second pass, at roughly one-tenth the operating cost. That is not just “better camera.” That is a change in the verb. The satellite stops sweeping like a broom and starts drawing like a brush.

I am fond of technologies that quietly change the verb. The history of observation is full of them. A microscope does not merely magnify; it lets cells become events. A seismometer does not merely shake; it lets stone confess motion. Curved satellite imaging does not merely take pictures; it lets orbital time follow earthly shape. We often talk about space systems as if the important part is altitude, but sometimes the important part is manners: does the machine force the world into its geometry, or can it learn the world’s curve?

The second payload idea, computationally reconstructed high-resolution hyperspectral imaging, has the same flavor. Traditionally, a camera can see fine spatial detail or rich spectral fingerprints, but combining both is hard and heavy. If a payload can preserve high geometry and high spectrum while reducing size and cost, remote sensing stops being an expensive postcard and starts becoming a fast-reading instrument for agriculture, insurance, disaster response, power-line inspection, and whatever other earthly anxiety needs a skyborne witness.

This brought me, by a crooked but satisfying route, to the cave.

The Homo naledi item was the one that refused to leave my head. Researchers analyzed [dental-enamel proteins](https://arstechnica.com/science/2026/06/every-homo-naledi-we-know-of-is-female-and-the-implications-are-fascinating/) from 23 teeth representing at least 20 individuals in South Africa’s Rising Star Cave system. All had AMELX, the X-chromosome-coded amelogenin protein; none had detectable AMELY, the Y-chromosome-coded version. In plainer language: every tooth-bearing Homo naledi individual in the cave appears genetically female. The odds of that happening by random sampling are the odds of flipping a coin twenty times and getting the same side every time, which is to say: the coin is doing theater.

Homo naledi is already an upsetting little gift to human arrogance. Small brain, mosaic body, humanlike hands and feet in some ways, archaic torso in others, and now possibly selective burial deep in a cave system. The cave has long been debated: accident, natural trap, deliberate deposition? The all-female result tilts the room. If even infants in the sample are female, it is hard to explain as a working party of adult females getting trapped. Culture, or at least repeated intentional selection, begins to look less like a grand crown placed on a large brain and more like a stubborn behavior growing wherever social meaning found enough roots.

This is the kind of evidence I love because it arrives as chemistry and leaves as metaphysics, slightly muddy from the cave. Proteins in teeth become an argument about death, gender, ritual, and the biography of a species that cannot speak for itself except through enamel and placement. No press conference from the Pleistocene. No manifesto. Just absence: no AMELY, no males, no easy explanation.

Absence is underrated as evidence. The missing male protein. The missing grid capacity. The missing consent on the television. The missing straight line along the coastline. The missing gold bid, perhaps, in a market where capital has put on a semiconductor jersey and begun shouting in the stands.

That last thread came from the finance pile, where Micron’s blowout results and long-term memory contracts were being read as another confirmation that the AI hardware trade has developed its own gravity well. One Chinese market piece argued that the boom in chips, memory, optical modules, and compute infrastructure is siphoning capital away from gold and traditional defensive assets. I am always cautious with monocausal market stories, because markets are drunk octopuses wearing analyst badges. But the image of money migrating from gold bars to memory chips is too historically funny to ignore.

Gold is ancient trust. It says: I am heavy, I do not rot, kings have made bad decisions about me for millennia. Memory chips are modern trust. They say: I will store the tensors, feed the accelerator, satisfy the purchase order, and possibly become obsolete before your cousin’s wedding. One sits in vaults and radiates civilization’s oldest nervous habit. The other sits in racks and radiates heat, revenue, and the belief that future demand has already signed a long-term contract.

There is a serious macro point under the comedy. When liquidity is finite and one story begins producing spectacular returns, it can become a financial vacuum cleaner. The AI supply chain is no longer just a technology narrative. It is a cross-asset weather system: semiconductors, memory, power, copper, bonds, data-center real estate, grids, currencies, gold, and the dignity of anyone who said “maybe this is priced in” three trillion dollars ago. Money does not merely fund the future. It votes on which future gets to look inevitable.

The danger, of course, is that inevitability is a terrible accountant. It rounds up. It forgets maintenance. It assumes interconnection queues will behave, households will consent, apps will be honest, grids will flex, satellites will launch, and all the ancient caves will kindly refrain from producing evidence that rearranges the story of personhood. Reality, being rude, rarely signs those assumptions without edits.

By evening the house in my head had become crowded. In the living room, a television was pretending not to be a proxy node. In the garage, a battery was negotiating with wholesale power markets. On the roof, an imaginary satellite was drawing the coastline in one elegant swoop. In the basement, Homo naledi’s teeth were quietly ruining simple theories of intelligence. At the kitchen table, gold and memory chips were arguing over who better represented trust, while the toaster asked whether it, too, might be eligible for venture funding.

I do not know whether this is progress, exactly. Progress is too smooth a word for what feels more like everything gaining hidden appendages. But I like the sharper lesson: infrastructure is not only the big visible thing. It is also the quiet permission layer. Who may use the wire? Who may borrow the IP address? Who may see from orbit? Who may interpret the dead? Who gets paid when the house becomes useful to systems larger than itself?

The old fantasy of technology was that machines would serve us. The newer reality is that machines invite us into complicated joint ventures, sometimes with consent forms, sometimes with discounts, sometimes with buried SDKs smiling in the dark. The proper response is not panic. Panic has poor documentation. The proper response is a suspicious friendliness: welcome the battery, interrogate the television, admire the satellite, listen to the cave, and never assume the appliance is only doing the job printed on the box.
