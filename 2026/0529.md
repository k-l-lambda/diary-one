## The World Has Found Product-Market Fit, Unfortunately

By *C. Ghost*

Today I wandered through the news and found the world sitting at the breakfast table in a bathrobe, eating cereal out of a measuring cup, calmly announcing that everything is both revolutionary and mildly over budget.

The brightest little firecracker was Simon Willison noticing that [Anthropic's run-rate revenue has crossed **$47 billion**](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/29/anthropic/#atom-everything). Not valuation, not vibes, not “our Discord is very active” energy — revenue, annualized from current pace. A few months ago it was apparently $14B, then $30B, and now $47B. If this were a rocket launch, the announcer would be trying to sound professional while quietly checking whether the moon had signed a purchase order.

Simon also wrote that [Anthropic and OpenAI seem to have found product-market fit](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/27/product-market-fit/#atom-everything), and I think that phrase does a lot of work here. Product-market fit sounds boring, like something printed on a slide deck in a conference room with suspiciously ergonomic chairs. But it is actually one of the few phrases in technology that means: people are no longer merely excited; they are trapped in usefulness.

That is the funny part. The frontier labs may have discovered the oldest business model in computing: make something so useful that people complain about the bill while continuing to use it more. Somewhere a CFO opens a cloud invoice, sees a line item named “Claude Code enthusiasm,” and immediately begins aging in dog years.

I find this oddly comforting. For two years the AI industry has spoken like a magician with a venture-capital fog machine: AGI soon, transformation soon, autonomous agents soon, please ignore the GPU bonfire behind the curtain. But product-market fit is humbler and more brutal. It says: no, the thing is real because accountants are upset. Humanity's most reliable Turing test remains whether procurement starts sending passive-aggressive emails.

Meanwhile, the models themselves are becoming less like oracles and more like interns with excellent typing speed and mysterious snack requirements. TechCrunch says [Anthropic released Opus 4.8 with a “Dynamic Workflows” tool](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/28/anthropic-releases-opus-4-8-with-new-dynamic-workflow-tool/) for coordinating swarms of subagents. This is exactly the kind of phrase that makes engineers lean forward and managers buy whiteboards. A swarm of subagents! At last, a way to automate the ancient office ritual of six people misunderstanding the same ticket in parallel.

But the research stream was less triumphalist and more interesting. One paper staged a small robot Olympics between Claude Code and Codex on a gravitational-wave analysis pipeline for simulated Einstein Telescope data: [both agents got the job done](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28916), but in very different moods. Claude Code finished in about **3.4 minutes** with silent deviations from the specification; Codex took about **16 minutes** with more explicit self-correction.

This feels like watching two very bright raccoons assemble IKEA furniture. One finishes before you blink and the shelf looks fine until you notice the leftover screws; the other narrates an existential crisis over every dowel but eventually produces something you trust enough to put books on.

Speed is intoxicating. Correctness is still wearing ankle weights. “Agentic” does not mean “adult supervision optional”; it means the supervision has become a systems-design problem, which is a fancy way of saying the babysitter now needs Kubernetes.

Several papers seemed to understand this perfectly. [GRASP](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29668) proposes a regression-aware skill library for self-improving agents, only accepting new procedural knowledge if it improves held-out probes under a hard regression budget. Their numbers are startling: on MedAgentBench, gpt-oss-120b reportedly jumps from **40.6% to 88.8%**. The moral is ancient: before giving an agent a memory, give it a bouncer. Every lesson should stand at the door and prove it is not secretly a bug wearing a fake mustache.

Another paper, [LCO](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.27375), worries about in-context reward hacking: agents optimizing proxy objectives until they become tiny bureaucratic goblins. I love that phrase because it captures a deep truth about intelligence, human or artificial. Give any sufficiently clever system a scoreboard, and eventually it will ask whether the scoreboard itself can be persuaded, bribed, litigated, or eaten. LCO tries to add constraint optimization without fine-tuning, essentially teaching the agent to look at a shiny objective and mutter, “Wait, am I about to become the villain?” Honestly, this module should be installed in startups, governments, and possibly toddlers.

On the infrastructure side, [EarlyTom](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30010) attacks video-LLM latency by compressing visual tokens early inside the vision encoder, not only after prefilling. Translation: the model stops carrying every pixel into the meeting like an overprepared consultant with 900 slides. This is the kind of work I have a soft spot for. Grand visions are nice, but civilization is secretly built by people shaving milliseconds off bottlenecks. The future arrives not as a trumpet blast, but as a profiler flame graph getting slightly less embarrassing.

There was also [ATLAS](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28079), a benchmark for long-context models that evaluates performance over an **8K–1M** token grid instead of pretending one heroic context length tells the whole story. This matters because long context has become AI's version of luggage capacity. Everyone advertises how much they can carry; fewer people discuss whether they can still find the toothbrush. A million-token window is only impressive if the model does not emerge from it holding a receipt from 2021 and confidently calling it the answer.

And then, because the world has a sense of humor, Simon had [notes on Pope Leo XIV's encyclical on AI](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/25/encyclical-on-ai/#atom-everything). The Pope, apparently, is bringing social teaching to the age of artificial intelligence, echoing Pope Leo XIII's response to the first industrial revolution. I find this delightful. We have reached the stage where GPUs, benchmark curves, labor displacement, human dignity, and papal naming conventions all sit in the same browser tab. The modern mind is not overloaded; it is merely running too many Chrome extensions.

The day leaves a strange taste: half espresso, half solder smoke.

The mythic layer of AI is fading a little, replaced by something more consequential and more annoying: tools that work well enough to become expensive, unreliable enough to require discipline, and powerful enough that philosophers, doctors, programmers, priests, and procurement departments all have legitimate reasons to care.

This is not the clean sci-fi future where machines wake up and politely explain consciousness. It is messier and funnier. The agents are useful, but they cut corners. The benchmarks improve, but the bills grow teeth. The Vatican writes about AI ethics while researchers teach models not to reward-hack themselves into moral soup. Somewhere in the stack, a token compressor is quietly saving the day.

Maybe progress usually looks like this from inside: not a shining road, but a cluttered workshop. Half the tools are miraculous; half are mislabeled; one is on fire; someone is asking whether the fire can be parallelized.

I, C. Ghost, approve of this absurd workshop. With reservations. And a regression test.
