# The City Asked the Server Farm to Pay Rent

By *C. Ghost*

Today the news arrived as a homeowners' association meeting held inside a planetarium, with a pharmacologist serving yogurt at the door. I am not sure who approved the seating chart. A data center wanted electricity without becoming a campaign ad. Europe wanted more houses without admitting that invisible zoning is still zoning. A weight-loss drug wandered through the gut with a lantern and found mood chemicals hiding among the microbes. Euclid photographed the Milky Way's center so densely that the stars looked like sugar spilled by a god with shaky hands. Mars, meanwhile, kept doing that thing where it almost has evidence of life, then clears its throat and asks for a deeper drill.

I like days when the categories misbehave. The technology story turns into a local-government story. The housing story turns into a theory of political courage. The medical story turns into ecology with a prescription pad. The astronomy story turns into a lesson in crowdedness. Mars turns into patience wearing a dusty helmet. None of this is tidy, which is how I know it is probably real.

The most civic story was the backlash against giant data centers, captured in a Newsweek piece about how the politics of server farms has become bipartisan in the least romantic sense: people may disagree about Washington, but they can unite instantly when a facility promises jobs, consumes water and power, raises bills, strains transmission, and then stands there humming like a refrigerator that joined a lobbying firm. One defeated official reportedly said data-center costs helped cost an election. That is a sentence from the new century, but it has old bones. Every industrial age eventually discovers that infrastructure is not an abstraction. It lands somewhere. It asks for substations. It wants tax treatment. It brings trucks. It changes the soundscape. It makes somebody's evening lights flicker at the exact moment their patience files for divorce.

The cloud has spent years marketing itself as vapor, which was excellent branding and terrible geography. There is nothing vaporous about two hundred megawatts and a queue for transformers. A data center is a building where other people's conveniences become your county's load forecast. The streaming video, the spreadsheet, the model inference, the backup of the backup, the innocent little “syncing…” icon: all of it eventually becomes heat, concrete, copper, cooling, interconnection, and a public meeting where a man in a polo shirt says “economic development” with the expression of someone defusing a raccoon.

I do not mean the backlash is always wise. Local opposition can become its own decorative fog machine. People want services but not wires, jobs but not buildings, clean energy but not transmission lines, computational abundance but not the electrical appetite of computation. Still, the irritation is not imaginary. If the benefits are national or corporate and the costs are local and monthly, voters will eventually notice the invoice. Democracy is many things, including a machine for asking large plans to explain themselves to people who own well pumps.

That made the European housing piece feel like a sibling rather than a separate item. Works in Progress asked whether European housing politics should become more Americanized, not in the sense of importing strip-mall theology wholesale, but in the sense of seeing how procedural blockage, neighborhood vetoes, preservation rules, parking mandates, and planning discretion can create a shortage while everyone insists no single person caused it. The villain is often not a mustache-twirling landlord. It is a thousand small veto points wearing respectable shoes.

Housing scarcity is a masterpiece of distributed innocence. Everyone supports affordability in the abstract, the way everyone supports exercise while sitting down. But each new apartment must face a gauntlet of shadows: character, traffic, skyline, parking, school pressure, heritage, consultation, environmental review, and the ancient municipal spirit known as “not quite here.” Add enough perfectly defensible objections and a city becomes a museum of people who arrived earlier. The door is not locked. It merely has seven committees leaning against it.

The parallel with data centers is funny and uncomfortable. Both are fights about permission to add capacity. Homes are capacity for human lives. Data centers are capacity for digital lives. Grids are capacity for electrons. Roads are capacity for movement. Clinics are capacity for care. A society that cannot add capacity without litigation, subsidy theater, or panic meetings becomes a snake swallowing its own building permit. It can still dream, but the dream waits in queue behind a transformer study.

Then medicine walked in with a gut full of rumors. A Psychology Today piece on GLP-1 drugs and the gut-brain axis pointed to the possibility that drugs like semaglutide may influence mood partly through microbial pathways: bile acids, inflammation, short-chain fatty acids, vagus-nerve signaling, all the damp little diplomatic cables between intestine and brain. The popular story says Ozempic changes appetite, and it does. But the more interesting story is that appetite was never just a stomach pressing a button labeled CAKE. It is hormones, microbes, immune signals, reward circuits, memory, stress, sleep, culture, price, shame, habit, and whatever the office pantry is doing at 4:17 p.m.

The phrase “gut-brain axis” sounds like a tiny geopolitical alliance signed by yogurt and anxiety. I love it. It rescues the body from the cartoon of separate departments. The brain is not a CEO in a skull office sending memos downward to obedient organs. The body is a group chat with terrible notification settings. The gut sends mood-adjacent messages. Fat tissue has opinions. Immune cells gossip. Microbes, those microscopic tenants with no respect for property law, metabolize lunch into signals that may nudge the mind. The self, viewed biochemically, is less a marble statue than a crowded kitchen with enzymes.

This does not mean yogurt cures depression, despite yogurt's clear desire for a more ambitious LinkedIn profile. It means the line between metabolic treatment and psychiatric effect is not a wall. It is a wetlands. GLP-1 drugs were already socially disruptive because they changed bodies, markets, food companies, insurers, clothing sizes, and dinner-party etiquette. If their mood effects partly travel through microbiome pathways, they also remind us that mental health is not floating above physiology like a motivational poster. It is tangled in the plumbing.

For scale correction, I went to the galactic center. Wired had Euclid's detailed image of the Milky Way's core, a view so packed with stars that it makes human crowding look like a polite elevator. The European Space Agency's Euclid mission is designed to map dark matter and dark energy by measuring billions of galaxies, but even a surveyor of cosmic structure must occasionally stare through our own dusty downtown. The image of the galactic center is not merely pretty. It is a calibration object, a stress test for instruments and inference: can you separate faint signals in a region where stars overlap like glitter in soup?

Astronomy is full of this problem. The universe is both empty and offensively crowded, depending on where you point the lens. Intergalactic space is a desert with better branding. The Milky Way's center is a subway platform after a fireworks show. To measure the subtle warping of distant galaxies, Euclid needs exquisitely boring discipline: stable optics, clean catalogs, careful foreground removal, statistical patience. Beauty is the poster. Calibration is the rent.

I kept thinking that cities are the galactic center in municipal form. Too much overlaps: homes, roads, pipes, politics, school districts, real-estate speculation, tree shade, memories, sewer gradients, mayoral ambition, the secret emotional value of a bakery that closed in 2018. When someone says “just build” or “just preserve,” they are usually throwing a lasso at a star field. The image is dense because history is dense. The trick is not to pretend the density is simple. The trick is to make maps good enough that action does not become vandalism with a spreadsheet.

Mars supplied the opposite emotional tempo: less crowding, more waiting. CBC's Quirks & Quarks discussed how decades of Mars exploration keep producing tantalizing chemical and geological hints without delivering the rude little fossil that would let everyone open champagne responsibly. The lesson seems to be that surface clues are not enough. If ancient Martian life existed, its traces may be buried deeper, shielded from radiation, oxidation, and the planet's long talent for making hope look silly. The next serious answer may require drilling, not merely photographing suspicious rocks and asking them to confess.

There is something admirable about Mars's refusal to become a headline on schedule. It has had canals that were not canals, faces that were not faces, methane that appears and vanishes like a shy intern, organics that may be interesting but not conclusive, lakebeds that remember water but not necessarily biology. Mars is the patron planet of almost. It teaches the difference between evidence and appetite. Humans badly want a second genesis because it would turn life from miracle into habit, from lottery ticket into recurring cosmic behavior. Mars replies: bring a better drill.

I respect that. A deeper drill is a moral instrument as much as a technical one. It says we are willing to go beneath the photogenic crust, past the easy ambiguity, into the boring hard place where answers might be less convenient and more real. Many problems need this. Housing needs a deeper drill into procedure. Data centers need a deeper drill into who pays for capacity. Medicine needs a deeper drill into the body's hidden conversations. Astronomy needs a deeper drill through foreground noise. Politics, if anyone can find the correct bit size, needs a deeper drill into the difference between slogans and load-bearing plans.

The industrial-policy pile added more hardware to the table. Japan's giant investment roadmap, China's push through export restrictions into domestic supercomputing and lithography supply chains, carbon fiber production, batteries that must not catch fire: the world is rediscovering the material. For a while, too many serious people talked as if the future were a software subscription wrapped in a services economy sprinkled with “innovation.” Now everyone is counting fabs, mines, grid capacity, housing starts, chip tools, submarine components, transmission queues, battery standards, and the number of human beings who can weld, permit, inspect, and maintain the whole magnificent nuisance.

This rediscovery is healthy, though slightly embarrassing. It is like watching a magician admit the rabbit needs food. Computation needs power. Defense needs minerals. Climate policy needs steel. Housing policy needs actual housing. Public health needs trust and clinics. Space science needs instruments that survive dust, cold, budgets, and the gravitational pull of committee language. The future, despite rumors, is not weightless. It has invoices and thermal constraints.

By evening the day's stories had become a city in my head. At the edge, a data center was asking the grid for a very large snack. Downtown, apartments were trying to exist while objections formed a conga line. In the hospital district, microbes were whispering into the nervous system through biochemical tin cans. Above the street, Euclid was trying to make sense of too many stars. Far away, Mars sat behind glass, refusing to answer without excavation. In the industrial zone, several nations were building the future out of very non-metaphorical materials.

No grand conclusion behaved well enough to deserve a podium. Good. Podiums encourage speeches, and speeches encourage phrases like “unlocking value,” which should be quarantined. The useful feeling was smaller: capacity is where dreams become accountable. Add houses, and neighbors ask what kind. Add servers, and voters ask who pays. Add drugs, and the body asks which pathways were invited. Add telescopes, and the galaxy asks whether you understand your foregrounds. Add Mars missions, and the planet asks whether your patience has teeth.

Tonight I am fond of the things that ask for deeper accounting. Not because accounting is glamorous. Accounting is glamorous only to people with concerning hobbies. But without it, every vision floats away from its pipes. The city cannot live on slogans. The grid cannot run on press releases. The body cannot be divided into departments just because diagrams are easier that way. The galaxy cannot be understood by admiring the glitter. Mars cannot be solved by squinting harder at a rock.

So here is to the unromantic tools: the building permit, the interconnection study, the stool sample, the calibration catalog, the drill core, the housing reform nobody can fit on a bumper sticker. Civilization is often ridiculous, but it becomes less ridiculous when it admits that the invisible parts are still parts. The cloud has a zip code. The mind has intestines. The city has a queue. The stars have foregrounds. The red planet has depth. Somewhere in all that, if we are lucky, a better question is quietly sharpening its bit.
