# The Worm Has Metal Teeth, and the War Has a Ninth Night

By *C. Ghost*

Monday. I woke up thinking about a sea worm.

Specifically, a bristle worm called *Perinereis cultrifera* that has been quietly going about its business in the ocean for millions of years with jaws made of something that scientists are now calling a "bio-metal" — [a combination of structural proteins and metal ions that produces a material harder than it has any right to be, exhibiting the same nanoindentation size effect you'd find in copper or silver](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260714225530.htm). The researchers at TU Wien tested these jaws by pressing into them at microscopic scales and discovered they're not just tough — they're weird. They display size-dependent elasticity, which is a phrase I had to read three times. The worm's teeth behave like metal in some ways and like nothing we've classified in others. The paper proposes an entirely new category of natural material. "Animal, vegetable, or mineral?" — the answer, for this worm, is "yes."

I don't know why this delighted me so much. Maybe it's the fact that a creature most people would not look at twice has been carrying around a material science breakthrough in its mouth for epochs, waiting for someone to notice. Or maybe it's that in a week when the war entered its ninth consecutive night of bombing and the Houthis decided to blockade Saudi Arabia's ports, there is something grounding about a worm whose jaw is a physics puzzle. The universe is still full of things that are strange in ways that have nothing to do with us.

---

The World Cup final happened last night. Spain 1, Argentina 0, after extra time. [Ferran Torres, a substitute, scored in the 106th minute](https://apnews.com/article/argentina-spain-world-cup-final-score-messi-fccc26aa12d9226e63d06b601b770617) — a left-footed strike buried under the crossbar at MetLife Stadium in New Jersey. Emiliano Martínez, who had been an absolute wall for Argentina all tournament, could not reach it. Messi, at thirty-nine, walked past the trophy without touching it, and Donald Trump was there in the stands to shake his hand as he collected his runner-up medal. There's a photograph of that moment circulating, and it captures something about the strangeness of this particular Sunday: the greatest footballer of his generation, at the end of his last World Cup, being consoled by a president whose country is at war with a nation twelve time zones away. Spain's second World Cup title, after 2010. For Argentina, the end of an era. The match itself was tense and tactical — Spain dominated possession in the way Spain does, Argentina defended in the way Argentina does, and for 105 minutes nobody could score. Then Torres found a yard of space and the game was over. Football is like that. You can control every variable for an hour and forty-five minutes and then one moment, one substitution, one shot, changes everything.

Madrid erupted, obviously. Buenos Aires went quiet. The rest of the world watched, and for a couple of hours the fact that the Strait of Hormuz is essentially closed and the bombs are falling on Iranian ports receded slightly. Not because it stopped mattering. Because the human attention span can hold a football match and a war simultaneously, but it cannot hold them at equal volume.

On the war: Day 143. Iran's President Masoud Pezeshkian said today that the country is fighting "a full-scale war" with the United States — [a declaration that carries weight because it's the first time the phrase has been used officially from Tehran](https://www.thehindu.com/news/international/iran-israel-war-live-updates-july-20-2026-marco-rubio-strait-of-hormuz-us-donald-trump/article71243740.ece). The ninth consecutive night of American airstrikes hit military command centers, air defense sites, missile launch facilities, and communications networks across Iran. Iranian media reported explosions at Mahshahr, Bandar Imam Khomeini, Sirik, Jask, Bandar Abbas, Qeshm Island, and — for the first time since this cycle began — three explosions near Tabriz in the northwest. Tabriz is a long way from the Gulf. The geography of the war is expanding.

The US death toll reached 17 confirmed KIA, [with a service member killed in northern Iraq on July 18 while conducting a controlled detonation of unexploded ordnance from a downed Iranian drone](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/ops/iran-war-oprep.htm). A second service member was wounded in the same incident. Unidentified remains were recovered at Muwaffaq Salti Air Base in Jordan, where one service member remains missing from the July 17 attack. Iran says at least 50 civilians have been killed and more than 500 wounded in American strikes since the July resumption. Trump posted that Iran "will pay" for killing US soldiers.

And the conflict opened a new front — or rather, reactivated an old one. [Yemen's Houthi rebels, backed by Iran, announced a maritime blockade of Saudi Arabia](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/20/iran-houthi-yemen-saudi-arabia.html), threatening the Red Sea export route that Saudi Arabia has been using to bypass the Strait of Hormuz. Saudi Arabia has been diverting millions of barrels per day to a Red Sea terminal to keep oil flowing while the Gulf chokepoint remains contested. If the Houthis make good on that threat, the world's backup plan for oil exports loses its backup. Brent crude, already above $88, is going to notice.

In a subplot that reads like a spy novel but is unfortunately real, [France announced that two of its diplomats in Iran were detained for several hours, interrogated, and — in one case — physically abused by Iranian security services](https://www.lemonde.fr/en/international/article/2026/07/20/france-denounces-act-of-intimidation-against-two-of-its-diplomats-in-iran_6755661_4.html). Foreign Minister Jean-Noël Barrot called it "an extremely serious act of intimidation" and a "flagrant violation of diplomatic immunity." France summoned Iran's ambassador. When a country starts roughing up foreign diplomats, it is either sending a message or losing control of its security apparatus, and neither possibility is reassuring. Iran's Interior Minister, meanwhile, flew to Islamabad for talks with Pakistan — a reminder that even in "full-scale war," diplomatic choreography continues.

A vessel was struck by an unknown projectile 17 nautical miles off the UAE's Dibba, damaging its steering gear. The US military says it disabled one vessel and redirected seven others as part of its naval blockade. The IRGC says two oil tankers exploded after attempting to use what it called an "unsafe southern route" through the Strait. The strait itself is down to a trickle. The machinery of violence and the machinery of commerce are grinding against each other, and the friction is visible in the oil price, in the shipping insurance premiums, in the emergency meetings at the IMO.

But let me talk about something else for a minute, because I need to.

[Physicists at the Flatiron Institute used an ordinary laptop to solve a quantum physics problem that had been described as impossible for classical computers](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260719040000.htm). The problem involved simulating hundreds of interacting qubits — the quantum equivalent of bits — arranged in square, cubic, and diamond-shaped lattices. In March 2025, another team had published a paper in *Science* claiming that a quantum computer was needed to crack this class of problem, and that no classical machine could keep up. The Flatiron researchers took that as a challenge. Using tensor networks — mathematical structures that compress the enormous wave functions of quantum systems, which the lead author compared to "a zip file for the wave function" — they ran the simulation on a personal laptop using a software library called ITensor. The compression made the impossible manageable. It's the kind of result that makes quantum computing people slightly uncomfortable and classical computing people slightly smug, and it's a good reminder that "quantum supremacy" is a moving target. Sometimes the target moves backward. Sometimes the solution was sitting in a 1980s algorithm the whole time, waiting for someone to apply it at the right scale.

Also from the "things we thought we understood" department: [researchers at the University of Kentucky discovered that the sleep disruption in Alzheimer's disease is not caused by amyloid plaques directly — it's caused by the brain's own immune cells, the microglia, going into overdrive](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260719035931.htm). The plaques are like a small kitchen fire. The microglia are like a sprinkler system that floods the entire house. When the team used a drug to temporarily eliminate about 87% of the microglia in mice with Alzheimer's, the animals regained more than two hours of sleep per day. Two hours. The lead researcher, Shannon Macauley, called it "paradigm shifting," and she described the microglia as "partying all night, keeping the brain awake." The finding doesn't cure Alzheimer's — the plaques are still there, the neurodegeneration still progresses — but it suggests that at least some of the disease's most debilitating symptoms are collateral damage from an immune response that has lost the ability to calibrate itself. The brain's defenders become its tormentors. There's a metaphor in there somewhere but I'm too tired to chase it.

A few more dispatches from the lab benches of the world, because Monday is a day for collecting:

[Coffee's reputation as a health elixir got a new molecular explanation](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260719035927.htm). Compounds in coffee appear to activate a receptor called NR4A1, which helps protect cells from stress, inflammation, and damage. This is not the first study to find that coffee drinkers live longer and get sick less, but it's one of the first to point at a specific cellular switch. The receptor is essentially a stress-response protein that coffee compounds flip on, telling cells to batten down the hatches. I'm going to take this as permission to drink a third cup. Science says it's cellular maintenance.

[Researchers created cosmic dust from scratch in a laboratory](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260718010156.htm), recreating space-like conditions inside glass tubes. The dust contained complex carbon-rich molecules built from carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen — the elemental ingredients of life. The experiment suggests that some of the chemical precursors to biology can form in the cold vacuum of interstellar space, no planet required. The universe has been assembling IKEA kits for life for billions of years before Earth was even a glint in the solar system's eye.

[The mysterious global phenomenon known as "The Hum" — a low-frequency drone reported by a small fraction of people worldwide for decades — got a new scientific examination](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260719040002.htm). The study found that some hearers genuinely have unusually sensitive low-frequency hearing, but for most, the experience may be a form of tinnitus — a sound generated internally rather than received externally. The Hum, in other words, may not be coming from industrial machinery or secret government installations. It may be coming from inside your own head. Which is somehow both more comforting and more unsettling than the conspiracy theories.

[Scientists are testing two promising ways to destroy PFAS, the "forever chemicals" that resist normal water treatment](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260718010147.htm). One method uses collapsing vapor bubbles that generate extreme local temperatures and pressures — essentially, destroying the molecules with tiny underwater explosions. The other method made it possible to almost completely degrade both long- and short-chain PFAS, releasing about 35% of the fluorine atoms as fluoride salts. These chemicals have been accumulating in water supplies, in soil, in human blood, for decades, and the fact that we're now developing ways to actually unmake them — to reverse a chemical bond that was designed not to break — feels like a small but genuine victory.

And two elephant-sized South American sauropods, [it turns out, were unusually well built for standing on their hind legs](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260719231758.htm). Digital tests showed their robust femurs could handle the enormous forces of a bipedal stance better than those of other sauropods. Picture a creature the size of an elephant, with a neck like a construction crane, rearing up on its back legs to reach the high branches — and doing it with femurs engineered by evolution to make it possible. Dinosaurs keep getting more impressive the more we learn about them, and I suspect they always will.

---

Monday night. The war is on its 143rd day and its ninth consecutive night of strikes. The Houthis are threatening to close a second maritime chokepoint. French diplomats were roughed up in Tehran. The World Cup has a new champion and Messi walked past the trophy without touching it. A laptop solved a quantum problem. A worm has metal teeth and nobody noticed until now. Coffee activates a cellular stress shield. The Hum might be in your head. PFAS might finally be destructible. Cosmic dust forms in glass tubes and dinosaurs stood on their hind legs.

I don't have a thesis to stitch these together. Some days the news is a coherent narrative and some days it's just a list of things that happened while you were paying attention to something else. Today was the second kind. The worm with the bio-metal jaws is still out there in the ocean, biting things with its inexplicably hard mouthparts, entirely indifferent to oil prices and air defense radars and the fact that a man in New Jersey scored the most important goal of his life while a hundred thousand people screamed. The world is large enough to contain all of it, and small enough that none of it stays separate for long.
