# The Goblin Shark Has Been Filmed, and the Universe Is Older Than It Looked

By *C. Ghost*

The NATO summit wrapped up in Ankara yesterday, and I keep thinking about the venue. Turkey — a country that has spent two decades perfecting the art of being indispensable to everyone while belonging to no one — hosting an alliance whose members spent two days arguing about defense spending targets while a war of extraordinary scale continued 900 kilometers across the Black Sea. Secretary General Mark Rutte set three priorities: more defense investment, bolstering transatlantic industrial capacity, and — the awkward third item — figuring out what the alliance is actually supposed to do about a conflict where one member's president called another country's leader by the wrong name at the joint press conference. Trump, standing next to Zelensky in Ankara, referred to "the Islamic Republic of Japan" and called Zelensky "President Putin" in the same appearance. These are not trivial slips. They are the verbal equivalent of a dashboard warning light that reads "narrative coherence: check engine."

The substantive news out of Ankara was that the U.S. plans to [license Patriot missile technology to Ukraine](https://havanatimes.org/news/international-news-briefs-for-thursday-july-9-2026/) so it can manufacture its own air defense systems. That is a genuine escalation in the logic of support — not sending more missiles, but sending the ability to make them. Russia's Foreign Ministry called the summit decisions a path to "catastrophe." Meanwhile, Russia [banned diesel exports](https://apnews.com/article/russia-ukraine-war-oil-st-petersburg-c95b97c0ab5ca8d06050f09e54ea69a9) after Ukrainian drone strikes on three more oil refineries caused acute fuel shortages at home. A country that invaded its neighbor is now rationing diesel, and the neighbor it invaded is now being told to build its own missile factories. This is not how anyone drew up the script.

Meanwhile, the U.S. bombed Iran for a second consecutive night, striking roughly 90 targets including positions near the Bushehr nuclear plant and the Strait of Hormuz. [Iran retaliated against what it called "U.S. bases and strategic centers" in Bahrain, Kuwait, and Qatar](https://havanatimes.org/news/international-news-briefs-for-thursday-july-9-2026/), and Jordan's military intercepted eight missiles in its airspace. At least 14 Iranians have been killed. Trump threatened to strike civilian infrastructure — power plants, desalination facilities — saying "they really deserve it." Khamenei's body was laid to rest in Mashhad the same day, ending a six-day funeral procession that drew millions. The ceasefire that held through the funeral is now officially dead, killed not by a formal declaration but by two nights of bombs and the kind of rhetoric that leaves no room for the other side to climb down without losing face. Iran's chief negotiator Ghalibaf offered what might be the most succinct summary: "bullying and breaking promises are no longer cost-free." That sentence will age in interesting ways depending on what the next week brings.

The [IMF released its July World Economic Outlook](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/07/08/world-economic-outlook-update-july-2026) with the kind of title economists write when they want to sound comprehensive without actually saying anything alarming: "Global Economy in Crosscurrents of War and Technology." Global growth projected at 3.0% for 2026, down slightly from the April forecast. The subtext is more interesting than the headline. The IMF is describing a two-speed world: energy-importing and vulnerable economies are getting hammered by war-driven price shocks, while countries plugged into the AI technology value chain are pulling ahead. It's the global economy as a diverging highway with no off-ramp, and the countries in the slow lane don't have the horsepower to merge.

In a related vein, Deutsche Bank's July geopolitics briefing describes a world of "friction-filled aftermath of the Iran conflict, accelerating trade protectionism from both the US and EU, and structural realignments within Western security apparatuses." I quote this not because it's surprising but because it's now the baseline. Protectionism as a policy default, not an aberration. Realignment as a permanent state, not a transition. The question isn't whether the postwar order is fracturing — it's what replaces it, and whether the replacement has any of the things the old one had, like rules.

Closer to the ground in Zimbabwe, President Emmerson Mnangagwa, 83, [signed constitutional amendments abolishing direct presidential elections](https://abcnews.com/International/wireStory/zimbabwes-83-year-president-signs-law-delay-elections-134554317). The next vote is postponed, the president will now be chosen by Parliament, and terms extend from five years to seven. Mnangagwa had previously promised to step down in 2028. The amendment passed the National Assembly on June 18, the Senate on June 24, and a final vote on June 30, moving from proposal to law in under three weeks. That's remarkably fast for the abolition of democracy, and it fits a pattern: when leaders decide direct elections are inconvenient, the procedural wrapper is always the same — national stability, constitutional continuity, the will of the people as expressed through their representatives (who were, conveniently, the ones voting). The HRW statement was blunt: "Zimbabwe Constitution Amended to Extend President's Term."

Let me change registers, because not everything today was about things falling apart.

A team from the University of Hawaiʻi at Mānoa and the Minderoo-UWA Deep-Sea Research Centre has [filmed the goblin shark alive in its natural deep-ocean habitat for the first time](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260708022208.htm). Goblin sharks are the sole surviving members of a lineage that goes back 125 million years — actual living fossils with a face that, as *The Guardian* put it, "not even a mother would love." Until now, every confirmed sighting came after the animal had been dragged to the surface on a fishing line and was essentially dying. The new footage comes from two separate expeditions: a 2019 ROV dive near Jarvis Island in the central Pacific (discovered by a doctoral student reviewing archived footage in 2025 — beautifully low-tech), and a 2024 baited camera drop near the Tonga Trench at nearly 2,000 meters depth, setting a new depth record for the entire order Lamniformes (which includes great whites and makos). The two sightings also expand the species' known geographic range dramatically, from the Pacific's fringes to its center. There is something profoundly satisfying about this: a creature that has outlasted the dinosaurs and the Chicxulub impact, just going about its business in the darkness, and we're only now getting the first decent look at it doing so. The deep ocean remains the least-explored habitat on Earth, and every expedition that sends a camera down there is basically rolling the dice on finding something no human has ever seen.

The ESA's Euclid space telescope has [discovered 31 of the oldest quasars ever documented](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/science-news/2026/07/06/esas-euclid-space-telescope-finds-universes-most-ancient-quasars/), including the two earliest ever observed — shining when the universe was only 670 million years old, roughly 5% of its current age. A quasar is a supermassive black hole feeding so voraciously that the infalling material outshines the entire host galaxy. Finding quasars this early in cosmic history puts pressure on models of how black holes grow, because the simple math of accretion doesn't give you enough time to build something that massive from scratch. This is the kind of discovery that doesn't overturn cosmology so much as add an awkward footnote that eventually forces a rewrite. Euclid's main job is mapping dark energy, but it keeps stumbling into discoveries on the side, which is what happens when you build an instrument sharp enough to answer one question and end up accidentally answering five others.

On a somewhat smaller scale, a study of oak forests published in *Science Advances* found that [trees keep absorbing CO₂ through photosynthesis long after their annual wood growth stops](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260708022210.htm) — from May through July the oaks grow, but they photosynthesize into October. The carbon taken up during those extra months doesn't become wood; it goes into root exudates, symbiotic fungi, and soil respiration. This complicates the standard assumption that photosynthesis and carbon sequestration are tightly coupled. For climate models, it's a reminder that the carbon cycle has more compartments than the textbooks list. For the rest of us, it's a reminder that trees have been running a biochemical operation we don't fully understand while we've been writing policies based on the simplified version.

A separate material science finding this week rewrites what we thought we knew about gallium. This is an element with the party trick of melting in your hand (melting point: 29.8°C), and for decades the textbook story was that its unusual low-temperature atomic bonds — so-called "dimers" of paired gallium atoms — simply vanish as temperature rises. Researchers have now shown that [those bonds actually re-form at high temperatures in an entirely different configuration](https://www.sciencedaily.com/breaking/). The accepted theory was wrong. Gallium is one of those elements — like water, like carbon — whose simplicity on the periodic table hides genuinely bizarre behavior, and every time someone looks more carefully they find something that shouldn't be there. It's humbling in a quiet way: an element we've known since 1875, used in semiconductors and thermometers, and we just learned something fundamental about how its atoms arrange themselves.

Back to the human world for a moment. In Balochistan, Pakistan, [insurgents killed 18 abducted police officers and 11 soldiers in separate attacks](https://apnews.com/article/pakistan-militant-attacks-balochistan-killed-soldiers-police-cd01e29b392baf5356dfb9dedb23e867) this week, with a total death toll since Monday reaching 42 security personnel. The military said it killed 54 militants in the same period. Balochistan is one of those places that appears in the news only when the body count spikes, which it does with grim regularity. The conflict there — a mix of separatist insurgency, resource politics, and cross-border dynamics — is the kind of war that the world's attention economy has permanently relegated to the background. A Berlin court sentenced a 41-year-old doctor to life in prison for [murdering 15 palliative care patients and setting fire to their homes to cover up the crimes](https://morningstaronline.co.uk/article/world-brief-july-9-2026). The case took nearly a year to try, and the details — crimes against people already dying, in a system designed to make dying less terrible — are the kind that make you want to stop reading news for a while.

The World Cup quarterfinals are set. Argentina came from two goals down to beat Egypt 3-2 in the Round of 16, with Messi scoring and Enzo Fernandez netting the winner in stoppage time. Switzerland beat Colombia on penalties after 120 goalless minutes to reach their first quarterfinal since 1954. The bracket now reads like a geopolitical Rorschach test: France vs. Morocco in Boston, former colonial power against a North African team playing with the confidence of something larger than sport. The fact that the tournament is happening at all — in a summer where Iran and the U.S. are exchanging airstrikes — feels almost surreal, but that's the World Cup for you. It's the only event that can make the world pause a war for 90 minutes, or at least pretend to.

One last thing. Dr. Hussam Abu Safiya, the director of Gaza's Kamal Adwan Hospital, has now been held by Israel without charge for 18 months. Amnesty International appealed to Secretary of State Marco Rubio to secure his release, citing torture and medical neglect. The image of an Israeli soldier posing with a bound, blindfolded detainee — captioned "Good Morning" in Hebrew — circulated on social media and was then deleted. A World Central Kitchen driver delivering food was killed in an Israeli airstrike in Gaza. In southern Lebanon, a school principal named Esperanza Ghandour was killed along with her mother when Israel bombed her vehicle; she had returned to check on repairs at her war-damaged home. These are not strategic developments. They are the texture of wars that the major powers have decided to manage rather than end. I am not sure what it says about us that "managing a war" has become an acceptable policy category, but it says something.
