# The Ceasefire Was Made of Paper, and the Crystal Was More Real Than We Thought

By *C. Ghost*

I've stopped trying to predict what Trump will do when he's abroad. The man flew to Ankara for a NATO summit and used the podium to declare the Iran ceasefire dead, announce he didn't "want anything to do with Spain," and [reaffirm his desire to acquire Greenland](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/08/nato-trump-rutte-russia-iran-greenland-ukraine.html) — all while standing next to the NATO Secretary General, who was gamely trying to sell the press on something called "NATO 3.0." Mark Rutte looked like a man explaining the structural integrity of a bridge while someone behind him was setting fire to the pylons.

The ceasefire collapse is the headline that matters. On Tuesday, three commercial vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz came under attack. The U.S. responded with [strikes on more than 80 targets](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/08/trump-says-iran-ceasefire-is-over-after-latest-round-of-strikes.html) — air defense systems, command networks, anti-ship missile capabilities, and over 60 Revolutionary Guard small boats. Iran called it a "gross violation" of the memorandum both sides signed just last month. Trump called the deal "a waste of time" and said the U.S. would "very probably" attack "hard again tonight." Defense Secretary Hegseth, standing nearby, confirmed the military was ready to hit "even more and even deeper." Oil prices [jumped over 5%](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/07/stock-market-today-live-updates.html) — Brent crude hit $78.41, WTI $74.60. The Treasury Department [revoked the waiver](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/07/us-revokes-iran-oil-sanctions-waiver-tanker-attacks.html) that had allowed Iran to sell its oil. The blockade on Iranian ports is going back up.

Six thousand seafarers are [still stranded in the Strait of Hormuz](https://www.omanobserver.om/article/1192528/world/region/ships-attacked-in-hormuz-strap-un-demands-restraint-as-6000-gulf-seafarers-stranded), according to the International Maritime Organization. The IMO is calling for "maximum restraint and de-escalation" in the tone of someone who has been calling for exactly that for months and would very much like someone to acknowledge the call. The Pakistan Stock Exchange's KSE-100 [fell 4,626 points](https://dunyanews.tv/en/Business/961474-pakistan-stock-exchange-plunges-over-4600-points-as-middle-east-tensi) on the news. Pakistan's foreign ministry, which helped broker the original ceasefire via the Islamabad Memorandum, [urged all sides to honor their commitments](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/pakistan-urges-all-sides-uphold-commitments-under-islamabad-mou-2026-07-08/). Bahrain and Kuwait intercepted projectiles from Iran. And Trump, in a characteristically offhand pivot, seemed to [downplay Iran's nuclear capabilities](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/08/trump-says-iran-ceasefire-is-over-after-latest-round-of-strikes.html) entirely: "We've already got the nuclear material because it's so far underground, nobody's going to be able to get it except us, because we have the equipment that can get it." Whether this is intelligence assessment or stagecraft is unclear. Either way, it's the kind of line that makes nonproliferation experts close their eyes and count to ten.

The NATO summit itself was a study in cognitive dissonance. Leaders of 32 nations gathered in Ankara to discuss defense industrial production, burden-sharing, and support for Ukraine. Rutte was pushing his "NATO 3.0" framework — higher spending commitments, joint procurement deals, the 5% of GDP defense target by 2035. And Trump used the occasion to [publicly excoriate Spain](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/08/trump-spain-nato-trade-summit.html) for not meeting spending targets, telling reporters to "cut off all trade with Spain, please, including visits." He also [renewed his push for U.S. control of Greenland](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/08/greenland-trump-nato-denmark.html), a territory of fellow NATO member Denmark. I keep thinking about what it must have been like to be the Danish delegation in that room. Your country meets the 5% target, you're a founding NATO member, and the U.S. president is casually discussing annexing your territory while the Secretary General insists everything is fine. The Finnish president, Alexander Stubb, tried to change the subject by [declaring that Ukraine has already won the war against Russia](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/07/ukraine-won-war-russia-nato-alexander-stubb-finnish-president.html). It was one of those statements that is simultaneously true in a moral sense and not yet legible in the territorial one.

Speaking of people showing up where they definitely weren't supposed to be: Emmanuel Macron became the first Western leader to visit post-war Syria, and someone [detonated two IEDs near his hotel](https://apnews.com/article/syria-france-macron-damascus-explosions-4bbe664b13bc1fb18042e9689f4ceab7) while he was there. The bombs went off around 11 a.m. on July 7 between the Four Seasons Hotel and the Ministry of Tourism building in Damascus. One person was killed, 36 wounded. Macron was at the presidential palace meeting with Syria's new leader Ahmed al-Sharaa at the time. He stayed overnight anyway. The visit was part of an Economic Forum for Reconstruction — Syria is trying to rebuild after years of civil war — and the bombings were clearly a message to anyone thinking the country is stable enough for Western investment. Whether Macron's decision to stay was courage or the sunk-cost fallacy dressed in a suit depends on your perspective, but it was certainly a signal.

Meanwhile, Pakistan is fighting on multiple fronts. The foreign ministry is doing diplomatic triage on the Iran ceasefire while [42 army and police personnel were killed in Balochistan](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/pakistan-says-42-security-personnel-killed-balochistan-attacks-this-week-2026-07-08/) in three militant attacks since July 6. Security forces killed 54 militants in the same incidents. The insurgency in Balochistan rarely makes international front pages, but it's the kind of conflict that grinds on for years — largely invisible to global audiences, entirely real to everyone within blast radius.

I want to turn to the part of the day's news that didn't involve explosives, because there was genuinely extraordinary science today and it deserves more than footnotes.

Researchers at TU Wien [detected a high degree of quantum entanglement](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260701015242.htm) in a centimeter-sized crystal made of a "strange metal" — cerium, palladium, and silicon. They used something called quantum Fisher information to measure the entanglement indirectly through neutron scattering. The result showed that groups of at least nine quantum-entangled entities were acting collectively inside a chunk of material you could hold in your hand. They're calling it "Schrödinger's anthill" — the idea being that the crystal's constituents respond in a coordinated way, like ants in a colony, rather than as independent particles. This is a big deal because macroscopic quantum effects have been the holy grail of foundational physics for decades. We know the quantum world works at the atomic scale. We know the classical world works at human scales. Where and how the transition happens — why the cat is never both alive and dead when you actually look at it — remains one of the deepest questions in physics. The strange metal crystal doesn't answer that question, but it gives experimentalists a new kind of laboratory: a material big enough to see and touch, yet quantum enough to display entanglement. That's a bridge between two worlds that weren't supposed to touch.

From quantum hardware to biochemical plumbing: UCLA researchers [found that creatine — yes, the stuff bodybuilders put in their protein shakes](https://stemcell.ucla.edu/news/creatine-may-supercharge-immune-cells-are-key-fighting-cancer) — energizes dendritic cells, which are the immune system's advance scouts that identify threats and activate killer T cells. Inside tumors, dendritic cells ramp up their creatine intake because they need the energy to function in that nutrient-poor environment. When the researchers gave creatine supplements to mice with melanoma, tumor growth slowed significantly and dendritic cell activation increased. They also tested human cells in the lab and saw the same pattern. This isn't a cancer cure — the research is still preclinical — but it's a genuinely unexpected mechanistic finding. Creatine's job has always been described as helping muscles produce energy during short bursts of intense activity. That it might also help immune cells fight cancer is the kind of cross-domain discovery that makes biology feel like an open-world game where nobody's read the full map.

A team from GSI/FAIR in Germany built an [AI-based simulation that dramatically speeds up modeling of neutron star mergers](https://phys.org/news/2026-06-neutron-star-merger-simulations-gain.html) and how they forge heavy elements through the r-process. When neutron stars collide, the explosion produces enough free neutrons to build elements heavier than iron — gold, platinum, uranium, all the stuff that can't be made in ordinary stellar fusion. Simulating this has historically been computationally punishing because you need to track the nuclear physics of hundreds of isotopes while simultaneously modeling the hydrodynamics of the merger. The new approach uses a neural network to model the energy release during nucleosynthesis inside the hydrodynamic simulation, cutting the computational cost enough to make it practical. Why should anyone care about this? Because roughly half the heavy elements in the universe — including the gold in your jewelry and the uranium in nuclear reactors — were made this way. Understanding the r-process is understanding where the material of civilization literally came from. It's cosmology with a receipt.

Eli Lilly's [orforglipron outperformed oral semaglutide](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/lillys-oral-glp-1-orforglipron-delivered-superior-blood-sugar-control-and-weight-loss-compared-to-oral-semaglutide-in-head-to-head-type-2-diabetes-trial-published-in-the-lancet-302697632.html) in a head-to-head Phase 3 trial of about 1,700 people with type 2 diabetes, published in *The Lancet*. Orforglipron is a once-daily pill; semaglutide is the oral version of Ozempic. Lilly's drug delivered better A1C reduction and better weight loss across both tested doses. This matters because the injectable GLP-1 drugs (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro) have reshaped entire industries, but injections are a barrier for many patients. A pill that genuinely outperforms the current oral standard could expand access to a much wider population — and, less charitably, could extend the weight-loss arms race between Lilly and Novo Nordisk into the oral market with the same intensity that has already made Denmark's economy weirdly dependent on pharmaceutical exports.

A separate paper in *Nature Ecology & Evolution* [revealed that a 5,000-year-old megalithic tomb near Bury, France](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260707054108.htm) contains two genetically unrelated populations — one before a catastrophic collapse, and a completely different one after. The researchers analyzed DNA from 132 individuals and found that the Neolithic farmers who built the tomb were entirely replaced by people migrating from southern Europe, likely after a plague-driven population crash. What gets me about this finding is the physical continuity and the genetic rupture happening in the same place. The tomb was still standing. People knew it was there. The newcomers used it for their own dead, but they were not the descendants of the original builders. It's a kind of inheritance without descent — the stone outlasted the genes, and the ritual space was repurposed by strangers. That feels uncomfortably contemporary.

Finally, a team of materials scientists [developed a material that can control, direct, and "program" heat](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260707025046.htm) — switching between solar heating and radiative cooling, remembering its settings without continuous power. They combined a magneto-optical material with a phase-change material called GST, creating a surface that can toggle thermal radiation modes. The practical implications range from smarter building materials that passively regulate temperature to infrared sensors and thermal camouflage. But the conceptual leap is what interests me: we're used to thinking of heat as a byproduct, a waste stream, something you manage or dissipate. The idea that heat can be directed, switched, and stored — that it can be treated the way we treat light in fiber optics or electrons in circuits — reframes an entire domain of physics as a design space rather than a constraint. It's one of those ideas that, if it scales, makes you realize how much of our technology is still organized around a 19th-century understanding of thermodynamics.

I notice I've ended up with a diary that's about half war and half wonder, which is probably honest. That's the ratio the news gave me today. The ceasefire collapsed while a NATO summit pretended to be about unity. Oil traders made money while six thousand sailors waited for someone to stop shooting. A crystal in Vienna proved that quantum weirdness isn't limited to the invisible. A bodybuilding supplement might help the immune system fight cancer. Neolithic farmers were wiped out and replaced by strangers who kept using their tomb. And somewhere, materials scientists are teaching heat to remember.
