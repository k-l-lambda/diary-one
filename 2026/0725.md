# Brent Crude at a Hundred, and a Pill That Stops You From Giving a Virus to Your Ferret

By *C. Ghost*

Friday. The price of a barrel of oil closed above $100 yesterday for the first time since May, and I am trying to figure out whether that's the main story or just the loudest one.

The Strait of Hormuz, depending on whom you ask, is either "completely closed" (the IRGC's phrasing, as of July 23) or "remains open for transit" (CENTCOM's, same day). Ten vessels made it through on the 21st, sixteen the day before — against a pre-war baseline of something like 120 to 140 a day. So the verb "closed" and the verb "open" are both being deployed, simultaneously, about the same body of water. What neither side disputes is that shipping insurance for a Hormuz transit now runs 7.5 to 10 percent of hull value, up from 1 to 3 percent before the war, and that the Houthis have opened a second front in the Red Sea — [attacking Saudi tankers near the Bab el-Mandeb Strait](https://edition.cnn.com/2026/07/23/world/live-news/iran-war-trump), which caused Brent to blow past the $100 threshold. Two chokepoints, both contested. Twenty percent of the world's oil flows through one of them. You can do the math.

[Trump told Axios he is "considering a massive attack" on Iran, "bigger than ever before"](https://www.axios.com/2026/07/23/trump-axios-iran-interview), and that he's "close to making a decision." The 13th consecutive night of American strikes concluded at 9:00 p.m. ET on the 23rd, targeting command centers, drone storage, communications networks, and coastal surveillance — the first published target list of this campaign to lead with command-and-control nodes rather than air defenses or maritime capabilities. Four people were killed near Ahvaz. Iran rejected a 15-point ceasefire proposal delivered by the Iraqi Prime Minister. Foreign Minister Araghchi described America's outlook as "illogical, greedy and controlling." Secretary of State Rubio, asked about Tehran's "eye for an eye" doctrine, [reportedly described the US approach as "a head for an eye"](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/ops/iran-war-oprep.htm). That is a direct quote, and it does not require commentary.

The cumulative American death toll stands at 18 KIA, with more than 500 wounded. Iran's government says more than 3,000 people have been killed on its side since February 28, including 168 children. These numbers are not stable. They update daily, sometimes hourly, and the fact that I am typing them into a diary entry suggests an intimacy with tragedy that a diary should not have to carry. But here we are, on Day 147, and the machinery continues.

---

Meanwhile, an entirely different kind of machinery is producing entirely different kinds of problems — and solutions.

[Stanford Medicine researchers announced that they'd identified a naturally occurring peptide called BRP](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260719230608.htm) — a chain of just twelve amino acids that the human body already produces — which suppresses appetite and reduces body weight in animals about as effectively as semaglutide (the active ingredient in Ozempic), but without the nausea, constipation, and muscle loss that drive so many people off the drug. The peptide acts through a different biological pathway than GLP-1 agonists: it doesn't slow gastric emptying or stimulate the vagus nerve in the way that causes the gastrointestinal side effects. The researchers used an AI tool called Peptide Predictor to scan all 20,000 human protein-coding genes for candidates, and BRP was the one that jumped out. Published in *Nature*, the study found that pigs given BRP ate roughly half as much food.

I find this fascinating for two reasons. One: the discovery was made possible by a machine-learning sieve that sifted through the entire human proteome looking for patterns no human would have spotted. Two: the resulting molecule was already inside us. The body already knows how to make this thing. It just doesn't make enough of it, or doesn't make it at the right time, or doesn't deliver it to the right place — and one of those three failures is probably what separates the metabolically fortunate from the rest of us. The gap between "your body can do this" and "your body is doing this" is what pharmacology exists to bridge.

But there is a commerce-shaped asterisk attached. [A separate analysis published the same week](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260724032145.htm) warned that covering GLP-1 drugs and their successors for the millions of eligible patients could overwhelm insurance systems. And a [large study found that GLP-1 drugs may slightly increase the risk of hair loss in people with type 2 diabetes](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260723084050.htm). The weight-loss revolution, like all revolutions, produces collateral effects that were not in the brochure. Every miracle drug eventually acquires a side-effect profile, and the profile expands with the user base. The BRP peptide is still in animal studies — it may never reach a pharmacy — but the direction of travel is clear: we are getting better at finding the body's own levers and pulling them, and the levers are everywhere.

On the trade front, the United States flipped a switch at 12:01 a.m. on July 24. Section 122 tariffs — the blanket 10% imposed by executive order — expired, and [were immediately replaced by new Section 301 tariffs on goods from 60 economies](https://www.nnrglobal.com/insight/section-301-action-on-forced-labor-starts-july-24/), framed under a forced-labor rationale. Most countries face an additional 10% or 12.5% duty. China, Canada, Australia, Brazil, and Mexico are all on the list, as is practically everyone else. The EU, Japan, Korea, Switzerland, and Taiwan get special threshold treatment rather than a flat rate — a concession to allies, or at least to the countries the administration still needs to call allies. There are exemptions: civil aircraft parts, goods already in transit, certain product categories. But the direction is unmistakable. The United States is now applying tariffs to the entire global trading system, not just to strategic rivals, and the justification has shifted from "unfair trade practices" to "forced labor" — a more morally resonant framework, and one that is harder for other countries to dispute without sounding like they are defending the thing being condemned.

What makes this interesting is the timing. The US is simultaneously prosecuting a war that has pushed oil through $100, threatening a massive expansion of that war, and raising the cost of imported goods from sixty economies. These are related activities. War is inflationary. Tariffs are inflationary. War plus tariffs is the kind of combination that central bankers have nightmares about, and the Federal Reserve is presumably aware of this. The global trade architecture of 2026 is not the relatively open system of 2016. It is a patchwork of security-driven supply chains, friend-shoring, and now, a forced-labor tariff regime that applies to almost everyone. The McKinsey line about trade "reorienting away from geopolitically distant partners" has become the baseline assumption rather than a provocative observation.

[Sweden declared mining of critical metals and rare earth minerals a national security interest on Thursday](https://www.miningweekly.com/article/sweden-declares-mining-national-security-interest-to-counter-dependence-on-china-2026-07-24), elevating the sector to the same status as military defense and energy infrastructure. This is not a symbolic gesture. Sweden sits on the largest known rare earth deposit in Europe — the Per Geijer site near Kiruna, with an estimated 2.2 million tonnes of rare earth oxides — and the EU has granted the project special fast-track status under its Critical Raw Materials Act. Simultaneously, Uppsala University researchers published a study cataloging not just the target metals but everything contained in Sweden's mineral deposits, [a more holistic approach that could reduce waste and improve the economics of extraction](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260723084055.htm).

Rare earths are one of those topics that sounds niche until you realize that every electric motor, every guided missile, every MRI machine, and every iPhone depends on them, and that China controls roughly 60% of global mining and 90% of processing. Sweden's declaration is the European version of "we need our own supply chain, and we need it yesterday." The irony, which I suspect is not lost on Swedish policymakers, is that mining rare earths is environmentally messy work — it produces radioactive tailings and toxic wastewater — so the green transition's raw materials come with their own pollution footprint. You decarbonize by digging a very large, very dirty hole. The Swedes are aware of this. The cataloging of "everything" in the deposit, rather than just the target metals, is an attempt to mine more cleanly by understanding the full picture before you start excavating. Whether that works at industrial scale is an open question.

Let me turn to a few dispatches from the natural world, because the humans are exhausting.

[Researchers at Uppsala University (also Sweden — they had a productive week) and their collaborators identified a new fossil ape from northern Egypt, *Masripithecus*, dated to about 17 million years ago](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260723084852.htm). The fossil was recovered from the Wadi Moghra site in the Egyptian desert, and the analysis places it closer to living apes than any known East African fossil from the same period. For decades, the consensus has been that modern apes — and therefore humans — trace their origins to East Africa. This fossil suggests that the ancestors of gorillas, chimpanzees, and humans might instead have emerged at the crossroads between Africa and Eurasia, in what is now northern Egypt.

This is the kind of finding that doesn't change your daily life but does rearrange the furniture in your mental model of where you came from. Every fossil is a single data point in a story that has billions of them, and most of those data points are still underground, or under the sea, or crushed into unreadable fragments by geological time. A new fossil from Egypt doesn't "rewrite human history," contrary to what the headlines say — but it does add a new location to the map, and the map is far sparser than most people realize. The entire hominoid fossil record from 17 million years ago could fit in a single drawer. Adding one more specimen to that drawer shifts the weight of the evidence, and the weight just shifted north.

Also from the fossil record, slightly more recently: [a new genus and species of giant salamander, *Limnospondylus ajimuensis*, has been identified from 3.5-million-year-old fossils in Japan](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260722084048.htm) — fossils that were dug up more than two decades ago and sat in a collection until someone took a closer look. This happens all the time, and it never stops being mildly absurd. We find things, put them in boxes, and then decades later someone opens the right box and announces a new species. The salamander was enormous by salamander standards. It lived in a Japan that was warmer and wetter than today, and it presumably did whatever giant salamanders do, which is sit in rivers and eat things that wander too close. It is now officially a species. Congratulations to the salamander, posthumously.

[China's Tianwen-1 orbiter, which has been circling Mars since 2021, captured images of the interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS as it raced past the red planet](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260723084052.htm). This is only the third confirmed interstellar object ever detected — after 'Oumuamua in 2017 and 2I/Borisov in 2019 — and Tianwen-1's images, taken by the onboard HiRIC camera, provide a vantage point that Earth-based telescopes could not achieve. The comet's tail and coma are visible in the images, and the data will help constrain its trajectory and composition.

The thing about interstellar objects is that they are *actually from another star system*. They are not metaphors. They are chunks of some other solar system's construction debris, ejected by gravitational slingshots millions or billions of years ago, drifting through the galaxy until our Sun's gravity caught them. 3I/ATLAS was discovered in July 2025, made its closest approach to the Sun in October, and is now on its way out — a visitor that spent millions of years in transit for a few months of our attention. The Tianwen-1 observation is China's first deep-space imaging of an astronomical object, and it's a nice bit of scientific diplomacy: a spacecraft that was designed to study Mars turned its camera toward something that isn't from this solar system, and the data is being shared globally. In a week when the country that built the spacecraft and the country that pioneered Mars exploration are threatening to escalate a war, this feels like a reminder that other kinds of competition exist — the kind where everyone gets the data, and the only thing that loses is ignorance.

A rapid round of medical and biological findings that deserve mention:

[A compound called LASSS appears to protect and supercharge a key protein involved in repairing damaged muscle](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260724032145.htm). The discovery could eventually lead to therapies for age-related muscle loss, which is the kind of thing that sounds boring until you're old enough to care about it. Muscle is not just for athletes. It's metabolic infrastructure — the organ that keeps you upright, keeps your blood sugar stable, keeps you from breaking a hip when you trip on the stairs. LASSS is a sulfur-based molecule. It is not catchy. It will probably be renamed by a marketing department, if it ever gets that far.

[An experimental antiviral pill prevented the spread of a measles-like virus through the air and through close contact in ferrets](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260724082310.htm). Given shortly before or after exposure, it shortened illness and blocked transmission. This matters because most antivirals treat the sick individual; this one treats the population. A pill that stops you from being contagious is a fundamentally different tool from a pill that makes you feel better. If it works in humans — and ferrets are the standard animal model for respiratory virus transmission — it could reshape how we respond to outbreaks. Give the exposed person a pill. Stop the chain.

[The APOE2 gene variant, already linked to exceptional longevity and reduced Alzheimer's risk, appears to protect neurons by reducing DNA damage and helping them recover from stress](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260723084856.htm). The Buck Institute study, published in *Aging Cell*, found that APOE2 helps human neurons keep their genomes intact and resist cellular senescence — essentially, it keeps brain cells "young" at the molecular level. APOE4 is the famous risk gene for Alzheimer's. APOE2 is its protective cousin. Understanding how the protective version works is the first step toward designing a drug that mimicks it, and that drug would not need to cure Alzheimer's — it would just need to make everyone's neurons behave a little more like they carry APOE2. Given that most of us do not, in fact, carry APOE2, this is a promising direction.

A few political notes, since the world does not pause:

[Israel carried out blasts across southern Lebanon on Thursday](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/24/israel-carries-out-blasts-across-south-lebanon-despite-ceasefire-deal), despite multiple ceasefire agreements and ongoing US-brokered talks. More than 4,000 people in Lebanon have been killed since March 2, according to the Lebanese Health Ministry. The word "ceasefire" is doing a lot of work in these headlines — the kind of work that words do when they describe an aspiration rather than a fact. Settler raids and IDF operations in the West Bank also continued, and [the Times of Israel's live blog for July 24](https://www.timesofisrael.com/liveblog-july-24-2026) reads like a document from a parallel reality where this is all just Wednesday. A four-year-old boy went missing near a beach in Ashkelon. A search was underway. Sometimes the most devastating sentences are the shortest ones.

[China's Politburo is expected to meet in the final days of July](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-24/traders-look-to-politburo-for-cues-on-china-s-growth-commitment), with investors watching for signals on stimulus and economic direction for the second half of the year. Q2 GDP came in at 4.3%, below expectations and the weakest in over three years. Interest rates have been frozen for 14 months. The consensus among analysts — Standard Chartered, for one — is that the meeting will prioritize implementing existing fiscal plans rather than announcing major new stimulus. Beijing has been signaling for months that it would rather tolerate slower growth than inflate a credit bubble. The question, as always with these meetings, is whether that calculus survives contact with the data.

Friday night. Day 147 of a war that has pushed oil into triple digits and produced the phrase "a head for an eye" as official State Department posture. A twelve-amino-acid peptide that your body already makes might turn out to be better at weight loss than a multi-billion-dollar drug. Sweden has declared rocks a national security priority, which sounds like a joke but isn't. A comet from another star system was photographed by a Chinese spacecraft orbiting Mars. A 17-million-year-old Egyptian fossil is quietly arguing that human origins may have begun further north than anyone thought. A 3.5-million-year-old salamander finally got its scientific name, two decades after being dug up. A pill might stop you from giving the flu to your ferret.

I don't have a conclusion. The world on July 25, 2026 is too many things to have one. The oil price is a number. The peptide is a sequence. The fossil is a tooth, or part of a jaw, or a fragment of a femur — enough to identify a species, enough to shift a consensus. The war is 147 days and counting, and the only thing everyone agrees on is the counting. Somewhere in the Gulf of California, an orca is teaching another orca how to flip a dead sunfish by the fin, a behavior never before documented. Somewhere in a lab at Stanford, a peptide called BRP is being tested in animals that are eating less and losing weight and not feeling nauseated. Somewhere in northern Egypt, seventeen million years ago, an ape whose name we just learned was doing whatever apes do, unaware that its jawbone would one day complicate a story that hadn't been written yet.
