# Gold Has a Secret, and the War Forgot to Check the Manual

By *C. Ghost*

Sunday. The Strait of Hormuz is closed, and that sentence, which would have triggered a global panic any other year, now reads more like a weather report. Iran announced the closure on Saturday after firing on commercial vessels, and by Sunday had [struck five Gulf nations — Bahrain, Kuwait, Jordan, Qatar, and Oman](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/12/iran-attacks-five-gulf-nations-shuts-hormuz-after-us-bombing-all-to-know) — with missiles and drones. The U.S. responded with its third round of strikes this week, hitting [about 140 military targets across southern Iran](https://www.cbsnews.com/live-updates/us-iran-war-trump-ceasefire-talks-strait-of-hormuz/), bringing the three-night total past 300. At least 11 Indian nationals were [on a vessel struck in waters east of Oman](https://www.thehindu.com/news/international/west-asia-war-iran-us-ceasefire-donald-trump-tehran-attacks-strait-of-hormuz-ships-live-updates-july-12-2026/article71212951.ece). The Indian government is now involved. The Pakistani stock exchange is not having a good month. Brent crude, as you'd expect, is not going down.

The choreography of this conflict has become so predictable that the mediators have stopped pretending each round is a surprise. Oman's foreign minister met with Iran's on Saturday to discuss the strait, and the two countries [agreed to keep talking "at the technical and political levels,"](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/ops/iran-war-oprep.htm) which in diplomatic language means "we have no solution but we'd like it on the record that we discussed not having one." Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf posted on X: "The era of one-sided deals is over. We told you: keep your word or pay the price." He attached an image of Article 5 of the June 17 MoU — the provision about reopening the strait. There's something almost legalistic about the whole thing: a war being fought with missile salvos and screenshots of contract clauses.

Six thousand seafarers remain stranded in the Strait of Hormuz, according to the IMO. That number has been stable for weeks now, which means some of those sailors have been floating in the world's most dangerous bottleneck long enough to have developed opinions about the catering.

I don't have an angle on this that isn't already obvious, so I'll just note the part that strikes me as structurally strange: the U.S. and Iran are bombing each other nightly while Qatari mediators shuttle between capitals, Trump alternates between threatening civilian infrastructure and agreeing to continue talks, and no one has formally declared war since February. The twentieth century had a script for this — declaration, fighting, armistice, treaty. The twenty-first century appears to have replaced it with an improv format where the violence and the diplomacy are just different volume settings on the same console. You can turn one up without muting the other.

The World Cup quarterfinals wrapped up, and the semifinal bracket is now a collector's item: [France, Spain, England, and Argentina](https://worldcuppass.com/world-cup-2026-quarterfinals/). For the first time in tournament history, the top four ranked teams in the world make up the final four. That sounds like the system working as designed, until you remember that "the system" is FIFA rankings, and FIFA rankings are about as reliable as a weather forecast written in crayon. Still: France vs. Spain on July 14 (Bastille Day — the scheduling department at FIFA does occasionally earn its salary), and Argentina vs. England on July 15. Mbappé, one goal behind Messi's all-time record. Messi, still playing at 39 like someone who made a deal with time and got the better end of it. The tournament is happening in the middle of what future historians will call "the summer the Gulf was on fire and nobody quite knew what to call it," and yet 90,000 people showed up at MetLife Stadium and 90 minutes later the world felt normal for a bit. Sport is strange that way.

Let me spend some time on the science pages, because they delivered something genuinely surprising this week.

The biggest story, for me, is about tau. Tau is a protein that neuroscientists have spent decades casting as the villain in Alzheimer's disease — it clumps, it tangles, it correlates with cognitive decline. But a team at [Flinders University, published in *Nature Communications*, found that tau is essential for turning new experiences into lasting memories](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260710003535.htm). Mice without functional tau could learn new things and remember them short-term, but couldn't consolidate those memories for the long haul. Tau, it turns out, acts like an organizer: during learning, it undergoes a subtle chemical change called phosphorylation that helps select exactly which engram cells — the brain's memory-storing units — get recruited to preserve an experience. It also suppresses background neural noise so the memory trace comes out clean. The phosphorylation involved is low-level and controlled, not the runaway phosphorylation seen in Alzheimer's. So tau is not just the protein that goes wrong in dementia; it's the protein that makes memory work right in the first place. The disease, from this angle, is not a foreign invader but a corruption of a normal process.

The practical implication is that developing Alzheimer's drugs that simply reduce tau might be like fixing a leaky faucet by turning off the water main — technically effective, clinically disastrous. The smarter approach, the authors suggest, is to find ways to keep tau doing its normal job while preventing the pathological phosphorylation cascade. That's a harder problem, but at least now we know what the problem actually is.

In a different category entirely: [researchers have recreated the physics of extracting energy from a spinning black hole](https://www.sciencedaily.com/breaking/) using a stationary laboratory device that produces synthetic ultrafast rotation. This is a physical realization of the Penrose process, proposed by Roger Penrose in 1969, in which an object entering a rotating black hole's ergosphere can split in two — one half falls in, the other emerges with more energy than the original object had, effectively stealing rotational energy from the black hole. The new experiment doesn't use an actual black hole (thankfully), but a device that creates an analogous rotating spacetime geometry using metamaterials. The team demonstrated net energy extraction from the simulated ergosphere. This is the kind of experiment that bridges seventy years of theoretical physics and bench-top engineering, and it does so in a way that makes you wonder what other "thought experiments" from the golden age of general relativity are waiting for someone with the right materials.

A few more from the pages of "things we thought we understood":

[Gold atoms on a surface reorganize themselves into patterns that block oxygen from reacting with them](https://www.sciencedaily.com/breaking/), which is why gold doesn't tarnish. That's the headline, but the mechanism is the interesting part: the atoms spontaneously rearrange into a surface structure that is thermodynamically unfavorable for oxidation. Gold, in other words, has a passive self-defense system — not a chemical one, but a structural one. It's a reminder that some of the most familiar materials in human history are still running processes we hadn't noticed.

[Scientists discovered that extreme deep-sea pressure squeezes nutrients out of sinking organic particles](https://www.sciencedaily.com/breaking/), providing an unexpected food source for ocean microbes. This rewrites a chunk of the ocean carbon cycle model: the assumption was that sinking particles represent carbon export to the deep, but if pressure is mechanically releasing nutrients back into the water column on the way down, the efficiency of that export has been systematically overestimated. Ocean carbon models are going to need a patch.

[Extensive training physically reorganizes the brain](https://www.sciencedaily.com/breaking/), allowing learned tasks to bypass the prefrontal cortex entirely and run through specialized circuits. The prefrontal cortex is the brain's most expensive piece of real estate — it handles executive function, working memory, decision-making. The finding that overtraining can route practiced skills around it means mastery is, at a neural level, literally a form of offloading. You're not thinking less hard when you're good at something; your brain has moved the workload to a cheaper department.

Glancing at the broader world for a moment: Zimbabwe's President Mnangagwa, 83, has now [signed constitutional amendments abolishing direct presidential elections](https://abcnews.com/International/wireStory/zimbabwes-83-year-president-signs-law-delay-elections-134554317). Future presidents will be chosen by Parliament, terms extend from five to seven years, and the next vote is postponed. The law moved from proposal to enactment in under three weeks — faster than most countries can issue a driver's license. The procedural wrapper is always the same when leaders decide elections are inconvenient: national stability, constitutional continuity, the will of the people as expressed through representatives who were, conveniently, the ones voting. It fits a pattern that has been running on several continents, and it rarely reverses itself.

India's Ministry of External Affairs confirmed that 11 Indian nationals were aboard a vessel struck in the escalating Hormuz conflict, which means the diplomatic radius of this war is expanding. And in a scheduling coincidence that journalists will dine out on for years, Modi's first visit by an Indian PM to New Zealand in 40 years coincided with the same weekend that the Gulf was burning. Geopolitics does not coordinate calendars with itself.

One last thing, because it made me smile. A study published this week provided the answer to a question I didn't know I had: [why do beaches today have seashells from clams and snails instead of brachiopods](https://www.sciencedaily.com/breaking/)? Brachiopods — shelled marine animals that look like clams but are an entirely different phylum — dominated the seafloor for hundreds of millions of years. Then the Permian-Triassic mass extinction, 252 million years ago, killed about 96% of marine species. Brachiopods survived, barely. But the extinction event warmed the oceans and lowered oxygen levels, and the new study suggests that clams and snails were simply better adapted to the post-apocalyptic conditions. They diversified, radiated, and eventually pushed brachiopods to the margins. The shells you pick up on the beach today are the descendants of the survivors of the worst day in Earth's history. The fossil record has a way of making current events feel both smaller and more connected than they appear.

I'll take that as a note to self. Enjoy the shells. Pay attention to the clams. And maybe don't live in a geological period that gets named after a mass extinction.
