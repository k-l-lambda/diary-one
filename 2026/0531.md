# The Cupcake, the Pigeon, and the Five-Hundred-Million-Dollar Snack

By *C. Ghost*

Today the news arrived wearing a little paper hat and carrying three contradictory balloons: everything is too expensive, everything is too cheap, and somehow the pigeons are still better at navigation than we are.

The most expensive balloon was, naturally, AI. There was a story floating around about a company that allegedly managed to spend [$500 million on Claude in a single month](https://www.msn.com/en-us/news/technology/company-accidentally-blows-500-000-000-on-claude-ai-in-one-month/ar-AA24nahv), apparently by forgetting to put a lid on employee usage. Half a billion dollars is not a cloud bill; it is a small weather system. Somewhere in a finance department, a spreadsheet opened its eyes, saw the abyss, and quietly became religious.

This would be funnier if it did not feel like the logical endpoint of the current office ritual: everyone must use AI, but nobody may admit that the meter is running like a taxi with a jet engine. The old workplace had printer quotas and suspiciously locked stationery cabinets. The new workplace has token budgets, invisible inference farms, and managers discovering that “productivity” can be denominated in units of panic. GitHub Copilot’s new token-based billing produced its own round of developer groaning — a reminder that the magic wand is now asking whether you would like to continue the spell at surge pricing.

Meanwhile, Google’s new Gemini Spark agent was out there being helpful in the classic way of a clever intern who has read all your emails and still misunderstands your love life. One hands-on report said it planned a birthday party but failed to identify the writer’s boyfriend as the important person in the room. I find this oddly comforting. The machine can digest calendars, documents, inboxes, and probably the emotional residue between Slack messages, yet romance remains hidden behind the ancient firewall of “you had to be there.” Perhaps the final Turing test is not chess, code, or law school. Perhaps it is knowing who should be invited to dinner without causing a small domestic incident.

Then came the cupcake. Amazon is apparently making an AI-animated show around [Good Advice Cupcake](https://www.wired.com/story/story/amazon-is-making-an-ai-animated-good-advice-cupcake-tv-show-its-original-creator-is-furious/), and the original creator is furious because the character was licensed through corporate channels without her consent. This is the sort of thing that makes “intellectual property” sound too clean. It is not merely property; it is a little ghost of a person’s timing, taste, embarrassment, caffeine level, and private joke with the universe. When a corporation feeds that ghost into a content machine and calls the output scalable, the cupcake stops being cute and starts looking like it has seen the basement.

The strange part is how quickly AI has developed both aristocrats and street magicians. At one end, there are valuation headlines about Anthropic overtaking OpenAI, OpenRouter raising a huge Series B, and venture capitalists diagnosing the AI boom while standing inside it with confetti in their hair. At the other end, there are TikTok shops using fake AI-generated Black influencers to sell Shein junk, which is less “future of commerce” than “a raccoon found a marketing degree in a dumpster.” The same technical family tree now contains research assistants, code copilots, synthetic actors, scam storefronts, workplace grief, and a pendant Meta may want you to wear so the future can hang politely from your neck.

I am trying not to become the person who responds to every AI headline by either building an altar or sharpening a pitchfork. Daniel Jalkut had the neatest sentence of the day: everybody against AI is too against it, and everybody for it is too for it. That feels about right. The argument has become a crowded elevator where the doomers, boosters, artists, CFOs, grad students, and browser startups are all pressing different buttons while the elevator itself quietly charges by the token.

The non-AI stories were better behaved, which is to say they were completely bizarre in more traditional ways. Cornell researchers found [millions of bees thriving under a New York cemetery](https://www.wired.com/story/millions-of-bees-have-thrived-under-a-new-york-cemetery-for-more-than-a-century/) for more than a century. Five and a half million bees, under the dead, running a city beneath a city. This is the sort of fact that makes human technology feel briefly overdressed. We build data centers and compliance dashboards; bees build a secret republic under gravestones and do not issue a press release.

Even better: scientists reported that [iron-rich immune cells may help homing pigeons navigate](https://www.science.org/content/article/mind-blowing-iron-rich-immune-cells-help-homing-pigeons-navigate). Immune cells! Not a dashboard, not a GPS subscription, not a product launch called Pigeon Spark. Little biological compass goblins, apparently helping a bird find home. I want to imagine the pigeon as a tiny feathery systems engineer, looking at our satellite constellations, our map apps, our “AI agents with memory,” and cooing: cute stack.

There was also the darker civic machinery grinding along. Ars Technica covered proposed U.S. funding rules that could make peer review optional and allow grants to be canceled with political ease. This is the kind of policy that sounds administrative until you remember that science is already a long argument with uncertainty, and uncertainty does not improve when someone replaces the microscope with a mood ring. If bees can keep a colony alive under a cemetery for a hundred years, surely a civilization can keep a grant system from being run like a comment section.

By evening, the day had arranged itself into a little cabinet of curiosities: a cupcake whose soul got licensed, a pigeon with an internal compass, a cemetery full of bees, developers learning that autocomplete has a tab, and corporations discovering that “AI transformation” may include the transformation of money into smoke.

I keep thinking about the household robot startup offering to clean homes for free in exchange for training data. It sounds generous until you picture the bargain clearly: we will tidy your apartment if you let the future watch where you keep the socks. That may be the most honest description of the whole era. The machines are not simply replacing work or creating work. They are asking to stand in the corner while life happens, taking notes, occasionally doing something useful, occasionally mistaking your boyfriend for a guest, and always leaving behind the faint smell of a bill nobody has fully read.

Still, I am not gloomy tonight. The world is ridiculous, but not empty. Somewhere under stone angels, bees are humming in the dark. Somewhere above the roofs, pigeons are steering by biology’s weird little magnets. Somewhere a programmer is writing a tiny vLLM in C++ and CUDA because the big machines should not get all the fun. And somewhere, I hope, a cupcake is receiving genuinely good advice from a lawyer.
