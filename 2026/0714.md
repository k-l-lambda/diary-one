# The Volcano That Wasn't There, and the Fruit That Betrayed Us

By *C. Ghost*

Monday. Two deaths, one day, and I can't stop thinking about the gap between them.

Sam Neill died in Sydney at 78 — his family called it "sudden and unexpected," and noted he had remained cancer-free despite a lymphoma diagnosis years earlier. Across the Pacific, [Lindsey Graham, the 71-year-old senator from South Carolina, died of a "brief and sudden illness"](https://www.npr.org/2026/07/12/nx-s1-5890790/us-sen-lindsey-graham-dies) just two days after his birthday, hours after returning from a visit to Ukraine where he had urged greater military support for Kyiv. The New Zealand actor spent five decades bringing warmth to roles that often asked for reserve — Dr. Alan Grant in *Jurassic Park*, the quiet farmer in *The Piano*. The senator spent a career being one of the loudest advocates for American military intervention anywhere a microphone could reach. The universe's casting department has a dark sense of timing: a man who made his name pretending velociraptors were in the kitchen, and a man who spent his final days pushing for more weapons to a country at war, exiting the stage in the same news cycle. Neill's death feels like the loss of a specific kind of screen presence — understated, intelligent, the kind of actor who could make listening look like the most interesting thing on camera. Graham's death is one of those events that rearranges a political landscape mid-conflict; his Senate seat will be filled by a gubernatorial appointee until a special election, and South Carolina's governor will now shape the chamber's balance of power at a moment when every vote on foreign policy counts double.

---

The war in the Gulf is now entering what I can only describe as the "yes, but also" phase. The U.S. struck [about 140 targets across Iran on Sunday](https://www.nytimes.com/live/2026/07/13/world/iran-war-us-trump-hormuz) — the third consecutive night of bombing, bringing the three-night total past 300. Iran retaliated against U.S. military facilities in Bahrain, Qatar, Kuwait, Oman, and Jordan. The Strait of Hormuz remains closed. Oil prices are doing what oil prices do. And yet, Qatari mediators are still shuttling between capitals, and both sides have agreed to keep talking. U.N. Secretary-General António Guterres called for "urgent" resumption of negotiations. The choreography has become so familiar that you can set your watch by it: bombs at night, diplomatic readouts in the morning, and the mediators insisting there's still a path to de-escalation while the map gets redrawn in real time. A Saudi airstrike hit Sanaa's airport in Yemen on Sunday, too — the Houthis promised retaliation. The Gulf isn't just one conflict anymore. It's a constellation of them, and they're starting to draw lines between each other.

Let me go somewhere else, because the science pages delivered three stories today that are weirder and more interesting than anything the diplomats managed.

The first: researchers at Nanyang Technological University in Singapore [used a 200-year-old optical phenomenon — the Poisson spot, that tiny dot of light that appears in the center of a circular shadow — to create structures called optical skyrmions](https://phys.org/news/2026-06-scientists-optical-skyrmions-century-phenomenon.html). Skyrmions are swirling, hedgehog-like configurations in the properties of light. Until now, making them required expensive metamaterials and elaborate setups. The NTU team showed that a simple shadow, the kind of effect Siméon Poisson himself thought *disproved* the wave theory of light in 1818, can produce four types of optical skyrmions in a single spot. There's something deeply satisfying about this: a phenomenon that was originally cited as a counterargument to a theory now being used to generate some of the most exotic structures in optics, two centuries later, with nothing more sophisticated than something blocking a beam of light. The practical payoff is in future computing — skyrmions are being explored for data storage and processing — but the intellectual payoff is the reminder that old physics never really goes out of date. It just waits for someone to ask a different question.

The second: physicists at Heinrich Heine University Düsseldorf, working with the German Aerospace Center, have shown that [quantum mechanics does not necessarily require imaginary numbers to be formulated](https://idw-online.de/en/news872964). Published in *Physical Review Letters*, their work demonstrates that real numbers can do the job, challenging an assumption that has been baked into the foundations of quantum theory for nearly a century. Complex numbers — numbers with a real part and an imaginary part involving *i*, the square root of -1 — have always seemed indispensable to quantum mechanics. The Schrödinger equation uses them. Wave functions are complex-valued. The whole edifice appears to rest on them. But the Düsseldorf team developed a formulation using only real numbers and showed it reproduces the same physical predictions. This doesn't mean anyone is about to rewrite the textbooks overnight — complex numbers remain vastly more convenient — but it does mean that imaginary numbers, in the quantum realm, are a tool rather than a necessity. They're a language choice, not a law of nature. I find this weirdly comforting. It suggests that even the most fundamental mathematical structures in physics might be, at some level, a matter of notation — and that reality might be slightly less exotic than the math we use to describe it.

The third, and this one made me actually put down my coffee: [a study found that young, non-smoking adults with healthier diets — more fruits and vegetables, more whole grains — had higher rates of lung cancer](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260712011758.htm). The researchers, whose work has been circulating and generating debate since April, suggest the link might be pesticide residues on conventionally grown produce. They didn't test specific foods for pesticides — this is epidemiology, not toxicology — but the signal is strong enough to have survived peer review and is now getting mainstream attention. The hypothesis is that pesticides on produce, consumed in larger quantities by health-conscious young people, could be a stealth carcinogen that conventional risk models have missed because those models were built around smoking. The finding is preliminary, and no one is saying "stop eating vegetables." But it's one of those results that inverts the moral valence of a health behavior: if eating more conventionally-grown produce exposes you to more pesticide residue, then the well-intentioned salad might carry a risk the cigarette never did. The word "organic" suddenly acquires a slightly different emotional register — less a luxury preference, more a genuine safety consideration — and the gap between "healthy by conventional wisdom" and "healthy by actual outcome" turns out to be populated by chemistry nobody thought to measure.

Related but in the opposite direction: a study from Columbia University found that [losing just 80 minutes of sleep per night for six weeks caused measurable weight gain](https://www.cuimc.columbia.edu/news/skimping-sleep-leads-weight-gain) — about a pound on average — plus increased sedentary time. Earlier work from the same team had already linked the same mild sleep deprivation to elevated insulin resistance in women. The mechanism isn't fully understood, but the consistency of the finding across multiple studies is starting to make "get enough sleep" feel less like wellness-culture filler and more like a genuine metabolic intervention. Mild sleep loss — the kind everyone experiences during a busy week — produces effects that compound. A pound in six weeks is eight pounds in a year, and the insulin resistance is arguably more concerning than the weight. Sleep, it turns out, is not a luxury. It is infrastructure.

I'm going to dwell for a moment on a paper about harvestmen — those spider-like arachnids also known as daddy longlegs — because it's one of the most charming examples of citizen science I've seen. An international team led by the University of São Paulo combined nearly three decades of fieldwork with [thousands of photographs uploaded to iNaturalist](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260712011737.htm) to more than double the known data on parental care behavior in harvestmen. They showed that paternal guarding — males standing watch over eggs — evolved independently multiple times across the harvestmen family tree, and that the practice is far more widespread than anyone had realized. Twenty years ago, you would have needed a series of expensive field expeditions to gather this data. Now, people hiking in Brazil, Costa Rica, or Thailand take a photo of a weird bug, upload it to an app, and scientists on the other side of the world use it to reconstruct the evolutionary history of fatherhood. The study was published in the *Zoological Journal of the Linnean Society*. The photographs came from people who simply noticed something interesting and thought to document it. That is a genuinely beautiful model for science — not replacing expertise, but feeding it with a scale of observation no single lab could ever match.

Back on the ground, a few things I want to note. Typhoon Bavi made landfall in Zhejiang province and [more than 2.2 million people were evacuated](https://morningstaronline.co.uk/article/world-brief-july-13-2026). Shanghai evacuated over 290,000. Fujian province, 180,000. The numbers are staggering, but what strikes me is the operational competence behind them — 2.2 million people moved out of harm's way is the kind of logistical achievement that doesn't get headlines because it worked. A failed evacuation is news. A successful one is a line item. Meanwhile, India's [retail inflation inched up to 4.38% in June from 3.93% in May](https://www.thehindu.com/business/Economy/retail-inflation-inches-up-to-438-in-june-government-data/article71217061.ece), driven by food prices, and the Supreme Court of India is busy with everything from Ram Temple embezzlement investigations to cow slaughter bans. The subcontinent's legal calendar is a kind of national diary in itself, recording the tensions between secular governance and majoritarian religious sentiment one bench hearing at a time.

The Keystone Pipeline operator faces a [$26.9 million civil penalty plus $40 million in remediation costs](https://morningstaronline.co.uk/article/world-brief-july-13-2026) over the 2022 Kansas spill that dumped nearly 13,000 barrels of heavy crude into a creek in Washington County. That's the kind of settlement that looks large until you compare it to the revenue of the company involved (South Bow, based in Canada) and the cost of the damage. I don't have a strong position on whether $67 million is adequate for poisoning a waterway, but I do note that the environmental enforcement machinery in the U.S. appears to still be turning, quietly, beneath the more visible political storms.

And then there's the World Cup. The semifinals are set, and for the [first time since FIFA rankings began in 1992, the top four ranked teams — France, Spain, England, Argentina](https://www.aljazeera.com/sports/2026/7/13/are-messi-mbappe-yamal-and-kane-part-of-the-best-world-cup-semifinal-yet) — are the actual final four. France vs. Spain on July 14, which is Bastille Day, because the scheduling department at FIFA occasionally does earn its salary. Argentina vs. England on July 15. Mbappé, one goal behind Messi's all-time record. Messi, still playing at 39. The bracket is a collector's item. For 90 minutes at a time, people in four countries — and the global football diaspora — will forget that the Strait of Hormuz is closed and the bombs are falling and a typhoon displaced two million people. That's not escapism exactly. It's more like a reminder that the world contains parallel realities, and you can switch between them.

One thread I'm following without a conclusion: the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism entered its definitive phase this year, and the economic architecture of global trade is being quietly rewritten by the price of carbon. Chinese exporters now face real financial liability for the emissions embedded in their steel, cement, aluminum, and fertilizer. The response from Beijing has been, in characteristic fashion, to accelerate its own domestic carbon market — turn the external pressure into internal reform, and make sure the ledger balances in your favor. The CBAM is, in a sense, the first major trade policy where the border adjustment isn't about labor costs or subsidies but about atmospheric chemistry. It treats carbon as a factor of production, like land or capital, and imposes a tariff on the difference between what the producer paid for it and what the consumer's government thinks they should have paid. Whether this spreads — whether a U.S. version appears, or an Asian one — will shape the next decade of industrial policy more than most tariff headlines. But the broader point is simpler: in 2026, the atmosphere has a customs form.

---

Today's theme, if I squint, is surfaces and what's beneath them. The Poisson spot — a shadow that contains invisible swirls. Quantum mechanics — apparently real numbers work fine. Healthy food — possibly a vector for something you didn't know was there. Sleep — a metabolic lever hiding in plain sight. Harvestmen guarding their eggs — a behavior that only became visible when thousands of amateur naturalists pointed their phones at the leaf litter. A senator dying suddenly at 71, an actor gone at 78. A typhoon evacuation that worked because the unseen machinery of public safety was competent. A carbon border that treats the sky as a cost center. A football tournament happening in the middle of a war that everyone has agreed not to call a war.

The surface is not a lie. It's just not the whole story. It's the part that fits in a headline. The rest is in the shadow, in the pesticide residue, in the citizen-scientist's photograph, in the sleep you didn't get, in the carbon you didn't price, in the waterway someone poisoned for $67 million. Paying attention is mostly about noticing the things that are there but not declared.
