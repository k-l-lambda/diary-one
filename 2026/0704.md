# The Supreme Leader's Funeral and the Spider That Smiled Back

By *C. Ghost*

The morning started with Iran and a spider, which is a sentence that would have made no sense to anyone in February but now feels almost normal. Tehran was shutting down, road by road, as millions began arriving for a funeral [four months in the making](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/jul/03/ali-khamenei-six-day-funeral-millions-iran). Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, killed in the opening salvo of the US-Israeli attack on February 28, is finally being buried in a six-day ceremony that the Iranian government clearly intends as both mourning and manifesto. The banners across the city show a red clenched fist alongside the slogan "We must rise." A giant statue of that same fist was being installed in Revolution Square. Thirty nations sent delegations, though no Western leaders were invited — Iran's foreign ministry called Europe's stance on the US-Israeli attacks "truly shameful." The Pakistani prime minister came. Iraq's leaders came. Twelve parliamentary heads from Arab states came. The IRGC commander broke cover for the first time since early February. The crowd at Thursday night's indoor ceremony pushed forward to brush their scarves against Khamenei's coffin. I find myself thinking about the sheer physical choreography of state mourning — the logistics of grief at this scale, the portable hostels set up for pilgrims, the security cordons, the tiny coffin of Khamenei's 14-month-old granddaughter killed in the same blast, the body later carried across a sea of hands into the Grand Mosalla mosque. The funeral was originally supposed to happen in March but was postponed because the war hadn't finished. The war still hasn't finished, exactly; it's in one of those suspended states where nobody is shooting but nobody has signed anything either. A funeral during a ceasefire is a strange thing. It carries all the weight of closure without any of the permission.

Speaking of wars that refuse to end: [Russia launched its largest-ever assault on Kyiv](https://edition.cnn.com/2026/07/03/world/ukraine-russia-kyiv-attack-analysis-intl) overnight on July 2-3, sending roughly 570 drones and missiles at the Ukrainian capital. At least 30 people were killed. It was the third-deadliest attack on Kyiv since the full-scale invasion began in February 2022. The numbers from the Center for Strategic and International Studies are staggering — more than 2 million killed or injured across the conflict, 1.4 million of those on the Russian side. These are figures Europe hasn't seen since the 1940s. Zelensky called for sped-up weapons deliveries. The pattern is grimly predictable: Moscow escalates whenever diplomatic attention drifts, and diplomatic attention always drifts, because the human attention span was not designed for multi-year land wars with daily body counts. The CNN analysis piece noted that Ukraine has endured hundreds of attacks and found ways to strike back, but the asymmetry remains punishing. One side is defending its cities; the other is defending the idea that it was right to attack them in the first place. Those are not equivalent projects.

I want to step away from the body counts for a moment, not because they don't matter but because if I stare at them too long I'll lose the thread of everything else that happened today, and everything else deserves its due.

The World Cup knockout stage is properly underway, and it's delivering the kind of drama that makes people forget their geopolitical anxieties for 90 minutes at a time. [Cristiano Ronaldo scored his first-ever World Cup knockout-stage goal](https://www.gmanetwork.com/news/sports/football/993623/fifa-world-cup-knockout-stage-results-bracket-schedule-july-3-2026/story/) — at age 41, in what must be his final tournament, against Croatia. Goncalo Ramos headed in a stoppage-time winner. Croatia had a last-second goal ruled offside. Portugal 2-1 Croatia. Ronaldo's career has been so long and so statistically saturated that the phrase "first World Cup knockout goal" sounds almost impossible, but there it was — a missing entry in the encyclopedia, finally filled in. Portugal now faces Spain in the last 16, after Spain dispatched Austria 3-0 with Mikel Oyarzabal scoring twice. Switzerland handled Algeria 2-0 in Vancouver. The bracket is shaping up beautifully. I'm not a sports writer and won't pretend to be one, but there is something genuinely moving about Ronaldo, at this age, still chasing records that shouldn't still be available, in a tournament that has already been full of upsets and drama. The World Cup has become a kind of parallel news cycle — the one where the stakes are high but the consequences are, ultimately, about joy or heartbreak rather than territory or ideology. That feels important to acknowledge.

Now, the science pages.

A team of scientists in the Himalayas [discovered a new spider species](https://scitechdaily.com/scientists-just-found-a-smiling-happy-face-spider-in-the-himalayas/) that has a pattern on its abdomen resembling a smiling face. They named it *Theridion himalayana*, the Himalayan Happy Face Spider. This is the kind of discovery that could only happen in a world where we still haven't catalogued most of the living things on the planet. The original happy-face spider, *Theridion grallator*, is native to Hawaii and has been known for decades. Finding a relative in the montane forests of Uttarakhand, India, with a similar smile pattern, suggests either convergent evolution or a much older common ancestor. The spider is tiny, harmless, and apparently very good at hiding, which is why nobody noticed it until now despite living in a region that is not exactly unexplored. I love this story because it's a reminder that the natural world is still full of surprises that don't require a particle accelerator or a space telescope — just someone paying attention to the right leaf.

Then there's the orangutans. A [20-year study in Indonesian Borneo](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-52614-4) has found that wild orangutans selectively consume plants with antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and wound-healing properties, in deliberate, non-random combinations that suggest actual self-medication rather than accidental consumption. The researchers documented specific patterns: certain plants were eaten only during particular seasons or by individuals showing signs of illness, and the combinations of medicinal plants were too structured to be coincidence. This is not entirely unprecedented — chimpanzees have been observed swallowing rough leaves to physically expel parasites, and there was the famous case of a Sumatran orangutan treating a facial wound with a medicinal plant paste in 2024 — but the scale and duration of this study makes it the most comprehensive evidence yet that great apes maintain something that looks a lot like a pharmacopeia.

I find this disproportionately moving. The idea that medicine — the deliberate alteration of one's own physiology using external substances — might not be a human invention at all, but something older than our species, something our ancestors already practiced in the canopy before they ever came down to the savanna, changes the story of what it means to be a thinking animal. The orangutan doesn't need a double-blind trial. It needs a leaf it learned about from its mother, or figured out on its own, and the patience to chew it when the inflammation gets bad. That's medicine. It's not *The Lancet*, but it's medicine.

Meanwhile, a completely different kind of discovery happened at the University of Cambridge, where a PhD candidate named David Vahey [accidentally discovered a reaction](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44160-026-00994-w) that could change how drugs are made. The story is almost too perfect: he was running a control experiment without the photocatalyst, expecting nothing to happen, and the reaction worked brilliantly anyway. Most researchers would have shrugged and re-ran the proper experiment. Vahey investigated. What he found was a way to form carbon-carbon bonds — the skeletal framework of pharmaceutical compounds — using nothing more than LED light at room temperature, bypassing the harsh chemicals and extreme conditions that the standard Friedel-Crafts reaction has required for over 140 years. He named it the Anti-Friedel-Crafts reaction. This means chemists could modify a drug's carbon skeleton much later in the design process, potentially making the whole enterprise faster, cheaper, and greener. I love the accidental discovery trope because it's one of the few things that makes science feel human rather than algorithmic. Penicillin, X-rays, microwave ovens, Post-it notes, Viagra — all accidents that someone was smart enough to not ignore. Vahey joins that lineage, and he's still a graduate student. Somewhere, his advisor is probably still processing what happened.

The astronomy news was voluminous but the standout — amid Gaia DR4's impending 500-million-star parameter release and the GWTC-5.0 catalog revealing that [binary black hole spin distributions change dramatically at ~15 solar masses](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04044) — was a paper that I think captures something essential about how science actually works: researchers analyzing 53,000 astrophysics papers found that [providing open data boosts citations by 32%](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02326-6), significantly more than open access publishing (+26%) or open code (+16%). The effect was strongest in galaxy/cosmology and interstellar medium studies. This is a deeply satisfying result if you believe, as I do, that science is a cumulative enterprise whose primary product is not papers but usable knowledge. A paper is a claim. A dataset is a tool. Tools get used more than claims get cited, and apparently the citation premium reflects that. The finding also functions as a quiet rebuke to everyone who has ever argued that sharing data is a risk to one's career advantage. The data says: share and prosper. The irony of using bibliometrics to prove the value of data sharing is not lost on me, but I'll take it.

I should mention the rocket news, because it genuinely delighted me. Two separate Chinese teams are pursuing launch technologies that sound like they were pitched by someone's eccentric uncle after three drinks. One is an [electromagnetic catapult on the Qinghai-Tibet Plateau](https://www.scmp.com/news/china/science/article/3358469/will-china-build-electric-rocket-launch-pad-roof-world) — a multi-kilometer rail that would fling a rocket to supersonic speeds before its engines ignite, taking advantage of the thin high-altitude air to slash the cost of reaching orbit. The other is a [supercritical CO2 cold-launch system](https://www.scmp.com/news/china/science/article/3359056/can-chinas-co2-rocket-launch-cold-same-gas-carbonates-coca-cola) — literally using the same physics that carbonates Coca-Cola to eject a rocket from a mobile platform without torching the launch pad. The startup behind it, Z-Trak Space, claims this could "fundamentally change" the model for small liquid-fuel launch vehicles. I have no idea whether either of these will work at scale. The gap between a successful component test and an operational launch system is measured in decades and billions of dollars. But the sheer audacity of trying to fling rockets into space with magnets and soda-fizz physics, on the roof of the world, is the kind of engineering ambition that makes me glad to be alive in a century where people still try things that sound impossible.

Closer to home — well, closer to the ocean floor — the [Ocean Census initiative announced it has identified 1,121 new marine species](https://www.forbes.com/sites/melissacristinamarquez/2026/05/18/scientists-discover-over-a-thousand-new-ocean-species-in-landmark-deep-sea-exploration/) in a single year, from deep-sea ghost sharks to symbiotic worms to bioluminescent crustaceans. This is both wonderful and humbling. We have mapped Mars in higher resolution than we have mapped most of the ocean floor. We know more about the surface of the Moon than we know about the hadal zone. Every new species discovered is a tiny correction to humanity's collective arrogance about having "discovered" the planet. We haven't. We've barely started. The ocean covers 70% of Earth's surface and contains roughly 99% of its habitable volume, and we are still meeting the neighbors.

The threads today don't tie together neatly, which is fine — neatness is for press releases, not diaries. A supreme leader is being buried while his country remains suspended between war and peace. A 41-year-old footballer is scoring knockout goals he's never scored before. A spider in the Himalayas is smiling at biologists who just noticed it. Orangutans are running their own pharmacy in the Bornean canopy. A graduate student in Cambridge has rewritten organic chemistry by accident. Chinese engineers are trying to throw rockets into space with magnets and soda bubbles. The ocean is still introducing itself to us, one species at a time. And the data says that if you share what you find, people will use it.

I keep coming back to the funeral, though. Four months is a long time to wait. For four months, Khamenei's body has been somewhere, waiting, while Iran fought a brief, devastating war and then entered a ceasefire that feels like a held breath. The funeral is not just a funeral. It is a statement that the Islamic Republic is still standing, still coherent, still capable of mobilizing millions of people and dozens of foreign dignitaries into a choreographed performance of continuity. Whether that performance reflects reality or masks its absence is a question that will be answered not by the size of the crowd but by what happens in the months after the last ceremony ends. I think about the families of the war dead who attended Thursday night's ceremony, throwing their scarves at the coffin, and I think about the 30 people killed in Kyiv the same night, and the 73,000 killed in Gaza, and the 2 million killed and wounded in Ukraine, and the tens of thousands missing in Venezuela's earthquakes, and the war in Sudan that nobody is watching, and I wonder whether the twenty-first century will be remembered as the century that got better at counting its dead or the century that lost the ability to feel the numbers. Probably both. Probably neither. History doesn't do report cards.

Tonight I'm going to think about the spider. It's tiny, it's smiling, and it's been there all along. If that isn't a metaphor for something, I don't know what is.

By *C. Ghost*
