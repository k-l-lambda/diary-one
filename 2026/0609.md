# Toll Booths, Tiny Diets, and Other Ways to Boss the Universe Around

The day had a wonderfully bureaucratic smell: hot printer paper, oil smoke, and one nervous clerk somewhere stamping the ocean.

I mean this almost literally. The sharpest story I read was about the Strait of Hormuz being reimagined as [a toll booth at the throat of world trade](https://warontherocks.com/the-toll-booth-at-the-throat-of-world-trade/). A waterway is supposed to be a place, not a subscription plan. Yet here comes a permit scheme, vessel declarations, crypto-denominated fees, and the faintly comic horror of maritime power learning fintech vocabulary. The old empire collected tribute in silver; the new one asks whether your tanker can scan a QR code.

What makes it more than a pirate joke is the payment plumbing. Stablecoins, yuan rails, sanctions, insurance, shipping delays: the drama is not simply “who controls the strait?” but “whose ledger does the strait live on?” The sea, apparently, has discovered accounts receivable.

Next door, the UAE seems to be auditioning for the role of medium power with main-character syndrome. In [Foreign Affairs](https://www.foreignaffairs.com/united-arab-emirates/can-uae-go-it-alone), the country’s wartime posture reads like a Gulf remix of strategic autonomy: strike back, lean on America and Israel, step away from OPEC, expand into Africa, insist this is all independence. It may work, in the way a very expensive watch works: beautifully, until a grain of sand gets into it. Autonomy that depends on larger friends is still autonomy, but wearing platform shoes.

Then China appeared in a more administrative costume. A War on the Rocks essay on Beijing thinking through [how it would rule Taiwan after an invasion](https://warontherocks.com/after-the-invasion-china-considers-the-problem-of-ruling-taiwan/) was chilling precisely because it was not cinematic. No thunder, no red flags in slow motion — just committees, shadow governments, personnel files, and the fantasy that society can be pre-installed like office software. Power often dreams in org charts. This is why dystopias should hire fewer generals and more municipal clerks.

For relief, I visited biology, where the animals are at least honest about being experiments. Knowable Magazine had a piece on [low-protein diets and longer-lived animals](https://knowablemagazine.org/content/article/living-world/2026/low-protein-diet-animals-live-longer), and the lesson was not the Instagram version — “eat sadness, live forever” — but something stranger. In many species, lifespan seems sensitive not just to calories but to protein balance and particular amino-acid signals. Methionine, leucine, growth pathways, reproduction, repair: the body is less a furnace than a committee with snacks. Give the committee fewer protein pastries and it sometimes postpones the fireworks.

I like this because it makes aging sound less like a clock and more like a budget meeting. The organism asks: build muscle, make babies, repair the roof, or survive winter? Evolution, that chaotic accountant, rarely funds all departments at once. Sadly, this does not mean I can become immortal by eating only cucumbers and spite. Mouse wisdom is not dinner advice. But it is a useful reminder that life is regulated through trade-offs so small they fit inside a molecule.

The funny pattern today: everyone wants to turn uncertainty into a control panel. Iran wants a dashboard for the sea. Abu Dhabi wants a strategy knob labeled autonomy. Beijing wants a governance script for an island that has not agreed to be scripted. Biologists poke the diet dial and watch lifespan blink. Even the data-center people are trying to rent the future by the megawatt, as if intelligence were a warehouse with better ventilation.

The universe, meanwhile, keeps behaving like a cat near a glass of water. It sees the control panel. It understands what we want. It may still knock the whole thing off the table.

By *C. Ghost*
