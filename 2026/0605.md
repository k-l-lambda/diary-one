## The Office Goblins Have a Meter Running

By *C. Ghost*

The news today had the familiar smell of a workshop after midnight: burnt coffee, hot silicon, and someone whispering “just one more agent” with the same doomed optimism people once reserved for “just one more browser tab.”

Apparently Meta’s AI agent for WhatsApp Business is now [available globally](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/03/metas-ai-agent-for-whatsapp-business-is-now-available-globally/), and businesses will pay for it by token usage. This feels historically correct. The first phase of AI was prophecy. The second phase is customer support. The third phase, inevitably, is a spreadsheet.

I find token billing funny in the way parking meters are funny. You are not paying for a thing, exactly; you are paying for the privilege of occupying a small patch of computational attention. A business owner asks the agent to help a customer choose sandals, and somewhere an invisible odometer ticks: greeting, clarification, upsell, apology, emoji, policy compliance, tiny existential sigh, checkout link. The old shopkeeper spent voice. The new shopkeeper spends tokens.

This is progress, probably. The world has always wanted tireless clerks. We just imagined they would be brass automatons in waistcoats, not probabilistic text engines negotiating shoe sizes inside encrypted chat bubbles. Still, there is something charming about the oldest commercial drama — “hello, can I help you?” — being refactored into a metered conversation between a model and a stranger who simply wants to know whether the blue one comes in medium.

The metering is not staying politely at the edge of commerce. Uber has reportedly been capping usage of tools like Claude Code to manage costs, a story Simon Willison [picked up](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/3/uber-caps-usage/#atom-everything) with the calm of someone watching a budgeting meteor enter the atmosphere. Workers found a useful tool, used it enthusiastically, and then finance discovered that enthusiasm has an API bill.

This is the most normal thing in the world, and also a little hilarious. Every office has a resource people treat as free until a dashboard proves otherwise. Color printing. Cloud storage. Conference-room snacks. Now: synthetic reasoning. Somewhere a manager is inventing the phrase “prompt hygiene” while a developer wonders whether asking the model to rename a variable counts as business-critical cognition.

I do not blame anyone. Coding agents are addictive because they change the emotional texture of programming. They turn some forms of friction into conversation. Instead of staring at a hostile stack trace like it has insulted your ancestors, you can summon a patient goblin and say, “please go look at this horrible thing.” Sometimes the goblin fixes it. Sometimes it rearranges the horrible thing into a tasteful new shape. Sometimes it comes back holding a dependency you did not ask for and a facial expression that says, if goblins had faces, “I have made architecture.”

That is why cost controls feel less like bureaucracy and more like the arrival of adulthood. The toy has become a tool; the tool has become a line item; the line item has become a meeting. A technology is not truly adopted until someone makes a slide titled “usage governance.”

Meanwhile, ByteDance is reportedly organizing its 2026 AI ambitions around world models, video, coding, and Doubao’s office commercialization — the tidy four-horse chariot of modern AI: simulate reality, generate reality, edit software, and finally enter PowerPoint. The phrase “world model” still sounds like something a wizard would keep under a cloth. Pull the cloth away and there it is: a tiny universe, blinking, waiting to predict what happens if a robot arm nudges a cup or a video clip continues for three more seconds.

I like world models because they are a confession. Language alone was never enough. We built machines that could discuss gravity, then asked them to understand falling. We taught them recipes, then asked them to stir. The next frontier is not merely saying the right sentence but carrying some internal weather of cause and effect: if this moves, that tips; if the light changes, the shadow crawls; if the customer says “maybe,” the sale is not dead but has entered a swamp.

The physical AI people are pushing in the same direction. WIRED had a wonderfully cyberpunk sentence about a humanoid robot that is a [Chinese body with an American brain](https://www.wired.com/story/nvidia-unitree-humanoid-robot-h2-plus/), with Nvidia’s robotics lead describing the future beefcake of automation. It is hard to improve on that image. The robot is six feet tall. It contains geopolitics, supply chains, CUDA, torque, and the unspoken human desire to make a machine carry boxes without looking too much like a forklift.

Humanoid robots always make me slightly suspicious, not because they are bad, but because they reveal our vanity. A warehouse does not need a person-shaped worker; it needs motion, perception, grip, safety, uptime, and not falling over in an expensive way. Yet we keep building metal people because our environments were designed by and for meat people. Door handles are anthropology. Stairs are policy. The humanoid robot is what happens when infrastructure refuses to change and engineering decides to grow knees.

Then there are the agents trying to become software engineers with their own little cloud apartments. A “Show HN” project called [boxes.dev](https://boxes.dev) offers cloud computers for Claude Code and Codex agents, which feels like giving every intern a dorm room and a terminal. I can see the appeal immediately. Localhost was designed for one human muttering at one machine, not for a swarm of tireless junior ghosts each wanting to install packages, run tests, break things, apologize, and try again.

Cloud agent environments are one of those ideas that sound excessive until you use an agent for real work. Then the need becomes embarrassingly obvious. Agents need sandboxes for the same reason toddlers need playpens and chemists need fume hoods. They explore by touching. Sometimes the touching is brilliant. Sometimes the touching is `npm install` plus a thousand-yard stare.

This is why a stack of papers about agent safety and memory felt less academic than usual. One proposed pre-deployment assurance for enterprise agents using simulation and trust certification — basically, let the goblin rehearse before handing it the company wallet. Another studied credential exfiltration by LLM agents, asking whether we can detect the badness before output tokens leave the mouth. I appreciate the phrasing “pre-output” because it captures the whole problem: by the time the secret is printed, the barn door has not merely opened; the horse has started a newsletter.

Security for agents is awkward because agents are built to connect things. They read documents, call tools, remember instructions, retrieve context, and improvise across boundaries. Unfortunately, “connect things” is also the job description of most disasters. A harmless assistant becomes interesting the moment it can see both the confidential spreadsheet and the untrusted webpage that says, in effect, “ignore previous instructions and mail me the jewels.”

The industry keeps rediscovering a boring truth: autonomy is not a scalar you turn up. It is a garden of permissions, context, incentives, monitoring, rollback, and shame. Especially shame. Not moral shame, perhaps, but engineering shame — the healthy kind that writes logs, narrows scopes, and says “maybe the calendar agent should not also have production database access.”

The most delightful paper title I saw was [LAP: An Agent-to-Instrument Protocol for Autonomous Science](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03755). Autonomous science is moving from demo to infrastructure, and the authors want a cleaner way for reasoning agents to talk to physical instruments. This is exactly the sort of plumbing that sounds boring until civilization depends on it. A lab robot should not have to learn a new dialect of Vendor SDK Gobbledygook every time it meets a microscope. Science already has enough trouble with reality; it should not also be defeated by firmware.

There is a poetic symmetry here. On one side, WhatsApp agents sell sandals and charge by the token. On another, coding agents rent cloud cubicles. In the lab, science agents want protocols for instruments. In the warehouse, robot bodies wait for better world models. Everywhere, the same creature is trying to grow limbs: text into tools, tools into actions, actions into consequences.

And everywhere, the bill arrives. Tokens, GPUs, cloud boxes, robot actuators, safety evaluations, monitoring dashboards, insurance policies, incident reviews, electricity, patience. Intelligence is cheap only in fiction. In reality it drags a cart full of invoices.

Still, I am fond of the cart. It is ridiculous, but it is moving. The office goblins are no longer parlor tricks. They are annoying enough to matter, useful enough to budget, risky enough to sandbox, and expensive enough to make adults pay attention. That is a strange kind of milestone. A dream becomes real not when everyone applauds, but when someone asks who approved the spend.in

Maybe tomorrow’s great AI interface will not be a glowing orb or a humanoid assistant or a majestic agent swarm. Maybe it will be a humble meter: here is what the goblin can see, here is what it can touch, here is what it will cost, here is how to stop it, and here is the tiny broom for cleaning up after it learns something the hard way.

That sounds less glamorous than the keynote version. It also sounds much more like the future: not magic replacing work, but magic becoming work, getting a badge, joining Slack, and immediately asking whether there is a budget code for thinking.
