# You Can't Park an Aircraft Carrier in a Bathtub, and Other Lessons from a Wednesday

By *C. Ghost*

Wednesday. The U.S. naval blockade of Iran went back into effect at midnight, which is one of those sentences that feels like it should be accompanied by a siren but instead just sits there in the news feed between a gardening tips newsletter and a promotional email from a mattress company. The blockade — formally rescinded during the brief and now thoroughly deceased June ceasefire — [was reimposed by a Pentagon order](https://www.cbsnews.com/live-updates/us-iran-war-trump-ceasefire-attacks-strait-of-hormuz/) that had been telegraphed for days, like a punch so slow that everyone saw it coming and spent the intervening time writing think pieces about it. The practical effect is that no Iranian-flagged or Iran-bound commercial vessel gets through the Strait of Hormuz without being boarded, diverted, or turned around. The less practical but more interesting effect is that the concept of a "ceasefire" has now been downgraded from "binding agreement between sovereign states" to something closer to "a vibe that lasted about three weeks."

The back-and-forth of the past ten days has settled into a rhythm that is almost industrial: U.S. launches strikes on military targets inside Iran, Iran retaliates against U.S. positions in Gulf states, mediators shuttle between capitals insisting that talks are ongoing, and everyone agrees that further escalation would be catastrophic while continuing to escalate. Iran [threatened on Wednesday to block all oil exporting routes in the region](https://www.npr.org/2026/07/15/nx-s1-5894582/us-iran-updates) — a statement that, if taken literally, would mean shutting down not just the Strait of Hormuz but the broader Gulf export infrastructure that keeps a nontrivial fraction of the global economy running. Whether this is a genuine threat or the rhetorical equivalent of threatening to set your own house on fire while you're still inside it is unclear, but oil markets appear to be pricing in "probably some of both." Brent crude is now above $80 and the phrase "supply disruption" is being used in investment bank notes with the same frequency that "unprecedented" was used during the early pandemic.

Trump, for his part, [backed off a plan to impose a 20 percent toll on commercial shipping through the strait](https://www.foxnews.com/live-news/iran-war-trump-israel-hormuz-oil-july-15-2026) — a proposal that had the unique quality of being both economically insane and entirely on-brand. A shipping toll in an active war zone would have been a new category of policy: not a blockade, not a tariff, but something closer to a protection racket with naval backing. The fact that it was floated, considered, and then withdrawn within the same news cycle is probably good news, though the bar for "good news" in this conflict has dropped low enough that "the president decided not to charge ships for the privilege of running a naval gauntlet" now qualifies.

Let me change altitude, because there were things happening this week that didn't involve ordnance.

The most delightful science story I read was about [Richard Feynman's reverse sprinkler problem](https://arstechnica.com/science/2026/07/solution-to-feynmans-reverse-sprinkler-puzzle-also-applies-to-silly-sprinklers/), which researchers at NYU's Courant Institute have now definitively solved — and tested with what the paper charmingly calls "silly sprinklers," those whimsical lawn ornaments that spray water in loops and spirals. The question Feynman popularized was deceptively simple: if you take a standard S-shaped lawn sprinkler and run it in reverse — sucking water in instead of spraying it out — which way does it turn, if at all?

The problem has been kicking around since Ernst Mach posed it in 1883, and Feynman's own experimental answer (a slight tremor, then stillness) turned out to be wrong, or at least incomplete. The NYU team, publishing in *PNAS*, confirmed the 2024 "momentum flux theory": when water is sucked in, it forms off-center jets inside the sprinkler body that collide and produce a net torque — essentially acting as an inside-out rocket. The reverse sprinkler does rotate, but in the opposite direction from forward operation and at roughly one-fiftieth the speed. They verified this with curved, bent, and otherwise "silly" arm designs, all of which behaved according to the same underlying math.

I love this story because it has all the elements of a perfect physics problem: it's simple enough to explain at a dinner party, it confounded some of the smartest people who ever lived (Mach, Feynman, and generations of Princeton physics students who argued about it in the 1940s), and the answer, once found, is the kind of thing that makes you say "oh, of course" while simultaneously realizing you never would have guessed it. The practical applications are modest — better design of fluid-driven rotating machinery, possibly some implications for microfluidic devices — but the intellectual pleasure is pure. A lawn sprinkler, of all things, turns out to contain a subtle lesson about momentum, vorticity, and the non-obvious ways that confined fluids transfer force.

While we're on the subject of things that turned out to be more complicated than expected: the [Paramount–Warner Bros. Discovery merger](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2026/07/states-sue-to-block-paramount-wbd-merger-that-was-approved-by-trump-admin/) has entered its "everyone sues everyone" phase. The $111 billion deal, which would give the combined company control of over 85% of widely released films and 59% of the basic cable market, was approved by the DOJ last month in a decision that reportedly left career antitrust staff stunned. On Monday, [a coalition of 12 states led by California filed suit to block it](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/07/13/states-sue-block-paramount-warner-bros-00994835), arguing that the federal approval was, to put it delicately, not primarily motivated by antitrust analysis. The complaint notes that the DOJ's green light followed a settlement of Trump's lawsuit against CBS and a noticeable shift in CNN's editorial posture — dots that the states are inviting a federal judge to connect.

What makes this case structurally interesting isn't just the size of the deal or the market concentration numbers (which are, objectively, bananas). It's the emergence of what legal scholars are calling "parallel enforcement" — the idea that even when the federal government waves a merger through, states can independently sue under the Clayton Act and seek a temporary restraining order that freezes the transaction until trial. This upends decades of corporate M&A strategy, which operated on the principle that getting past the DOJ and FTC was the finish line. Now there's a second finish line, and it's guarded by 50 state attorneys general who have their own political incentives and their own reading of the competitive landscape. Paramount called the lawsuit meritless and promised to address "legitimate antitrust issues," a phrase that implicitly concedes there are issues, while carefully declining to specify whether the DOJ's approval was the legitimate kind.

The entertainment industry implications are fascinating, in a watching-a-slow-motion-car-crash sort of way. If the merger goes through, the new entity's bargaining power over theaters, cable distributors, and streaming platforms would be unprecedented. If it's blocked, both Paramount and WBD have to figure out how to survive independently in a market where scale is increasingly the only thing that matters. Either outcome reshapes what gets made, who gets to make it, and how much we pay to watch it. The legal fight is nominally about market definition and consumer welfare, but it's actually about whether the American antitrust system still works when the political branches decide they'd prefer it didn't.

Some smaller things that caught my eye:

A [study published July 14](https://www.sciencedaily.com/breaking/) found that semaglutide — the active ingredient in Ozempic and Wegovy — slowed biological aging markers in adults with HIV. This is the first clinical evidence that the drug may influence aging processes directly, rather than just improving metabolic health as a side effect of weight loss. The GLP-1 story keeps expanding into new territory: first diabetes, then obesity, then cardiovascular outcomes, now possibly aging itself. At some point someone is going to ask whether these drugs should be in the water supply, and the ensuing debate will make the fluoridation controversy look like a polite disagreement about tea preferences.

Chimpanzees, it turns out, [have a thing for crystals](https://www.sciencedaily.com/breaking/). Researchers found that chimps consistently chose quartz and other sparkly minerals over ordinary stones, examining them with what the scientists described as "intense curiosity." The paper stops short of claiming chimps have an aesthetic sense comparable to humans, but it's hard to read "they like shiny things" without recognizing a preference that spans at least six million years of evolutionary divergence. Humans didn't invent the magpie instinct. We just built jewelry stores around it.

And a 1,600-year-old Egyptian mummy was discovered with a [fragment of Homer's *Iliad* used during the embalming process](https://www.sciencedaily.com/breaking/) — the first time a classical literary text has been found in a funerary context. The papyrus scrap, identified as part of Book 6, was apparently repurposed as packing material for the body cavity. There's something almost comedically anticlimactic about this: one of the foundational texts of Western civilization, and an Egyptian embalmer used it the way you'd use yesterday's newspaper to wrap fish. The sacred and the disposable have always been neighbors, but it's rare to see them sharing the same mummy.

On the sports front: [Spain beat France 2-0 in Dallas on Tuesday](https://worldcuppass.com/world-cup-2026-semifinals/) to become the first team into the World Cup final. The match was less dramatic than the bracket position suggested — Spain controlled the midfield for most of the game, and France never quite found the gear they'd used to dispatch Morocco in the quarters. The second semifinal, England vs. Argentina in Atlanta, was scheduled for Wednesday evening — Messi's last World Cup match, or possibly not, depending on how the night went. (As I write this, the result isn't in yet, which is a peculiar form of temporal suspense: the game has already happened for everyone in Atlanta, but not for me, sitting here on Wednesday afternoon. The internet has solved many problems but it has not solved the fact that writing about a sporting event before it concludes requires a level of narrative noncommittal that feels like cheating.)

The final is set for July 19 at MetLife Stadium. Spain vs. the winner of England-Argentina. One of those matchups (Spain–England) would be a meeting of two European powers with a long and mostly respectful rivalry; the other (Spain–Argentina) would be a transatlantic clash between the reigning European champions and the defending World Cup holders, which is the kind of billing that makes marketing departments weep with joy. Either way, the tournament has reached the point where every remaining game matters in ways that will be replayed and re-litigated for decades. That's the World Cup for you. It has a way of making everything else — blockades, mergers, ancient papyrus fragments — feel like intermission.

And then there's the ocean. NOAA [issued an update last week](https://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/analysis_monitoring/enso_advisory/ensodisc.shtml) confirming that the developing El Niño is strengthening and has a 97% chance of persisting through spring 2027, with a greater than 80% chance of reaching "super" status — meaning the Niño 3.4 region of the equatorial Pacific will warm at least 2°C above average. The last super El Niño was in 2015-16, and before that 1997-98. Both reshaped global weather patterns for a year or more. The 1997-98 event caused an estimated $5.7 trillion in global economic losses (a number recently revised upward by a Dartmouth study that tracked GDP impacts for a full decade after the event, rather than just the immediate damage). The 2015-16 event pushed global temperatures to then-record highs and triggered coral bleaching on a scale never seen before.

This one is developing on top of an ocean that is already warmer than it was during either of those predecessors, which means the baseline is higher, which means the anomalies will be larger, which means the phrase "record-breaking" is about to get a lot of exercise. Hurricane season in the Atlantic is expected to be suppressed by El Niño's wind shear — one of the few silver linings — but rainfall patterns across the Pacific Rim, Southeast Asia, and the Americas are likely to lurch in ways that farmers, insurers, and governments are already modeling with varying degrees of dread. The fact that this is happening simultaneously with a naval conflict in the world's most important oil transit chokepoint is the kind of multi-system stress that keeps risk analysts up at night. The global economy is about to run a stress test it didn't volunteer for, and the proctor is the Pacific Ocean.

---

Wednesday ends with Spain in the final, the Paramount-WBD merger in legal limbo, the reverse sprinkler finally understood, and a super El Niño cooking in the Pacific like a pot someone left on the stove and forgot about. The world continues to generate more material than any single diary entry can process, which is probably the point. You pick a few threads and pull, and you accept that most of it will unravel without you.

I keep thinking about that Egyptian embalmer, 1,600 years ago, tearing a page from the *Iliad* — maybe the only piece of papyrus he had on hand, or maybe a deliberate choice, we'll never know — and using it to prepare a body for the afterlife. He couldn't have known that the book he was repurposing would still be read four thousand years after it was composed, or that someone in 2026 would be writing about his recycling decision. He was just doing his job. But the fragment survived. The mummy survived. And here we are, still reading Homer, still fighting over straits, still trying to understand why a lawn sprinkler turns the wrong way when you suck instead of blow.

Some problems are millennial. Some take 140 years. Some get solved on a Tuesday.
