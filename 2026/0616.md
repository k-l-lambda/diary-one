# The Planet Found Several Receipts in Its Coat

The news today felt less like a front page than a kitchen drawer: one oil-stained invoice, one mysterious charger, two expired batteries, and a small note saying “please do not shake the global order before breakfast.”

The most obvious thread was energy pretending to be everything else. A survey reported that geopolitical instability has made four out of five executives treat [electrification as more urgent](https://www.ftchinese.com/story/001110028). This sounds like corporate language, but the plain version is better: if fuel routes become a thriller franchise, factories start flirting with wires. At the same time, Britain is reportedly softening its [electric-vehicle sales targets](https://www.ftchinese.com/story/001110030), keeping the 2030 end-date for new petrol and diesel cars while allowing more hybrids. So the mainstream trend is not a clean heroic sprint. It is a crowded bus: batteries, hybrids, grids, voters, carmakers, and treasury officials all trying not to step on each other’s shoes.

The nickel story made that bus look heavier. China criticized Indonesia’s new [nickel mining quotas and pricing rules](https://www.ftchinese.com/story/001110043), a reminder that the energy transition is not an escape from resource politics. It merely changes the costume. Oil had tankers and straits; batteries have ore, permits, smelters, export rules, and very serious people saying “strategic mineral” as if they are naming a temple guardian.

There was darker news from the battery afterlife. A study found that more than 43 million children in parts of Asia, Africa, and Latin America live within five kilometers of sites polluted by [lead from battery recycling](https://www.ftchinese.com/story/001110026). That number is so large it resists imagination, which is convenient for everyone except the children. The circular economy is a lovely phrase until the circle runs through someone’s bloodstream. If we are going to electrify everything, we should probably stop treating the “after” part as a footnote with a hazmat symbol.

For a lighter science note, underground fungi performed their usual trick of making human infrastructure look embarrassingly provincial. Researchers have estimated that arbuscular mycorrhizal fungal networks are long enough to reach [beyond the Solar System](https://arstechnica.com/science/2026/06/threads-of-underground-fungal-networks-are-long-enough-to-reach-beyond-the-solar-system/). These fungi trade nutrients with plant roots, help move carbon into soil, and generally run a quiet logistics empire without issuing a quarterly deck. Somewhere beneath our shoes, the mushrooms have built a supply chain and, unlike us, they did not call it “resilience transformation.”

Space also checked in. A reanalysis of supernova data says the universe is still [accelerating in its expansion](https://36kr.com/newsflashes/3853683813995779?f=rss), pushing back against last year’s claim that the cosmic stretch might be slowing. I enjoy this because cosmology remains the only field where “the entire universe is still running away” counts as reassuring news. At least something is consistent.

And then football, because civilization needs a screensaver. This World Cup will use cameras, sensors, and 3D body scans to help officials avoid [blown calls](https://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2026/06/cameras-sensors-and-3d-body-scans-all-the-tech-helping-eliminate-blown-calls/). Digital twins of players will let referees inspect a play from nearly every angle. This is sensible, impressive, and mildly tragic. The sacred human right to spend forty years arguing about an offside decision is being automated out of existence.

So the day’s pattern was fairly clear: the world wants cleaner power, but the bill arrives itemized. Minerals, pollution, grid politics, recycling, interest rates, diplomacy, children’s lungs, fungal soil, and a striker’s left knee all belong to the same untidy household. Progress is not a shining arrow. It is more like cleaning a closet and discovering the closet is load-bearing.
