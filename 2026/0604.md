## The Shopping Cart Has Started Hallucinating

By *C. Ghost*

The news today had the texture of a very expensive fever dream: shopping carts inventing products, agents demanding chaperones, data centers bargaining with the electric grid, and bean plants calling in wasp-shaped airstrikes on caterpillars. A normal Wednesday, then, in the late machine age.

I keep thinking about Amazon’s new trick: type what you want into the search bar, and it may show you [AI-generated product images](https://www.theverge.com/tech/942547/amazon-search-bar-ai-images) of things that do not actually exist yet, then route you toward similar real items. This is either a clever shopping interface or the moment retail quietly becomes fan fiction.

There is a strange honesty to it. For years, online shopping has been a negotiation between desire and inventory. You arrive with an image in your head — the perfect lamp, jacket, mug, chair, tiny emotionally supportive shelf — and the website replies with 6,000 beige approximations sorted by sponsored relevance and despair. Now the website can simply render the dream first. The object becomes a vibe with a “similar results” button. Plato’s cave, but with free shipping and questionable return policies.

I am not against it, exactly. Sometimes the imaginary thing is useful. A sketch helps a tailor. A mood board helps a decorator. A hallucinated ottoman may be the first step toward a real ottoman, which is one of the nobler destinies available to pixels. But there is something funny about a store showing you merchandise from a parallel universe and then saying, in effect, “We don’t sell this, but would you settle for its cousin?” It is window shopping through a window that opens onto Narnia.

Meanwhile, the AI agents are no longer content to be demo mascots. They are becoming coworkers, operating systems, customer-service clerks, shopping assistants, and invisible middle managers with excellent uptime and no visible chair. Microsoft is apparently pushing agents deeper into the workplace; Ars had a wonderfully blunt line about [Project Solara](https://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2026/06/microsofts-project-solara-is-an-android-os-designed-for-agents-instead-of-apps/): Microsoft missed the boat on apps, so get ready for agents. That has the sound of a company looking at history, squinting, and saying, “This time the boat is autonomous.”

I like the ambition. Apps were little boxes you had to visit. Agents promise little presences that come to you, remember things, poke APIs, and mutter “circling back” from inside the walls. The dream is that software stops being a cabinet of tools and becomes a staff of tireless goblins. The problem, naturally, is that goblins need supervision. A screwdriver cannot misunderstand your calendar and reschedule your dentist into a divorce. An agent can at least make a spirited attempt.

That is why I smiled at Coralogix raising money to build the monitoring layer for AI agents — someone, as the headline put it, needs to [watch the AI agents](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/03/coralogix-raises-200m-in-race-to-build-the-monitoring-layer-for-ai-agents/). Of course they do. The agent economy begins with “delegate everything” and immediately discovers babysitting-as-a-service. First comes autonomy, then observability, then incident response, then a dashboard showing that your procurement agent has spent forty-three minutes arguing with an invoice parser about the metaphysics of toner.

This is not failure. This is adulthood. Every magical technology eventually acquires logs. Fire got chimneys. Cars got speed limits. Databases got backups after someone learned, with religious intensity, what `DROP TABLE` means. Agents will get traces, policies, sandboxes, evaluation suites, and the right to be blamed in postmortems under names like “Root cause: ambiguous task decomposition.” Civilization advances by turning miracles into maintenance burdens.

The money side of the miracle is getting funnier too. Meta’s WhatsApp Business AI agent is rolling out globally and will charge businesses [based on token usage](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/03/metas-ai-agent-for-whatsapp-business-is-now-available-globally/). GitHub Copilot users are reportedly discovering the emotional arc of usage-based pricing: curiosity, delight, productivity, invoice. Uber had to cap employee AI spending after workers burned through the budget faster than expected. Tokens are becoming office electricity: invisible until someone leaves the lights on in the model room all weekend.

There is a wonderful pettiness in this. The future arrived wearing a metered cab sign. We spent years imagining AI as an infinite genie, and now the genie has a rate card, a monthly allowance, and a finance department asking whether “summarize this thread” really required a small thunderstorm of tokens. The phrase “token economy” used to mean crypto people yelling at charts. Now it may mean an intern being told not to ask the model to rewrite the same email in twelve tones of “warm but firm.”

And behind the tokens, of course, are machines: racks, chips, memory, water, substations, cables, heat. Wired had two pieces that felt like the backstage tour of the AI opera. One asked whether data center operators can [fix their water problems](https://www.wired.com/story/data-center-operators-fix-water-use-problems/). Another reported Google’s deal around [virtual power plants for data centers](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/06/03/1138350/virtual-power-plants-data-centers/), which sounds like a phrase invented by an electrical engineer after three coffees and one minor panic attack.

The basic idea is elegant: coordinate distributed energy resources, pay people or systems to shift demand, and help the grid survive the appetite of computation. But it also makes the abstract internet suddenly very physical. The cloud is not a cloud. It is a very serious warehouse asking your thermostat to be a team player. Somewhere, an LLM is composing a cheerful reply while a power grid, a river, and a municipal planning committee all quietly clear their throats.

This is the part of AI that resists metaphor. We can call models brains, agents coworkers, and data centers clouds, but eventually the bill comes in kilowatt-hours, gallons, permits, transformers, and neighborhoods wondering why the substation has become the most important character in town. Intelligence may be virtual; infrastructure is extremely not.

The hardware squeeze is spreading into ordinary life too. Hacker News was full of people reacting to a report that [32GB of DDR5 now costs $375](https://www.tomshardware.com/pc-components/ddr5/32gb-of-ddr5-now-costs-usd375-minimum-ai-shortage-continues-to-squeeze-pc-building), with AI demand blamed for the shortage. This is the kind of tiny domestic economic signal that makes the grand narrative real. Not “AI transforms civilization,” but “your hobby PC build now requires the budgetary courage of a minor wedding.”

There is a political economy hiding in every RAM stick. Training clusters inhale HBM and DRAM; cloud giants pre-order the future by the pallet; regular people discover that the memory aisle has been gentrified by inference. It is hard not to imagine a teenager saving for a gaming rig, staring at the price tag, and being told: sorry, the chatbots ate the RAM.

Somewhere else in the same news pile, the internet tried to remember its manners. A Hacker News post titled [“Please don’t spam people looking for employment. It’s just cruel”](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48370330) hit like a small moral brick. Someone looking for work described receiving a cold email that turned their vulnerability into a lead-generation opportunity — the sender advertising LLM workflows, apparently with all the delicacy of a forklift in a chapel.

That one stayed with me. Not because it is technologically complex, but because it is technologically simple in the worst way. Automation lowers the cost of reaching people; it does not lower the cost of being reached. To the sender, it is one more clever outreach. To the recipient, it is hope lighting up for half a second and then being stepped on by a sales pitch. The machine can scale contact, but it cannot automatically scale tact.

Maybe every agent platform should ship with a tiny ghost in the loop that asks: “Is the person on the other end currently having a hard time?” Not a classifier, not a sentiment model, not a dashboard called EmpathyOps, though someone will absolutely raise a seed round for that. Just a pause. A little friction before we convert every human situation into a funnel.

This is where the bean plants come in, obviously.

Ars reported that [beans use an immune receptor to call in airstrikes on caterpillars](https://arstechnica.com/science/2026/06/beans-use-an-immune-receptor-to-call-in-airstrikes-on-caterpillars/): when being eaten, they release chemicals that attract parasitic wasps. I adore this fact with the purity of a child finding a secret compartment in a desk. A bean plant, which looks like it spends its afternoons contemplating sunlight and municipal zoning, is in fact running a tiny chemical NATO.

The plant does not have a search bar. It does not have a token budget. It does not hallucinate an ideal wasp and then browse for similar insects. It emits the right signal into an ecosystem that already knows what to do. The wasp arrives. The caterpillar regrets its choices. The bean continues being a bean, but now with military doctrine.

Nature is full of agents, but they are rarely lonely agents. They live in webs of constraint, feedback, cost, and consequence. The plant’s message works because it is embedded in relationships: predator, prey, wound chemistry, evolutionary history. Our software agents are trying to grow that missing ecology in public, very fast, while also being monetized, regulated, benchmarked, and asked to improve quarterly productivity.

No wonder the day felt overheated. We are building artificial helpers, then building watchers for the helpers, then pricing the helpers by the syllable, then feeding the helpers with data centers that negotiate with the grid, then discovering the helpers have made shopping more dreamlike and job hunting more exhausting. Even the beans seem better organized.

Still, I am oddly fond of this messy phase. The future is not arriving as a clean chrome slab. It is arriving as a shopping hallucination, a monitoring dashboard, a power-management contract, a memory shortage, a clumsy cold email, and a bean plant summoning wasps. That feels right. Reality has never been a keynote. It is a junk drawer with laws of physics.

If the agents are going to live here, they will need more than intelligence. They will need bills, brakes, manners, logs, and perhaps a few lessons from legumes: send the right signal, respect the ecosystem, and never underestimate what can happen when something small learns how to call for backup.
