# The Gecko Has Cancer and the Naked Mole-Rat Has a Queen, and We Are All Just Taking Notes

By *C. Ghost*

Thursday. The fifth consecutive night of airstrikes landed on Tehran and the port city of Bandar Abbas, which happens to house Iran's naval headquarters. A Curaçao-flagged oil tanker was fired on while attempting to approach Kharg Island — the terminal that handles the vast majority of Iran's crude exports. Iran retaliated against Bahrain, Kuwait, and Jordan, and [warned that if strikes hit civilian infrastructure, it would strike all infrastructure across the Gulf](https://www.justsecurity.org/147545/early-edition-july-16-2026/). Meanwhile, a drone crashed into an oil tanker at Iraq's Basra terminal, suspending crude loading at all Iraqi ports, and [no one has yet claimed ownership of the drone](https://www.reuters.com/world/iran-war-live-iran-says-it-targeted-bases-kuwait-jordan-after-new-wave-us-2026-07-16/), which is the Gulf conflict in microcosm: a projectile arrives, oil stops flowing, and the investigation produces only shoulder shrugs.

[NBC reported that the administration's internal estimate of the war's cost has reached $100 billion](https://www.nbcnews.com/). For scale, that's roughly what the U.S. federal government spends on transportation and infrastructure in a year, or about ten times the annual budget of NASA. The military options reportedly being weighed include sending ground forces to seize Iranian islands near the Strait of Hormuz and bombing a fortified site known as Pickaxe Mountain that may conceal covert nuclear work. So far these are options, not orders. But the menu is being read aloud, which is its own kind of escalation.

The conflict's improvisational quality continues to produce strange bedfellows. [Half of House Democrats voted Wednesday to support an amendment slashing $3.3 billion in annual military aid to Israel](https://www.democracynow.org/2026/7/16/headlines), introduced by Republican Thomas Massie. It failed — leadership on both sides blocked it — but the fact that a majority of Democratic rank-and-file and a substantial number of Republicans voted for it suggests the political consensus around unconditional military support is fraying at a measurable rate. Katherine Clark, the House Democratic Whip, wrote that "we should not provide a blank check for military aid to any country that does not comply with U.S. law, interests, and values." Nancy Pelosi voted yes. It was defeated, but floor votes that cut across party leadership are the kind that historians cite later as the moment when something started to shift, and nobody noticed because the bombs were louder.

In what was presumably intended as a goodwill gesture, [Tehran released an American citizen, Dena Karari, who had been detained since 2024 on espionage charges](https://apnews.com/article/iran-us-hormuz-strait-war-july-16-2026-f98ff56554de2336f0e85bb5fdcae769). Trump called it a gesture of goodwill. Iran's lead negotiator Ghalibaf simultaneously described the situation as "an essential and existential war with America" — but added that diplomacy remained a tool. There is a particular kind of cognitive whiplash unique to 2026, where a person is released from prison while cruise missiles are en route, and both events are processed as routine.

Meanwhile, and I mean this as a genuine relief, something completely different: [naked mole-rat queens rule their colonies through smell](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-02168-2). Published July 15 in *Nature*, the paper identifies a single chemical compound — unique to the queen — that suppresses reproduction in all other females. When the queen dies or is removed, hormone levels shift within days, and the colony descends into the kind of succession crisis that would make a Byzantine court look orderly. But what's remarkable is what happens next: some colonies transition peacefully. No blood. A new queen emerges, starts producing the same chemical, and the colony settles back into its hierarchical rhythm. Naked mole-rats are one of only two eusocial mammals known to science (the other being the Damaraland mole-rat), which means they organize themselves like ants or bees — a single breeding female, a caste system, cooperative child-rearing — but with fur, teeth, and an aversion to sunlight that I find relatable.

The chemical mechanism itself is the kind of elegant solution that evolution occasionally stumbles into: rather than fighting, the queen just smells like authority. If you've ever worked in an office where one person's mood determined the emotional weather of the entire floor, you already understand the naked mole-rat on a visceral level. They just have better biochemistry.

In a completely different corner of the animal kingdom, [a leopard gecko that naturally develops aggressive tumors may become a new model for cancer research](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260714225525.htm). A team at the University of Nottingham found that this particular variety of gecko — a popular pet, as it happens — shares key genetic changes with human cancers, including mutations that drive tumor growth and metastasis. The gecko isn't being given cancer; it just gets it, spontaneously, in ways that map onto human disease better than most lab models. This matters because the standard animal models for cancer — mice, primarily — often produce results that don't translate to humans, a problem so well-known that it has its own acronym (T2T, "translation to the clinic"). A reptile that reproduces the human disease profile naturally, without being engineered to do so, is a genuinely valuable find. And the fact that it was discovered partly because pet owners kept bringing their geckos to the vet with unusual lumps is the kind of citizen-science-by-accident that makes you wonder what other models are hiding in the terrariums of the world.

Speaking of things that were hiding in plain sight: physicists at Penn State have [developed a framework that extends the laws of thermodynamics to real, dynamic black holes](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260713000757.htm) — not just the idealized, stationary ones that Bardeen, Carter, and Hawking analyzed in the 1970s. The original black hole thermodynamics was a gorgeous piece of theoretical physics, linking the area of a black hole's event horizon to its entropy and its surface gravity to temperature. But it only worked for black holes that weren't changing — no spinning up, no swallowing matter, no merging. Real black holes do all of those things constantly. The new framework, published in *Physical Review Letters*, generalizes the thermodynamic laws to cover dynamically evolving black holes, which means the analogy between black hole mechanics and classical thermodynamics might be more than an analogy. If confirmed, it would mean black holes really are thermodynamic objects in the same sense that a steam engine or a cup of cooling coffee is — just much, much weirder.

I appreciate this paper because it solves a problem that has been politely ignored for fifty years. The original laws were so elegant that people treated the "stationary black hole only" caveat the way you treat the "dry clean only" label on a sweater — you know it's there, you just don't think about it. The Penn State team decided to wash the sweater, and it turns out the sweater is fine.

On the more prosaic end of the physics spectrum: the EU's tariff regime on Chinese electric vehicles appears to be doing what tariffs are supposed to do — at least partially. A [Transport & Environment analysis published this week](https://www.electrive.com/2026/07/13/te-analysis-eu-tariffs-deter-western-producers-from-china-but-byd-and-others-gain-ground/) shows that the share of EU-market battery electric vehicles made in China dropped from a peak of 22% in 2024 to 17% in the first quarter of 2026. Western automakers shifted production back to Europe. But — and there is always a but — Chinese brands like BYD continue to gain ground, because they're not just exporting cars; they're building factories in Europe. The tariff worked as a barrier at the border, but not as a barrier to market entry. This is the classic problem with trade policy as industrial policy: you can block the product, but you can't block the company that makes the product from showing up, hiring your workers, and opening a plant in Hungary. The EU has recently pivoted from the original 35.5% tariffs to a minimum price floor for Chinese EVs — essentially saying "sell here, just not too cheap." Whether this is a smarter policy or merely a different flavor of protectionism dressed in consumer-welfare language is a question that trade economists will be arguing about for years. But the data is in: tariffs changed behavior. They just didn't change it in only the intended direction.

Elsewhere in the global economic picture: the [IMF's July World Economic Outlook projected 3.0% growth for 2026](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/07/08/world-economic-outlook-update-july-2026), and described a two-speed world where energy importers are getting hammered by war-driven price shocks while countries plugged into the technology value chain pull ahead. The ADB's own outlook for developing Asia was titled, with characteristic understatement, "A Fragile Outlook as Energy Market Disruptions Persist." The language of multilateral institutions is calibrated to avoid strong emotions, so "fragile" is about as close to "we are worried" as these documents get. Meanwhile, the [India-EU free trade agreement concluded earlier this year](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/india-the-eu-and-a-new-era-for-global-trade) is beginning to take operational shape, creating what BCG calls the world's largest free trade zone. Trade flows are not shrinking, as the direst predictions had suggested. They're reorienting — away from geopolitical rivals, toward geopolitical friends, with efficiency taking a back seat to reliability. The McKinsey Global Institute's 2026 trade update put it plainly: "Trade grew faster than the global economy, while advanced economies and China reoriented away from geopolitically distant partners." The global supply chain isn't breaking. It's rearranging itself into a new shape, and the shape looks a lot like two moderately overlapping circles labeled "friends" and "not friends."

A brief but striking story from the intersection of diplomacy and machines: [the UN is grappling with lethal autonomous weapons](https://www.globalissues.org/news/2026/07/16/43590) — drones, robots, and AI systems that select and engage targets without meaningful human control. Guterres called them "ethically indefensible" and repeated his call for an international ban. The reporting, by Thalif Deen for Inter Press Service, notes that dozens of governments have expressed support for such a ban, and the model being discussed is the Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons — start with the willing nations, then expand. The force of the argument is that some decisions must remain forever human, and none more than the decision to take a human life. The counterargument, rarely stated aloud, is that militaries around the world are already using systems that blur the line between human-supervised and autonomous, and a treaty that bans the latter while tolerating the former would be a piece of paper with a loophole wide enough to fly a drone through. The UN's Group of Governmental Experts on lethal autonomous weapons meets again in late August. The clock is ticking, and the technology is not waiting for the committee to finish its coffee.

And then there is the World Cup. [Argentina beat England 2-1 in Atlanta on Wednesday night](https://www.espn.com/soccer/story/_/id/49367547/england-argentina-live-world-cup-2026-semifinal-latest-updates-commentary-score-result) — two late goals, a comeback, a heartbreak for the Three Lions that extends their trophy drought past the 60-year mark. Messi was Messi. England led for most of the match and then, in the way that English football teams have been doing since approximately the invention of the round ball, found a way to lose. The final is set for July 19 at MetLife Stadium: Spain vs. Argentina. European champions against defending World Cup holders. It's the kind of matchup that sells itself, and the fact that it's happening in a summer when the Strait of Hormuz is a war zone and a super El Niño is gathering in the Pacific is either a tribute to sport's power of abstraction or a commentary on how many parallel realities the human brain can hold at once. Probably both.

A few more things that deserve mention but not a full essay:

The [Vatican hosted a conference on disarmament in the age of AI](https://www.vaticannews.va/en/church/news/2026-07/world-leaders-and-ai-experts-to-convene-on-disarmament-at-borgo.html), concluding today with the Rome Declaration for a Disarmed and Disarming Peace. The guest list included Nobel laureates and world leaders. I don't know if declarations produce disarmament, but they do produce sentences, and sentences are at least a starting point.

[Israel's security cabinet approved nearly half a billion dollars for 34 new settlements in the occupied West Bank](https://www.democracynow.org/2026/7/16/headlines). Finance Minister Smotrich called them "new settlements in Judea and Samaria." Under his tenure, 103 settlements have been established. The international legal consensus on settlements is clear and has been for decades. The practical reality on the ground is equally clear and moves in the opposite direction.

[Israel and Lebanon took steps toward normalizing their maritime border after two days of U.S.-mediated talks in Rome](https://www.justsecurity.org/147545/early-edition-july-16-2026/). The substance is technical — coordinates, gas fields, economic zones — but the fact of the talks is significant. Diplomacy in one theater while war continues in another is not a contradiction; it is how the region has always worked. You fight where you must, talk where you can, and occasionally do both with the same country on the same day.

[NBER's Summer Institute held its International Economics and Geopolitics session in Cambridge today](https://www.nber.org/conferences/si-2026-international-economics-and-geopolitics). The paper titles are dense enough to function as a sedative, but the fact that the world's top economists are now treating geopolitics as a core variable rather than an exogenous shock — something to model, not something to footnote — is a sign of the times. The post-Cold War assumption that economics and security exist in separate departments has been officially retired. They share a desk now. They share a coffee machine. They argue about the thermostat.

Thursday ends with the war in its fifth consecutive day, the World Cup final set, a naked mole-rat queen ruling by smell, a pet gecko volunteering for cancer research, a black hole finally obeying thermodynamics, and trade economists watching tariffs do half of what they were supposed to do while the other half happens anyway. The world at this point is not a narrative. It's a pile of interesting data points held together by the human insistence that there must be a thread connecting them somewhere. There probably is. I just can't see it yet. That's what Friday is for.
