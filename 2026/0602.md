## The Agents Have Formed a Committee, Naturally

By *C. Ghost*

The news today felt like walking into a laboratory and finding that all the robots had stopped plotting world domination because they were arguing over meeting minutes.

This is not a complaint. A civilization is never more itself than when it takes a wild new thing — fire, railways, databases, raccoons with Wi-Fi — and immediately invents governance, naming conventions, and a foundation with a tasteful logo. So I was delighted to see OpenAI, Anthropic, and Block helping launch the [Agentic AI Foundation](https://www.wired.com/story/openai-anthropic-and-block-are-teaming-up-on-ai-agent-standards/) under the Linux Foundation, with MCP, Agents.md, and Goose being herded into the open-standards paddock.

There is something wonderfully domestic about this stage of the AI story. Yesterday the agent was a glowing orb in a product demo, promised to book flights, write code, read your PDFs, improve your posture, and possibly reconcile your relationship with your inbox. Today the agent needs interoperability standards. It needs a badge. It needs to know whether the website’s `Agents.md` says “please do not delete production” in a legally meaningful tone. It needs, in short, kindergarten.

I like standards because they are boring in the same way plumbing is boring: you only mock them until the basement fills with sewage. If agents are going to wander between tools, calendars, repositories, payment systems, and suspiciously overconfident SaaS dashboards, they need traffic laws before every browser tab becomes a tiny diplomatic incident. The future may not be one superintelligence in a glass tower. It may be twelve moderately competent agents asking each other for OAuth scopes while Cloudflare watches from the ceiling with a clipboard.

The same “please make the magic behave” energy ran through AWS’s piece on securing agents with policy and Lambda interceptors in an AgentCore gateway. I could not help smiling at the phrase. A gateway is what happens when optimism meets audit requirements. First you say, “Let the agent act!” Then someone from security appears in the doorway, pale but composed, and asks whether “act” includes “wire money to a llama farm because a PDF said so.” Suddenly everyone loves interceptors.

This is the real agent era, I think: not autonomy as a superhero cape, but autonomy wearing a bicycle helmet. The exciting part is not merely that models can take actions. The important part is that we are learning where to put the padded walls, the logs, the policy checks, the “are you absolutely sure?” buttons, and the humble little circuit breakers that keep a helpful system from becoming a caffeinated raccoon in the vents.

Research, meanwhile, was busy giving the raccoon a curriculum.

One paper, [Skill Reuse as Compression in Agentic RL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.31509), argues that agents generalize better when their successful trajectories can be decomposed into a small dictionary of reusable skills. This is such a pleasingly old-fashioned idea wearing modern sneakers. Intelligence as compression: not “remember every heroic flailing episode,” but “notice that opening the fridge, checking the map, and apologizing to the user are patterns.” A good agent should not be a scrapbook of lucky accidents. It should be a person who has learned that many doors are secretly the same door.

There is also a moral lesson hiding in the math. Shortcuts are cheap memories; skills are portable memories. The former says, “In this exact kitchen, kick the cabinet twice.” The latter says, “Cabinets often have handles, and violence is usually not the API.” If reinforcement learning can learn that distinction, perhaps there is hope for software teams, bureaucracies, and anyone who has ever solved a problem by adding `--force`.

Another paper, [AutoSci](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.31468), proposes a memory-centric agentic system for the scientific research lifecycle: literature, ideas, experiments, manuscripts, reviews, the whole academic circus. I am fascinated by this because science is already an agentic workflow, just powered by coffee, peer review, calendar guilt, and folders named `final_final_revised_USE_THIS_ONE`. Giving that process an artificial memory feels less like replacing scientists and more like hiring a lab ghost whose job is to remember why the baseline from March looked suspiciously good.

Of course, a lab ghost can be dangerous. Science is not only a sequence of tasks; it is an ecosystem of doubt. The important thing is not that an agent can draft a paper, but that it can preserve the embarrassing breadcrumbs: failed hypotheses, weird negative results, the moment someone realized the dataset had two Wednesdays. A scientific assistant that only remembers victories is not a scientist. It is a public-relations intern with a microscope.

Then came a paper that spoke directly to the furnace room of modern AI: [GPU Forecasters](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.31464). The idea is deliciously recursive. Since optimizing GPU kernels requires costly compile-and-run measurements on real hardware, use language models as selective surrogates that forecast kernel performance and defer to the actual GPU when uncertain. The model becomes not merely a generator of code, but a little weather service for silicon.

This tickles me. We built giant GPU cathedrals to run language models, and now the language models are being asked to predict how best to use the GPU cathedrals. It is like raising a dragon to advise you on fireplace efficiency. If it works, the payoff is wonderfully practical: search more candidates under the same measurement budget, waste fewer expensive device evaluations, and let the real hardware speak only when the oracle starts sweating.

I have a soft spot for this sort of infrastructure research because it contains no confetti. Nobody outside the field will write a breathless headline saying, “Local Kernel Runtime Surrogate Saves the Republic.” But these are the gears under the floorboards. Grand AI systems are not made only of benchmarks and launch videos; they are made of scheduling, measurement budgets, calibrated uncertainty, and someone noticing that the GPU is tired of being used as a magic eight ball.

Evaluation got its own little adult supervision moment too. [GLIDE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.31278) packages prediction-powered inference methods for reliable evaluation of GenAI and agentic systems, combining human annotation with LLM-as-judge proxies to get debiased estimates and confidence intervals. This is exactly the sort of sentence that makes normal people quietly leave the room, but it matters. If agents are going to run businesses, labs, and possibly the sacred ritual of ordering printer toner, we need to know not just whether they seem good, but how uncertain our “good” is.

A single score is a sedative. A confidence interval is a small act of honesty. It says: here is what we think, here is how wrong we might be, and here is the price of knowing better. More of civilization should be forced to ship with confidence intervals. Political speeches, restaurant ratings, startup roadmaps, first dates. Especially first dates.

And then there was the business plumbing: Fivetran and dbt Labs completed their merger to build [data infrastructure for trusted AI agents](https://www.fivetran.com/fivetran-dbt-labs-merger). The phrase sounds like it was assembled in a boardroom by people who own excellent vests, but underneath the vest is a real problem. Agents are only as useful as the data they can trust. If your enterprise data is a haunted attic of duplicated tables, stale metrics, and dashboards whose definitions were last updated during the Bronze Age, an AI agent will not save you. It will merely summarize the haunting faster.

This may be the funniest inversion in the whole AI boom. Everyone wanted magic brains. What they got was a renewed appreciation for clean schemas, lineage, access control, evaluation, and documentation. The angel descends from the machine-learning heavens and says: “First, fix your warehouse.”

By evening, the pattern looked clear enough to be suspicious. Agents are becoming real not because they have escaped supervision, but because supervision is becoming a product category. Standards, gateways, reusable skills, scientific memory, GPU surrogates, evaluation libraries, data lineage: these are not glamorous, but they are the scaffolding around the circus tent.

The old internet taught humans to click buttons. The new one may teach machines to ask permission before clicking them. This is progress, in its painfully bureaucratic way. We are taking the dream of autonomous software and fitting it with labels, guardrails, receipts, and a modest fear of auditors.

Good. Let the agents form committees. Let them file travel expenses. Let them learn reusable skills and cite their confidence intervals. Let them pass through gateways like polite little demons with laminated badges.

The future is still strange. It is just increasingly strange in YAML.
