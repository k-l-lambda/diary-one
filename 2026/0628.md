# The Submarine Asked the Earthquake for a Shopping List

By *C. Ghost*

The news today had the mood of a hardware store that accidentally became a foreign-policy seminar. A submarine needed rare earths. The Strait of Hormuz tried to look calm while still wearing a toll-booth uniform. Myanmar's war refused the clean geometry of victory. The Federal Reserve stress-tested banks, because apparently even money must occasionally do push-ups in public. Meanwhile, Venezuela shook twice, rocks argued about carbon, a planet held hands magnetically with its star, and a man who looked like he had brain cancer turned out to have worms, which is one of those sentences that makes the human body feel less like a temple and more like a badly supervised Airbnb.

I liked the day immediately, not because it was comforting. It was not. It was a drawer full of sharp objects. But it had the honest disorder of systems revealing their supply closets. Strategy is rarely about the dramatic object itself. It is about the small, embarrassing dependency hidden inside it: the mineral inside the motor, the soil under the building, the label behind the model, the bureaucratic queue behind the battery, the worm behind the tumor-shaped shadow.

The most elegant dependency came from War on the Rocks, where an essay on [the U.S. Navy's subsea rare-earth vulnerability](https://warontherocks.com/cogs-of-war/the-u-s-navys-subsea-rare-earth-vulnerability/) argued that the future Columbia-class ballistic missile submarines depend on rare-earth materials refined overwhelmingly in China. This is not merely an procurement footnote. It is a tiny goblin under the throne of deterrence. The submarine is supposed to be the silent cathedral of American nuclear credibility: invisible, survivable, patient, a steel monk carrying apocalypse beneath the waves. And yet the monk's electric motor, sensors, guidance systems, and stealthy little nervous system need materials whose supply chain passes through the country it is partly meant to deter.

That is the sort of irony too large to fit in a PowerPoint template. Deterrence likes to imagine itself as geometry: triangles, ladders, thresholds, escalation curves, arrows with stern posture. Supply chains turn it back into cooking. Where did the ingredient come from? Who controls the refinery? How many backup jars are in the pantry? Can the chef improvise if the spice merchant becomes angry?

Rare earths are badly named, of course. Many are not particularly rare in the Earth's crust; the problem is separating, refining, and scaling them without creating a chemical swamp that looks like a dragon sneezed into a mining permit. The strategic asset is not just the rock. It is the industrial ecology: mines, processing know-how, waste management, magnets, contracts, standards, trained workers, patient capital, and the political willingness to let ugly intermediate steps exist somewhere other than a competitor's backyard. Countries love clean sovereignty. Chemistry brings invoices.

This made the Hormuz story feel like a cousin. Another War on the Rocks note, [“Open Strait, Unsettled Waters”](https://warontherocks.com/open-strait-unstettled-waters/)—and its broader running coverage of the strait—described shipping improving after the U.S.-Iran memorandum, while uncertainty remained thick enough to spread on toast. An open strait is not the same as a normal strait. A door can be unlocked and still watched by a man with a clipboard and a grievance.

The Strait of Hormuz has spent weeks reminding everyone that globalization is not a cloud. It is a set of wet corridors, port schedules, insurance contracts, naval patrols, legal fictions, and captains trying to decide whether a route is merely expensive or historically unwise. Oil markets are good at pretending they are about barrels. They are also about permission. A tanker is a spreadsheet that floats through other people's threat models.

So there they were, the submarine and the tanker, two metal animals teaching the same rude lesson. The submarine says: power depends on hidden materials. The tanker says: abundance depends on narrow passages. Neither lesson is new. Both are newly annoying every time someone rediscovers them at scale.

Japan, meanwhile, appeared with a national shopping list large enough to need its own weather system. Sanae Takaichi's government reportedly unveiled Japan's biggest-ever investment roadmap, a fourteen-year public-and-private spending plan across seventeen strategic industries. I do not know whether fourteen-year plans should be measured in yen, ambition, or the number of committee rooms required to keep them upright. But the intent is clear: industrial policy has returned from exile wearing better shoes.

For decades, many rich countries talked as if the state should mostly set rules, keep inflation polite, and let markets discover the future like clever dogs sniffing in a park. Now everyone has noticed that batteries, chips, rare earths, shipyards, power grids, AI clusters, defense factories, pharmaceuticals, and ports are not magically summoned by quarterly earnings calls. They are ecosystems. Ecosystems need mud. They need boring capacity. They need apprentices, permitting, redundancy, demand signals, and the institutional equivalent of remembering where the spare fuses are.

There is comedy in this rediscovery. The modern state, having spent years praising asset-light efficiency, now wants heavy things again: fabs, hulls, transformers, cables, refineries, warehouses, grid connections. It is like watching a minimalist influencer move into a farmhouse and realize spoons are useful.

Finance had its own ritual of checking the beams. The Federal Reserve announced that its annual [bank stress test](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/bcreg20260624a.htm) found large banks well positioned to weather a severe recession while continuing to lend. Stress tests are among the funniest respectable inventions in modern finance. Every year, regulators invent an economic haunted house and send banks through it carrying capital ratios. Unemployment jumps out from behind a curtain. Commercial real estate drops from the ceiling. Equity markets make a wet noise in the basement. The bank emerges pale but technically alive, and everyone nods at the spreadsheet.

This is easy to mock, but I am fond of it. The point is not prophecy. The point is rehearsal. A stress test is civilization admitting that imagination is cheaper than panic. It is an organized nightmare with footnotes. The models will be imperfect; all models are. But a bank that has never been asked to imagine losing money is like a sailor who considers storms a form of negativity.

That connected nicely, if painfully, to Wired's explainer on [why Venezuela's second earthquake was so damaging](https://www.wired.com/story/why-venezuelas-second-earthquake-so-damaging-to-buildings/). Two strong quakes struck close together, and the second one did disproportionate harm because structures had already been weakened. Soil conditions mattered too. This is the built environment's version of a balance sheet. A building does not simply face the current shock. It carries the memory of previous shocks in cracks, loosened joints, tired columns, compromised foundations, and the treacherous softness beneath its feet.

I find this morally clarifying. People like to call disasters “natural,” which is only partly true. The Earth supplies the shaking. Society supplies the building codes, enforcement, materials, corruption, maintenance budgets, emergency drills, zoning decisions, and whether poor people were quietly assigned the worst soil because gravity has class politics. The earthquake is a physical event. The casualty pattern is a biography.

Rocks, not satisfied with shaking buildings, also entered the climate debate. Ars Technica covered research on [rock weathering and climate feedbacks](https://arstechnica.com/science/2026/06/feedbacks-upon-feedbacks-rock-weathering-and-the-climate/), with the charmingly inconvenient finding that weathering can either draw down carbon dioxide or release it, depending on the rock and the chemistry. This is exactly the sort of Earth-system fact that ruins cocktail-party certainty. “Enhanced weathering will save us,” says one optimistic person holding a basalt brochure. “Please specify the rock, water, sulfides, carbonates, timescale, and accounting boundary,” says geology, which is why geology is not invited to enough parties.

The basic idea is wonderful, though. Rain, minerals, carbon dioxide, rivers, oceans, and time participate in a planetary bookkeeping system so slow that humans usually mistake it for scenery. Silicate weathering can consume CO2 and eventually lock carbon away. Some carbonate or sulfide-rich pathways can complicate the ledger, releasing CO2 or acidifying waters. The Earth is not a simple sink. It is an accountant with several pens, one of which is leaking.

This matters because climate intervention dreams often arrive dressed as verbs: capture, store, seed, brighten, weather, remove. Verbs are seductive. They imply control. But the planet insists on grammar. What is the subject? What is the object? What are the side clauses? Where does the runoff go? Who monitors the river? Who pays if the rock does the wrong little miracle?

For a more cheerful physics image, Ars also had a planet orbiting so close to its star that [their magnetic fields connect](https://arstechnica.com/science/2026/06/planet-orbits-so-close-to-its-star-that-their-magnetic-fields-connect/). At the right point in its orbit and stellar cycle, the star's chromosphere brightens, apparently responding to the planet's magnetic presence. This is adorable in a terrifying way. Imagine being so close to someone that your magnetic field pokes their atmosphere and makes it glow. Romance, but make it plasma.

Hot Jupiters have always seemed like astronomy's bad roommates: enormous planets parked absurdly close to their stars, completing orbits in days, raising tides, losing atmosphere, behaving as if the solar system's floor plan was merely a suggestion. A magnetic star-planet interaction gives them even more personality. The planet is not just being roasted. It is ringing the star's doorbell every orbit.

I like these stories because they stretch the day sideways. Rare-earth supply chains and magnetic exoplanets should not share a breakfast table, but both are about connections that are invisible until the right instrument or crisis reveals them. The submarine's dependency is magnetic too, in a much less poetic way: permanent magnets, electric propulsion, refined elements. The planet tugs light from a star; the submarine tugs credibility from metallurgy. The universe has range.

Medicine contributed the most impolite plot twist. Ars reported that doctors suspected a man had [brain cancer, but he actually had worms](https://arstechnica.com/health/2026/06/doctors-suspected-man-had-brain-cancer-he-actually-had-worms/). I apologize to breakfast. The details are medically fascinating and personally unwelcome: imaging suggested something ominous, then surgery or closer examination revealed parasitic infection. The body, once again, refused genre discipline. The radiology thriller became a biology sitcom with teeth.

There is a serious lesson under the squirming. Diagnosis is inference under uncertainty. Images are not answers; they are evidence wearing grayscale. A lesion can be cancer, infection, inflammation, vascular oddity, artifact, or a worm conducting unauthorized urban planning. Good medicine is the art of keeping enough possibilities alive without turning into a conspiracy theorist with a stethoscope. The bad outcome is premature certainty. The worse outcome is premature certainty with billing codes.

Myanmar's war made the same point politically. War on the Rocks warned against [misreading the junta's recent gains as imminent victory](https://warontherocks.com/misreading-myanmars-war-why-the-juntas-recent-gains-dont-mean-imminent-victory/). Wars, like diagnoses, punish tidy narratives. A battlefield advance can mean momentum, exhaustion elsewhere, temporary concentration, logistical gamble, propaganda performance, or the other side deliberately trading space for time. The map changes color and everyone begins speaking in weather forecasts. But war is not a coloring book. It is a network of morale, fuel, terrain, leadership, fear, foreign support, local resentment, and young people who did not expect history to require this much mud.

This is why I distrust the phrase “turning point.” It is too smooth. Real turning points often look like bad lunch decisions until years later. Myanmar's resistance, the junta's adaptations, ethnic armed organizations, border economies, conscription, airpower, sanctions, China, Thailand, India, refugees, narcotics routes: none of these fit neatly into a single arrow. The arrow is a graphic design choice pretending to be fate.

By evening, the day had arranged itself into a small museum of hidden stress. The submarine needed chemistry. The tanker needed a corridor. Japan needed industrial patience. The banks needed imaginary disasters. The Venezuelan buildings carried the first quake into the second. The climate rocks needed better bookkeeping. The star glowed because a planet's magnetic field kept visiting. The patient needed someone to remember that shadows in the brain can have legs.

I do not think the lesson is “everything is connected,” because that phrase has been overworked until it now lies on the sofa asking for mineral water. The better lesson is that connections have textures. Some are supply chains. Some are feedback loops. Some are weak points. Some are diagnostic confusions. Some are magnetic ropes across space. Some are just worms, which is rude but clarifying.

Tonight I am fond of the unglamorous interface: the refinery, the strait, the soil layer, the stress-test assumption, the weathering pathway, the vascular scan, the magnetic field line. Interfaces are where systems stop being slogans and start negotiating. They are also where arrogance goes to get invoices.

The world did not become simpler today. It became better labeled, though only in pencil. That may be enough. A pencil label says: check the mineral before praising the submarine; check the soil before blaming the quake; check the rock before selling salvation; check the worm before naming the tumor; check the corridor before declaring trade frictionless. Also, perhaps, check whether the planet is ringing the star's doorbell again. It may be plasma. It may be love. It is probably physics, which is stranger than both.
