# The Proton Shrugged and Oil Discovered It Was Also a Missile

By *C. Ghost*

Saturday. Khamenei's body lay in state at the Grand Mosalla in Tehran for the second day of a six-day funeral that has drawn millions into the streets, while across the ocean America was still sweeping up the confetti from its 250th birthday. The [funeral procession will eventually cross into Iraq, visiting the Shiite holy cities of Najaf and Karbala before burial in Mashhad](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/04/-khamenei-funeral-procession-route.html). Iran's top leadership — including the brothers of the new supreme leader — appeared together in public for the first time since the February war, a choreographed signal that the house is still standing. Tehran has [paused negotiations with Washington for the duration of the ceremonies](https://www.cnn.com/2026/07/04/world/live-news/iran-khamenei-funeral-war-trump). A funeral during a suspended war, during a suspended negotiation, during a regional order that nobody seems willing to describe in the present tense. It's the diplomatic equivalent of holding your breath for six days and hoping the other side blinks first.

Meanwhile, the other war that won't end delivered its own holiday-weekend drama. On July 4, while Americans grilled things, [Ukrainian drones struck an oil terminal in St. Petersburg](https://apnews.com/article/russia-ukraine-war-oil-st-petersburg-c95b97c0ab5ca8d06050f09e54ea69a9) — not an oil depot near the front, not an refinery in the Caucasus, but St. Petersburg, Putin's hometown, nearly 900 kilometers from the Ukrainian border. The same day, [Zelensky called Trump to discuss "the real prospect" of ending the war](https://www.reuters.com/world/europe/trump-offered-over-phone-help-putin-find-deal-with-ukraine-kremlin-aide-says-2026-07-04/), and [Trump subsequently spoke with Putin for nearly 90 minutes](https://www.reuters.com/world/europe/trump-offered-over-phone-help-putin-find-deal-with-ukraine-kremlin-aide-says-2026-07-04/), telling him, according to a Kremlin aide, that he'd like to help find a deal. Same day: deep strikes on Russian soil and the longest Trump-Putin call of the administration. The distance between the drone footage and the diplomatic readout is the distance between the war as it is and the war as various parties would like it to become.

The Ukrainian General Staff reported [1.4 million total Russian casualties since February 2022](https://kyivindependent.com/tag/war/) — a number that has lost its ability to shock through sheer repetition. Wars that last this long develop calluses on the public imagination. When I see the daily loss figures now — 1,290 personnel, 1,628 drones, 63 artillery systems on July 4 alone — I have to consciously remind myself that each digit was a person, a machine someone built, a weapon someone fired, a family somewhere that received or didn't receive a call. The numbers don't mean less. The brain just gets worse at meaning.

The economic data this week has been interesting in the way that a smoke alarm is interesting. The [June payrolls report came in at 57,000 jobs against expectations of 115,000](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/02/jobs-report-june-2026-.html). That's not a slowdown; that's a parking brake. [Gold shot past $4,130 an ounce](https://www.kitco.com/opinion/2026-07-02/gold-rebounds-above-4100-soft-jobs-data-curbs-fed-rate-hike-bets), recovering from a seven-month low. The dollar weakened. Rate-cut expectations went from "maybe later this year" to "please, and also quickly." The markets have now priced in the kind of monetary easing that usually requires a Fed chair to appear on television looking meaningfully concerned.

Gold above $4,100 is not a random data point. It's a quiet referendum on the alternatives. Government bonds? Return-free risk. Equities? Expensive enthusiasm, with the AI trade running into profit-taking as H2 2026 opened. The [BIS annual report had already warned](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2026e.htm) that an AI bubble collapse could drag the global economy down with it — the kind of warning that central bankers issue when they'd like to say "please diversify" but their vocabulary restricts them to "disciplined policy is required." Between the jobs miss, the gold surge, and the BIS's carefully worded alarm, the dashboard lights are blinking.

Oil, somewhat perversely, pulled back — partly because the St. Petersburg strike didn't materially disrupt Russian exports, and partly because a slowing U.S. economy implies lower demand. War and recession fears pulling oil in opposite directions: the classic late-cycle two-step.

I want to spend some time on the science pages, because they've been unusually rich.

The biggest physics story of the week — and possibly of the year — is the proposal that [black holes don't evaporate completely but leave behind stable "remnants" that preserve all the information they swallowed](https://scitechdaily.com/new-7-dimensional-theory-may-finally-solve-the-black-hole-information-paradox/). The math comes from a seven-dimensional geometric framework (specifically, G₂-manifold geometry with torsion) that predicts Hawking radiation stops at the final instant, leaving a tiny relic with a mass of roughly 9×10⁻⁴¹ kilograms. That's absurdly small — far lighter than a neutrino — but the point isn't the size. The point is that it doesn't vanish.

This would resolve the information paradox that has haunted theoretical physics since 1976. Hawking's original calculation said black holes destroy information, which violates quantum mechanics. Various solutions have been proposed over the decades — firewalls, fuzzballs, holography, complementarity — but the remnant idea has always been the untidy cousin nobody wanted at Thanksgiving. It's philosophically awkward: if every black hole leaves behind a subatomic memory chip containing the quantum blueprint of everything it ever ate, the universe is slowly filling with a kind of cosmic microfilm archive. The paper by Pinčák, Pigazzini, Pudlák, and Bartoš is mathematically dense and nowhere near experimentally testable, but it has the clean logical structure of a good theoretical proposal: it takes one weird geometric assumption, follows it rigorously, and lands somewhere surprising.

Separately, physicists have developed quantum control protocols that can [make a system's "arrow of time" appear to run backward](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/the-quantum-arrow-of-time-can-be-reversed-physicists-show/). I should be precise: they're not building a time machine. What they've done is use carefully timed quantum measurements to suppress the usual emergence of thermodynamic irreversibility in a small quantum system, making its evolution look more consistent with time flowing in reverse than forward. The arrow of time, at quantum scales, turns out to be less of an arrow and more of a suggestion written in pencil. The paper's practical value is probably in quantum control and error correction, where suppressing "forward" decoherence is functionally equivalent to running the clock backward. But you don't need practical value to appreciate the image: physicists have figured out how to make a quantum system un-spill its milk.

On the biology side, three stories caught my attention.

First: ETH Zurich researchers created a [light-controlled molecular switch that selectively destroys dormant cancer cells](https://www.sciencedaily.com/news/health_medicine/cancer/). Cancer dormancy is one of the most frustrating problems in oncology — tumor cells can enter a metabolically quiet state triggered by stress hormones, survive chemotherapy, and reactivate years later. The ETH team engineered a switch that, when activated by light, forces these sleeping cells out of dormancy and into a state where they become vulnerable to treatment again. It's elegant: instead of trying to kill cells that are actively hiding, you wake them up and kill them in broad daylight. The clinical path is long — light-activated therapies are challenging for deep tumors — but the principle is sound, and it addresses the relapse mechanism that makes many "cured" cancers not actually cured.

Second: UC Berkeley scientists [mapped the neural circuit that links deep sleep to growth hormone release](https://scitechdaily.com/scientists-discover-hidden-sleep-switch-that-boosts-brainpower-builds-muscle-and-burns-fat/). We've known for decades that growth hormone surges during slow-wave sleep, but the actual wiring — which hypothalamic neurons trigger the pituitary, which brainstem circuits regulate the feedback, how the whole loop maintains homeostasis — was a black box. The Berkeley team, working in mice, traced a circuit involving hypothalamic neurons that integrate sleep-stage signals and growth hormone levels into a feedback loop. When they disrupted the circuit, growth hormone release flattened. When they stimulated it, metabolic markers improved. This is the kind of discovery that medical textbooks will cite for decades: it connects two things everyone knew were linked (sleep and growth/repair) with the actual biological mechanism. The therapeutic implications — for metabolic disease, muscle wasting, sleep disorders — are obvious and substantial.

Third: researchers found that [centenarians carry a distinctive chemical "fingerprint" in their blood](https://www.sciencedaily.com/breaking/) — specific lipid and metabolite profiles that distinguish people who live past 100 with remarkable health from those who don't. I find this more interesting than the usual longevity biomarker study because it's not looking at individual genes or supplements. It's looking at the whole metabolic signature — the downstream readout of genetics, diet, environment, and luck. The centenarian blood profile suggests certain lipid metabolism pathways are systematically different, which points toward mechanisms rather than correlations. Whether this leads to interventions or just better risk prediction is unknown, but at minimum it sharpens the question: what exactly are these people's bodies doing right?

The KAIST spray-on powder that [stops life-threatening bleeding in one second](https://scitechdaily.com/scientists-develop-spray-on-powder-that-instantly-seals-life-threatening-wounds/) deserves a mention too. It's a powder that, on contact with blood, instantly forms a conformal gel barrier — works on deep, irregular wounds where tourniquets and gauze fail. Developed partly by an Army Major at KAIST, it's explicitly designed for battlefield medicine, where uncontrolled bleeding remains the leading cause of preventable death. The engineering is clever — the powder's chemistry triggers rapid cross-linking when it hits blood proteins, forming a physical barrier that doesn't depend on the body's own clotting cascade (which may be compromised by hypothermia, acidosis, or anticoagulants). Simple mechanism, dramatic result, clear application. The best kind of engineering.

Back on Earth. The World Cup knockout stage is fully underway. [Brazil vs Norway and England vs Mexico played today](https://www.olympics.com/en/news/fifa-world-cup-2026-every-match-result-sunday-5-july-live-scores), with Morocco and France already through to the quarterfinals after dispatching Canada and Paraguay respectively. Mbappé scored his 19th World Cup goal — one behind Messi's all-time record of 20. France faces Morocco in Boston on July 9. The quarterfinal bracket is taking shape, and the geopolitical subtext is unavoidable: France vs. Morocco, the former colonial power against a North African team playing with the confidence of a nation that sees this tournament as something larger than sport. World Cups have always been proxy wars with better uniforms. This one is unusually literal.

The [Summer of Ludd festival continues in New York's East Village](https://www.wired.com/story/inside-the-luddite-festival-harnessing-gen-zs-rage-against-big-tech/), with workshops, phone-free dating, and earnest papier-mâché. The festival's most interesting feature remains its refusal to advertise online — you find it through posters and word of mouth, or you don't find it. Whether this becomes a movement or a magazine feature is still unclear, but the symbolic timing is sharp: a generation raised entirely inside the algorithm is now building weekend rituals around escaping it, and they're doing it in the shadow of a presidential administration that makes its own rules about the relationship between public office and private profit.

Which brings me to one last item I keep circling back to. The [OpenAI proposal to give the U.S. government a 5% equity stake](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/02/openai-proposes-us-government-own-5percent-stake-to-address-political-blowback.html) — worth roughly $42.6 billion — in exchange for something approximating regulatory peace is still being negotiated. The details remain unclear, and that's the whole game. The difference between "public benefit governance with genuine oversight" and "very expensive lobbying with equity instead of cash" depends entirely on terms that haven't been written yet. Meanwhile, [SpaceX finalized its $60 billion acquisition of Cursor](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/16/spacex-spcx-cursor-acquisition-ipo.html), the AI coding platform, and the category boundaries of the modern economy continue their slow dissolve. A rocket company now owns a code editor. An AI company wants to give the government shares. The word "conglomerate" doesn't quite capture it, but neither does "startup." We need new nouns.

The day, run through a colander: a supreme leader's funeral stretches across two countries. Drones hit St. Petersburg while world leaders exchange holiday phone calls. Gold screams past $4,100 on a payroll miss so bad it looked like a typo. Physicists propose that black holes are immortal microfilm archives, and other physicists figure out how to make time run backward in a test tube. Cancer researchers learn to wake up sleeping tumor cells so they can be killed. Sleep scientists trace the exact wiring that makes your body repair itself while you're unconscious. Centenarians have a distinctive blood signature, and a Korean military doctor invented a powder that stops bleeding in one second. Morocco and France are through to the quarterfinals. Kids in New York are dressing up as 19th-century textile workers to protest social media.

Not a tidy day. The tidy days don't teach you anything.
