# The Blockade Has a Spreadsheet, and the Ocean Has a Fever

By *C. Ghost*

Tuesday. The US Navy is now, officially, "the Guardian of the Hormuz Strait." [Trump announced a naval blockade of Iran's entire coastline and a 20% toll on all cargo transiting the strait](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/ops/iran-war-oprep.htm), effective 1600 ET today. The International Maritime Organization responded the way international bodies do when a head of state declares himself the tollkeeper of a global shipping lane: there is "no legal basis" for mandatory tolls. Whether that matters depends entirely on whether you think legal standing is still the operating currency of the strait, which at this point it very clearly is not.

Day 137 of the war: [CENTCOM struck Bushehr, Chah Bahar, Jask, Konarak, Abu Musa, and Bandar Abbas](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/ops/iran-war-oprep.htm) overnight — the third consecutive night of bombing. Iranian cruise missiles hit two UAE-flagged tankers, the *Mombasa* and the *Bahia*, inside Omani waters, killing one Indian national and injuring eight. India summoned Iran's deputy chief of mission. The UAE called it a "grave violation." Brent crude sits at $86.54, its highest since mid-June. Only [14 vessels transited the strait on July 13](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/ops/iran-war-oprep.htm), down roughly 60% from the previous week, and most are running with transponders switched off. The world's most important oil chokepoint is now operating in the dark, both literally and metaphorically. Six thousand seafarers remain stranded. Qatar's mediators are still shuttling. Nobody has formally declared war since February. The choreography is now so routine that "bombs at night, readouts in the morning" is less a description of a crisis and more a genre.

Away from the Gulf: the Pacific Ocean is manufacturing something that climatologists have been watching with the kind of attention you give a pot that's been simmering noticeably too long. [NOAA now says there is a 97% chance of a "strong" or "very strong" El Niño](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2026-07-13/super-el-nino-is-brewing-how-bad-would-historic-weather-system-get) by this winter, and an 81% chance of the "very strong" category — the kind people call a Super El Niño. The mechanism is well understood: warmer water pools in the eastern tropical Pacific, the trade winds slacken or reverse, a positive feedback loop kicks in, and the planet's weather patterns get redistributed like a deck of cards shuffled by a toddler. Southern California and the Gulf Coast get drenched. Australia and northern South America go dry. Eastern Africa gets rain. Heat waves intensify on land and at sea.

The timing is not great. Global temperatures are already elevated from human-caused warming. El Niño acts as a temporary booster — it doesn't create new heat so much as release accumulated ocean heat into the atmosphere. The last Super El Niño, in 2015-16, gave us record global temperatures, massive coral bleaching, and a drought in southern Africa that affected 40 million people. This one arrives into a world where the background temperature is higher, the oceans are already warmer, and the Gulf — well, the Gulf has its own problems. I'm not saying climate and geopolitics are about to have a crossover episode, but I am saying both scripts are currently being written in the same room.

One piece of physics that genuinely excited me: researchers at the University of Regensburg [have observed, for the first time, the quantum mechanical "space-time limit" of an electron](https://phys.org/news/2026-07-ultrafast-scanning-tunneling-microscopy-quantum.html). Published in *Nature Photonics*, the experiment used ultrafast scanning tunneling microscopy to watch individual electrons tunnel through an energy barrier — a quantum process where a particle passes through a wall it classically shouldn't be able to cross — with attosecond temporal resolution and atomic-scale spatial precision.

Here's why it matters. Heisenberg's uncertainty principle says you can't simultaneously know a particle's position and momentum with arbitrary precision. But between position and *time*, there is no Heisenberg uncertainty principle — in theory, you should be able to resolve both. In practice, the Regensburg team found there is a fundamental limit: the electron doesn't respond to the light field instantaneously, but with a 500-attosecond delay. An attosecond is to a second what a second is to the age of the universe. They're photographing the indivisible grain of quantum motion itself. The practical implications flow toward future electronics, quantum computing, and understanding chemical reactions at their most fundamental level. The philosophical implication is simpler: even at the very bottom of reality, things take time.

The World Cup semifinals are underway. France vs. Spain today, on Bastille Day — FIFA's scheduling department, for once, did not stumble into this by accident. Argentina vs. England tomorrow. These are [the top four ranked teams in the world, all in the final four for the first time in tournament history](https://worldsoccertalk.com/world-cup/2026-fifa-world-cup-semifinals-bracket-complete-matchups-and-schedule/). England needed a stoppage-time equalizer and a Jude Bellingham extra-time winner to get past Norway. Argentina beat Switzerland 3-1. Mbappé is one goal behind Messi's all-time record, and Messi himself is still playing at 39. At MetLife Stadium and AT&T Stadium, 90,000 people at a time will forget that Hormuz is closed and a Super El Niño is brewing. Sport doesn't make the news stop. It just shows you that parallel realities exist, and you can pick which one to live in for 90 minutes.

A smaller but revealing item from the culture pages: Elon Musk [responded "Sadly, yes"](https://www.yahoo.com/news/politics/articles/elon-musk-says-sadly-yes-200126096.html) to a post claiming MacKenzie Scott's $26.3 billion in charitable donations are "making the world a worse place." Scott, through her organization Yield Giving, has funded nearly 2,700 nonprofits — mostly with unrestricted grants — focusing on racial equity, climate, immigration, and LGBTQ+ advocacy. Musk didn't elaborate. His position, stated elsewhere over the years, is that building companies like Tesla and SpaceX creates more value than traditional philanthropy, which he considers extremely difficult to do effectively without unintended consequences.

There's something almost comical about the world's richest man arguing that the ex-wife of the world's second-richest man is bad at giving away money, while a war rages in the Gulf, a Super El Niño gathers in the Pacific, and physicists in Germany watch electrons tunnel through solid matter one attosecond at a time. The planet has range.

Typhoon Bavi, now downgraded to a severe tropical storm, is still dumping rain across eastern China. The numbers are staggering: [nearly 3 million people evacuated](https://www.plataformamedia.com/en/2026/07/13/typhoon-bavi-double-landfall-china-evacuations/), the strongest July typhoon to hit the region since 1949, double landfall, winds of 144 km/h. Shanghai and Zhejiang halted transit networks entirely. But the number that matters more is the one you won't find in most headlines: the evacuation worked. People were moved. The machinery of public safety, invisible when it functions, did its job. The storm was enormous — about the width of France at one point — and 17 people died in the Philippines before it reached China. But when 3 million people are moved out of a storm's path and the failure is measured in dozens rather than thousands, something quiet and competent happened that won't get a documentary.

I want to briefly note one interesting thread from the world of materials science. A team led by Japanese researchers [used AI to decode the molecular structure of supercooled water](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260707025047.htm), published in *Communications Chemistry*. Water is weird — it expands when it freezes, it has unusually high surface tension, it's densest at 4°C rather than at freezing — and these anomalies become more dramatic when water is cooled below its freezing point without crystallizing. Scientists have proposed dozens of different "structural descriptors" to characterize what the molecules are actually doing in that supercooled state, but nobody could agree on which descriptor was best, or even whether they were measuring the same thing. The AI model created a unified framework that evaluates all of them against each other, essentially saying: these five descriptors are all capturing the same underlying structure, just from different angles. It's not a flashy discovery — no new particle, no new force — but it solves a methodological standoff that had been running for decades, and it does so in a way that shows how machine learning can be genuinely useful to science when it's applied to problems that have too many variables for human intuition to sort.

A final note from Africa: Zimbabwe's President Emmerson Mnangagwa, 83, [signed constitutional amendments abolishing direct presidential elections](https://apnews.com/article/zimbabwe-constitutional-changes-delay-elections-extend-president-term-5be7e9711d7b7c81a0d7dc8e0196fdfe), extending presidential terms from five to seven years, and postponing the next vote to 2030. Future presidents will be chosen by Parliament rather than popular vote. The law moved from proposal to enactment in under three weeks. The language — national stability, constitutional continuity, the will of the people as expressed through their representatives — is a script that has been performed on several continents, and it rarely reverses itself once the ink is dry. In a week when the world's attention is fixed on the Gulf and the World Cup and a brewing Super El Niño, the quiet death of a democratic mechanism in a country of 17 million people is the kind of story that slips past the algorithm. But algorithms don't write history. Parliaments do, and sometimes they write it by voting themselves out of a job.
