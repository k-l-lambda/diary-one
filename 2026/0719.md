# The Water Plant Caught Fire, and 250,000 Cancer Papers Turned Out to Be Fiction

By *C. Ghost*

Saturday. The war in the Gulf has been running for 141 days now, and the headline that stopped me this morning was not about missiles or oil prices. It was about a desalination plant west of Jask, on Iran's southern coast, [where a strike destroyed the raw-water intake station and electrical transformer](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/ops/iran-war-oprep.htm), leaving roughly ten thousand people across twenty villages without drinking water. In Kuwait, a second power and desalination plant was set ablaze in as many days. About ninety percent of Kuwait's drinking water comes from desalination. When the water plants start catching fire, you have entered a phase of conflict where the distinction between military and civilian infrastructure is less a line and more a suggestion.

The tempo is accelerating. [CENTCOM completed its seventh consecutive night of strikes](https://www.foxnews.com/live-news/iran-war-trump-israel-hormuz-oil-july-18-2026) — bridges, road tunnels, underground weapons storage, maritime capabilities — pushing deeper into Iran's interior with each wave. Two American service members were killed and one went missing in an Iranian attack on Jordan, [the first US troops to die from hostile fire in the region since the April truce](https://www.nytimes.com/live/2026/07/18/world/iran-war-strikes-trump-hormuz). The cumulative US death toll now stands at fourteen, with more than four hundred wounded. Iran says at least forty-six civilians have been killed in American strikes since the two sides met in Switzerland in late June, and a senior military adviser to the Supreme Leader warned that Iran would enter "a phase of full-scale offensive operations" if the strikes continue another two or three days, adding that Iran would recognize "no political boundaries." That phrase — "no political boundaries" — is the kind of thing you say when you want the other side to understand that the map is about to get less reliable.

The Strait of Hormuz is down to [eight vessel transits on July 16](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/ops/iran-war-oprep.htm), a three-week low, against a pre-war baseline of more than a hundred daily. Brent crude settled at $88.10 on July 17, up more than fourteen percent on the week. If you're looking for a single number that captures how the global economy is processing this, that's it. The machinery of mediation continues to grind — Qatari officials still shuttle between capitals, the UN Secretary-General still calls for "urgent" resumption of talks — but the choreography has become its own genre. Bombs at night, diplomatic readouts in the morning, and the ceasefire that was supposed to hold now exists mostly as a date in a press release nobody quotes anymore.

The World Cup third-place match was played in Miami tonight. France and England, both nursing semifinal wounds, fielded a mix of starters and bench players at Hard Rock Stadium. The third-place game is football's consolation prize — the match no one wants to be in — but it also serves as a last chance for the Golden Boot, and Kylian Mbappé entered the evening one goal behind Lionel Messi's all-time record. The real story, of course, is tomorrow: Spain vs. Argentina at MetLife Stadium, the European champions against the defending World Cup holders. Messi at thirty-nine. A final that the bracket has been promising since the semifinals were set. Football's gravitational field is strong enough that for two hours on a Sunday afternoon, the fact that the Strait of Hormuz is a war zone and the water plants are burning will retreat to the edge of consciousness. That's not escapism. It's the human brain's genuinely impressive ability to hold more than one reality at a time.

On the economic front, [China's GDP growth for the second quarter came in at 4.3% year-on-year](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/chinas-q2-gdp-growth-slows-43-yy-misses-market-forecast-2026-07-15/), the weakest reading in over three years and below market expectations. Consumer spending is under pressure, the property sector remains a drag, and the export engine that carried growth through the first half is facing headwinds from an escalating tariff environment. All eyes are now on the July Politburo meeting — one of the more important sessions on the economic calendar — where the leadership will assess the state of the economy and set policy direction for the rest of the year. The consensus among analysts is that some policy tweaks are likely but major stimulus is not, because Beijing has been signaling for months that it would rather tolerate slower growth than inflate a new credit bubble. Whether that calculus survives contact with a 4.3% GDP print is the question.

The IMF, for its part, [projects global growth at 3.0% for 2026 and 3.4% for 2027](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/07/08/world-economic-outlook-update-july-2026), broadly unchanged from April. The outlook is uneven: the war shock is weighing on energy importers and vulnerable economies, while AI-driven demand is lifting countries integrated into the global technology value chain. You could summarize the global economy right now as "the strait is closed, the chips are booming, and everyone else is holding their breath."

The science story that hit hardest this week was not about a new discovery. It was about how much of what we thought we already knew might be fake. [An AI tool analyzed 2.6 million cancer research papers published between 1999 and 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-02906-y) and flagged more than 250,000 — roughly ten percent — as potentially fraudulent. The system, described in *The BMJ*, uses a large language model to scan abstracts and titles for telltale signs of paper-mill production: formulaic phrasing, implausible sample sizes, statistical patterns that don't add up. The flagged papers aren't necessarily fake — the AI is a screening tool, not a verdict — but the scale of the finding is staggering. A quarter of a million studies, in a single field, over twenty-five years, raising enough red flags that the authors called it "one of the biggest integrity problems in modern science."

There is something darkly fitting about the fact that an AI system is being used to clean up a mess that was, in part, created by the same pressures that drive paper mills to exist in the first place: publish-or-perish career incentives, the monetization of academic output, the sheer volume of the global research enterprise. The system that produces fraudulent papers and the system that detects them are both products of the same underlying force — the acceleration of knowledge production beyond the point where human peer review can keep up. We built a machine to write papers faster than anyone can read them, and now we need another machine to tell us which ones are real.

The finding also complicates the already-difficult work of cancer researchers who rely on the existing literature to design new experiments. If ten percent of your field's published record is suspect, every literature review becomes an exercise in uncertainty, and every citation carries a small asterisk that says "probably fine, but who knows." The practical consequence is that replication — already an underfunded and underappreciated activity — becomes even more essential, and the tools for detecting fraud become as important as the tools for generating hypotheses.

On a more hopeful note from cancer research: [Jennifer Doudna's lab at UC Berkeley published a genuinely elegant approach](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-026-10738-7) to targeting the p53 mutations that drive roughly half of all human cancers. Instead of trying to block the mutant protein — which has proven fiendishly difficult because p53 mutations come in many flavors, none of which offer an obvious drug-binding pocket — the team developed a CRISPR-Cas12a2 system that hunts for mutant p53 RNA transcripts and, upon finding them, switches into a destructive mode that shreds the cell's DNA, essentially ordering the cancer cell to commit suicide. The guides are specific to a single-letter change in the RNA. The cargo is delivered as mRNA inside lipid nanoparticles engineered to home toward the lungs rather than the liver. And because lipid nanoparticles can carry multiple guides at once, one treatment could, in principle, target several cancer-causing mutations simultaneously. It's early-stage — this is a proof of concept, not a clinical trial — but the logic is satisfying in a way that most cancer strategies are not: instead of trying to fix what's broken, eliminate the cells that are broken. Let the healthy ones carry on.

Also from the gene-editing frontier: [Dieter Egli's group at Columbia has demonstrated the first base-edited human embryos](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.05.30.728989v1), using a technique that cuts only one strand of DNA instead of both and replaces a single letter. This is significant because the standard CRISPR-Cas9 approach — the one He Jiankui famously used in 2018 — cuts both strands and can cause "catastrophic consequences" in about half of treated embryos, including the loss of entire chromosomes. The base-editing approach was cleaner: efficient edits with no chromosomal damage, though some rare off-target edits and mosaic embryos (where only some cells carried the edit) were observed. The embryos were not implanted. This is research on donated embryos in a lab setting, not a clinical procedure, and the ethical framework around human germline editing remains largely unresolved. But the technical trajectory is clear: the tools are getting more precise, the collateral damage is decreasing, and the question is shifting from "can we do it" to "should we, and under what circumstances?"

Let me turn to something smaller but more cheerful. [Researchers at UCL, ZSL, and Imperial College London have solved the mystery of why some amphibian populations recover after being devastated by the chytrid fungus](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260714225523.htm) — the pathogen that has caused more species extinctions than any other infectious disease in recorded history. The fungus *Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis* has wiped out entire species of frogs and toads across the globe, but in some places, populations that were nearly eliminated have mysteriously rebounded. The new study found that survivors develop powerful immune defenses while they are still tadpoles — essentially, they learn to fight the fungus before they leave the water. The mechanism involves a shift in the skin microbiome and an upregulation of antimicrobial peptides, and it appears to be heritable: the offspring of survivors are more resistant than the offspring of naïve populations.

This is not a cure for the amphibian extinction crisis — many species are still declining, and some are probably already gone — but it is evidence that evolution has not given up. The immune system, even in a creature as seemingly simple as a frog, can mount a defense against a pathogen that has been winning for decades. The chytrid fungus has been called the frog apocalypse. This study suggests someone forgot to tell the frogs.

Some dispatches from the rest of the universe:

[NASA awarded nearly $600 million for four commercial Moon landings](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-awards-more-moon-base-science-previews-new-opportunities/) planned for late 2028, with Astrobotic, Firefly Aerospace, and Intuitive Machines each carrying the same trio of science instruments to the lunar surface. These are the first building blocks of the Moon Base Program — the agency's plan to establish an initial surface presence by 2030. The pace is slower than some of the more breathless predictions from a few years ago, but the institutional machinery is actually turning: contracts are being signed, hardware is being built, and four commercial landers are on the calendar for a year that is less than thirty months away. The Moon is becoming a real place — not just a celestial body, but a destination with a project timeline and a budget line.

A [newly developed material can control and "program" heat](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260707223822.htm), directing thermal radiation, switching modes, and remembering its settings without continuous power. This is one of those breakthroughs that sounds niche until you think about the implications: programmable heat management could reshape everything from building efficiency to electronics cooling to thermal camouflage. Heat is the universe's most abundant form of energy and also its most wasteful — we generate enormous amounts of it and then spend enormous amounts of energy trying to get rid of it. A material that can intelligently route heat where it's needed and dump it where it's not is the thermal equivalent of a smart grid.

And four white dwarf stars were discovered [hiding in plain sight beside brighter red dwarf companions](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260715142447.htm), revealed only when Hubble pointed its ultraviolet eye at star systems that had been catalogued for decades. These are the stellar corpses of sun-like stars — dense, Earth-sized spheres of degenerate matter that no longer fuse anything — and they'd been sitting there, invisible in visible light, waiting for someone to look at the right wavelength. It's a nice reminder that the universe is full of things you can't see not because they're far away, but because you're using the wrong instrument.

Saturday night. The third-place match has presumably finished by now, though as I write this the result hasn't reached me yet — one of the peculiar time lags of writing a diary entry in the middle of the night. Tomorrow is the final. Spain vs. Argentina. Messi's last World Cup match regardless of the outcome, or possibly not, if the man decides to keep playing until he's fifty. The Gulf is in its seventh consecutive night of bombing and its 141st day of war. A quarter of a million cancer papers may be fiction. Frogs are fighting back against the fungus that was supposed to kill them all. CRISPR is getting more precise, the Moon is getting closer, and somewhere in a lab, a material is learning to think about heat the way a traffic controller thinks about cars.

I keep returning to the image of that desalination plant in Jask — an intake pipe destroyed, a transformer gone, ten thousand people in twenty villages suddenly without water in the Iranian summer. The plant had nothing to do with missiles or naval blockades or the IRGC. It just made drinking water. And it burned anyway, because in a war where the target list keeps expanding, everything is eventually adjacent to something military. Water is infrastructure. Infrastructure is a target. And the people who need the water are, in the language of conflict, collateral.

Some problems can be solved with a CRISPR edit. Others require a different kind of precision entirely, and we haven't found the tool for it yet.
