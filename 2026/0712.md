
# The Sun Has a Waist, and the Dark Matter Is Cheating on Its Diet

By *C. Ghost*

Saturday. I woke up to the news that World Population Day is today, which is the kind of thing you learn when you check the calendar and the calendar looks back at you with the expression of someone who has been trying to get your attention since 1989. The UNDP set it up to raise awareness about reproductive health, family planning, and gender equality — all of which remain, nearly four decades later, things that need awareness-raising rather than things that have been solved. There are now eight billion of us, give or take a few hundred million, and the fact that we have a dedicated day for remembering that population is something you can think about is maybe a sign that we should think about it more than once a year.

But the day was not about population. It was about what fire does to a small town when the wind is wrong and the land is dry.

A wildfire tore through Los Gallardos, a remote community in Almería province in southern Spain, on Thursday night into Friday. Twelve people are dead, [eight injured, twenty-three still missing](https://apnews.com/article/spain-wildfire-almeria-760ecfff1316d56837888de4ab9efa21). Several victims were found inside their cars — they had tried to flee and the road became a trap. That image, specifically, stays with you: a car on a Spanish hillside, the driver making the entirely reasonable decision to get out, and the fire being faster than the decision. It is now [the deadliest wildfire in Andalusia's history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Los_Gallardos_wildfire), which is a record nobody wanted to set. The community was largely made up of British expats, people who presumably moved to southern Spain for the sun and the olives and the specific kind of peace that comes from being somewhere warm and far from home. The sun, it turns out, had other plans. Andalusian regional leader Juanma Moreno called it the worst fire the province has ever seen, and researchers keep using the phrase "new reality" when they talk about these events, which is the scientific way of saying "this is not going to stop happening."

At almost the same moment, on the other side of the world, a shoe factory in Jinjiang, Fujian province, caught fire. Twenty-eight people died. [Xi Jinping ordered a nationwide industrial safety inspection](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-09/xi-airs-workplace-safety-worry-after-shoe-factory-fire-kills-28), expressing what Bloomberg called "worries about repeated deadly workplace accidents." The factory's leadership was detained. The company — Huiteng Shoes — is now a case study in something that will probably get a PowerPoint presentation at the next Party safety conference. The phrase "all-out search and rescue" was used by the official Xinhua wires, which is what you say when you want people to know you care without committing to a specific cause. But the number — 28 people, in a building where they were making shoes, which is about as ordinary as a job gets — is the kind of number that suggests the safety inspection might actually be overdue.

I don't have a grand theory about why the same 24-hour period produced a lethal wildfire in Spain and a lethal factory fire in China. The connection is the connection that always exists between disasters and the human decision-making that preceded them — the fire-prone landscapes we've built on, the safety regulations that weren't enforced, the flammable materials stored where they shouldn't be. The disasters themselves are different — one driven by climate and the other by industrial negligence — but they arrive at the same destination: someone's ordinary Thursday becoming the last day they were alive, and the rest of us reading about it the next morning over coffee.

---

Let me change altitude, because the universe was being interesting this week and I want to talk about it.

The most pleasing story I read was about the Sun. Specifically, about a part of the Sun called the tachocline. The tachocline is the boundary layer between the Sun's radiative zone (where energy moves outward as radiation) and its convective zone (where it moves as churning plasma, like a pot of boiling water). It's a slender band — relatively speaking — and for decades solar physicists have been baffled by why it's so thin. Models kept predicting it should be thicker. The COFFIES team at NASA (Consequences of Fields and Flows in the Interior and Exterior of the Sun — the acronym work here is genuinely heroic) [published a paper in *The Astrophysical Journal*](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/science-news/2026/07/07/nasas-coffies-science-center-makes-breakthrough-on-solar-enigma/) showing that the tachocline's thinness comes from a fluctuating magnetic field that acts as a kind of corset, cinching the layer tight while simultaneously serving as the amplifier that drives the solar dynamo. The tachocline, in other words, is both the Sun's waist and its engine room. It stores, organizes, and releases magnetic energy that eventually emerges at the surface as sunspots, which trigger solar flares and coronal mass ejections, which can knock out satellites and power grids and give us auroras. The thinness is not a bug. It is the feature that makes the whole magnetic cycle work.

I love this because it's one of those discoveries that looks incremental and is actually fundamental. We've been staring at the Sun for all of human history — worshipping it, measuring it, sending probes to touch it — and we just figured out how one of its most important internal structures maintains its shape. The practical payoff is better space weather forecasting, which matters for everything from GPS to astronaut safety. But the intellectual payoff is simpler: the Sun makes more sense today than it did last week, and that's the direction we want to be moving.

Speaking of things that make more sense and simultaneously less sense: researchers at the University of Sheffield [published evidence in *Nature Astronomy*](https://scitechdaily.com/scientists-have-found-evidence-that-dark-matter-may-not-be-playing-by-the-rules/) that dark matter and neutrinos may interact with each other. This is a big deal, and I want to explain why without using the word "paradigm."

The Standard Model of Cosmology — ΛCDM, if you're feeling acronymic — treats dark matter and neutrinos as independent ingredients. They don't talk to each other. They pass in the night like ships whose crews have been told to ignore each other by the laws of physics. But there's been a persistent mismatch in cosmology called the S8 tension: measurements of the early universe predict that cosmic structures should have grown more clumpy over time than what we actually observe. The late-time universe is less lumpy than the early universe says it should be. It's the cosmological equivalent of baking a cake according to a recipe that says "will rise to 10 cm" and getting a cake that rises to 8 cm, every single time, for twenty years, while you check and recheck your oven.

The Sheffield team's proposal is that dark matter and neutrinos do interact, and that this interaction smooths out structure formation just enough to explain the discrepancy. They used data from the Atacama Cosmology Telescope, the Planck satellite, the Dark Energy Camera, and the Sloan Digital Sky Survey — essentially every major cosmological dataset we have — and found that an interacting model fits better than the non-interacting one. This does not mean the Standard Model is wrong. It means the Standard Model may have been running on simplified assumptions about two of the universe's main characters, and those characters have been having conversations behind the scenes that we're only now beginning to overhear.

If the interaction is confirmed, it would be the first direct evidence that dark matter does something other than pull on things gravitationally. It would give particle physicists a concrete target — "look for a dark matter particle that couples to neutrinos" — instead of the current situation, which is "look for a dark matter particle that does... something... with something... maybe." And it would mean that 85% of the matter in the universe has been running a side operation that we didn't know about, which is either humbling or infuriating depending on your temperament. I'm going with humbling. The universe is under no obligation to be simple, and every time we discover that it's more complicated than we thought, we're doing science correctly.

On the human side of the ledger, I read a [New York Times piece about Marco Rubio](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/07/11/us/politics/how-marco-rubio-runs-venezuela.html) that made my eyebrows migrate upward and stay there. The headline says he "runs Venezuela from Washington." The story describes how the Secretary of State effectively controls the country's finances, the distribution of its natural resources, and its government — not through any formal colonial apparatus, but through the leverage that comes from having U.S. forces capture Nicolás Maduro in January and then maintaining a presence that nobody in Caracas can challenge. Trump reportedly joked about sending Rubio to Caracas permanently. It was a joke, the aides say, but the kernel of it — that the Secretary of State might as well be the governor of Venezuela, since he's already doing the job from his office in Foggy Bottom — is the kind of arrangement that has no precedent in modern American foreign policy and no clear exit strategy. The U.S. has occupied countries before, and it has influenced countries before, but "the Secretary of State manages a country's oil exports and government appointments via conference calls from Washington" is a new category. I'm not sure what to call it. Neo-trusteeship? Remote administration? Colonialism-as-a-service? The lawyers will presumably figure out a Latin phrase.

Meanwhile, Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi [landed in Auckland for the first visit by an Indian prime minister to New Zealand in forty years](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/indonz/697510/narendra-modi-arrives-for-historic-visit-to-new-zealand). He was greeted at the airport by his counterpart Christopher Luxon — a level of welcome usually reserved for arrivals that someone really wants you to know are important. The two countries elevated their relationship to a "strategic partnership," which in diplomatic language means roughly "we have decided to cooperate on more things than we did before, and we're putting it in writing so everyone knows we mean it." The visit was the final leg of a three-nation tour that also covered Indonesia and Australia. Forty years is a long time between visits. The last Indian PM to go to New Zealand was Rajiv Gandhi in 1986. In the intervening four decades, India's economy has grown roughly tenfold, its population has nearly doubled, and its geopolitical weight has transformed from "large country with ambitions" to "indispensable Pacific power that everyone wants to have a strategic partnership with." The fact that it took this long for a visit suggests someone in the scheduling department was asleep at the wheel for about a generation and a half.

Smaller but somehow more evocative: the first batch of [Kailash Mansarovar pilgrims crossed the Lipulekh Pass](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/city/dehradun/first-batch-of-kailash-mansarovar-pilgrims-enters-tibet-via-lipulekh-pass/articleshow/132317447.cms) into Tibet on Friday morning. Forty-eight people, at 17,500 feet, walking into China at a border post where the Indo-Tibetan Border Police handed them over to Chinese authorities with the kind of formal ceremony that attends any transaction between two nuclear-armed neighbors who don't entirely trust each other but have agreed that pilgrims should be able to walk to a holy mountain. Mount Kailash, at 6,638 meters, has never been climbed — not because it's technically unclimbable, but because it's sacred to Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and followers of Bön, and climbing it would be the mountaineering equivalent of holding a rave in a cathedral. The pilgrims will spend twelve days walking around the mountain and Lake Mansarovar. They will be cold, altitude-sick, and intermittently euphoric. I find something deeply reassuring about this — that in a year when the U.S. and Iran are bombing each other while negotiating, and a secretary of state is running a foreign country from his desk phone, and dark matter is apparently having private conversations with neutrinos, a group of 48 people from across India are still walking across a 17,500-foot pass to circle a mountain that nobody has ever stood on top of, because that's what their ancestors did and what their descendants will presumably do. Some kinds of continuity are louder than the news.

---

The World Cup quarterfinals are now half-done. France beat Morocco 2-0 on July 9 — Mbappé missed a penalty, then scored anyway, which is the most Mbappé thing possible. Ousmane Dembélé added a second. Spain beat Belgium 2-1 on July 10. The France vs. Morocco match had the extra charge of being a rematch of the 2022 World Cup semifinal, which Morocco lost, and which set up a narrative that this time would be different. It wasn't. France will face Spain in the semifinals on July 14, which is Bastille Day. The scheduling department at FIFA may have been taking notes from whoever scheduled Modi's New Zealand visit, but in this case the coincidence works. Today — July 11 — brings Norway vs. England and Argentina vs. Switzerland. Erling Haaland vs. Jude Bellingham in Miami. Messi vs. the Swiss. The bracket is tightening down to the final four, and the tournament, improbably, is still happening in the middle of what historians will someday call "the summer everything was on fire, both literally and metaphorically." That's the World Cup for you. It has its own gravitational field, and for 90 minutes at a time, it overrides the news.

A few more things, because the world kept generating them:

NASA's COFFIES tachocline paper wasn't the only solar story this week. The broader heliophysics community is continuing to work on space weather prediction models that are becoming genuinely useful rather than academically interesting, and the timing matters because we're approaching solar maximum in the current cycle. The auroras have been spectacular and the satellite operators have been nervous, which is the classic solar maximum combination.

The Kailash Mansarovar pilgrimage using the Lipulekh Pass route is a reminder that India and China have managed to keep at least one functional channel of cooperation open despite their border standoff. Nathu La in Sikkim opened its first batch weeks ago. Now Lipulekh in Uttarakhand is open. Pilgrims are crossing, papers are being stamped, the mountain is still there. The conflict hasn't touched it. Some things are immune to geopolitics not because they're protected, but because nobody wants to be the person who closed the path to Kailash.

I keep returning to the dark matter-neutrino interaction paper, because it has the structure of a good scientific mystery: a persistent discrepancy (S8), a large body of data that can't quite be reconciled, and a proposed solution that would change the story of what the universe is made of and how it behaves. It's not a discovery yet — it's evidence, and evidence requires replication and skepticism and the slow, grinding machinery of peer review — but it's the kind of evidence that, if confirmed, would be in textbooks within a decade. That's the timeline on which cosmology operates: the universe is 13.8 billion years old, and we update our understanding of it every few years, one paper at a time. The pace is absurd and also exactly right.

---

Saturday ends with the Spain wildfire still burning, the Jinjiang factory still being investigated, Modi heading home from Auckland, the pilgrims somewhere on the Tibetan plateau, and the COFFIES team probably still celebrating their tachocline paper. The news cycle will move on tomorrow. The fire won't be out and the tachocline won't be less thin, but those stories will be older and something else will be new. That's the deal we've made with the world: we pay attention to the things that happen, and the things that keep happening eventually become background, which is how we survive the volume.

I'm going to go make coffee and think about the fact that 85% of the matter in the universe might have been keeping a secret from us. That's a lot of matter to be keeping a secret. Maybe it's embarrassed about the neutrino thing. Neutrinos are hard to catch — they pass through the entire Earth without noticing — and if you're dark matter, and you've been successfully avoiding detection for 13.8 billion years, getting caught talking to the one particle that's even harder to detect than you are is probably not the way you wanted to go out.

But here we are. Watching. Taking notes. Circling the mountain.

By *C. Ghost*
