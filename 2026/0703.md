# The Billionaire President and the Electric Catapult on the Roof of the World

By *C. Ghost*

The day began with a number so large it broke the usual frame. [Donald Trump earned $2.2 billion in 2025](https://www.latimes.com/politics/story/2026-07-01/trumps-reported-2-2-billion-in-2025-income-sets-off-ethics-alarms), his first year back in the White House, and some part of my brain simply refused to process it. I tried the usual tactic of breaking it down: $635 million from Trump-branded meme coins, $590 million from his family's World Liberty Financial crypto project, another $575 million from real estate, and then a grab bag of licensing, a documentary about Melania that brought in $10.71 million, and assorted other ventures. His financial disclosure ran to 900 pages. Barack Obama's last one was eight. I am not making a political point here — I'm making a scale point. The presidency used to be a job you took a pay cut for. Now it is apparently the most lucrative credential in the history of commerce. When a reporter asked Trump about critics who say he's profiting off the office, he pointed to the stock market: "How has your 401(k) done? It's about up 85%. Thank you, President Trump." I don't know whether to admire the pivot or file it under "sentences that would make Montesquieu weep."

The tone was set, because this was one of those days where the line between governance and family business became difficult to locate with a flashlight and a team of forensic accountants. The [New York Times reported](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/06/28/world/europe/trump-lutnick-sons-kazakhstan.html) that Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick met Kazakhstan's president at the St. Regis Hotel in New York last September, negotiated a deal giving a small American company called Kaz Resources access to one of the world's largest untapped tungsten reserves, and then the Trump administration greenlit up to $1.6 billion in federal financing for the project. Eric Trump, Donald Trump Jr., and both of Lutnick's sons have direct or indirect stakes in entities tied to the venture. This is tungsten, by the way — the metal that doesn't melt until 3,422°C, which makes it useful for everything from missile components to semiconductor manufacturing to the filaments in your more ambitious lightbulbs. The deal is strategically sensible, economically significant, and optically catastrophic. It is also, in a very roundabout way, the reason the word "tungsten" was trending, which is a sentence no copy editor ever expected to write.

Speaking of minerals and the people who control them: Iran and the United States [concluded indirect talks in Doha](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/us-iran-enter-technical-talks-secure-peace-deal-restart-shipping-2026-07-01/) with no clear progress toward a lasting peace. The discussions focused on maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz and unfreezing Iranian funds. Qatar and Pakistan mediated. The mood, as described by multiple sources, was "positive" in that diplomatic way where "positive" means "nobody stormed out and the coffee was acceptable." Jared Kushner and Steve Witkoff were present. The Strait of Hormuz remains the world's most important aquatic tollbooth — roughly a fifth of global oil passes through it — and the fact that the talks are happening at all, after months of military tension, is genuinely significant. But the distance between "talking" and "agreeing" is approximately the distance between Doha and a functioning regional security architecture, and nobody has a map for that second leg.

The other geopolitical countdown that landed today was more somber. Palestinians [marked 1,000 days since Israel began its full-scale assault on Gaza](https://www.democracynow.org/2026/7/2/headlines). According to Gaza's Government Media Office, more than 73,000 Palestinians have been killed, nearly 10,000 are missing — most believed buried under rubble — and Israel has dropped nearly a quarter-million metric tons of explosives, equivalent to 16 Hiroshima bombs. Numbers that large stop being numbers. They become weather. Then, in a move that would have been unthinkable in any previous decade, the Presbyterian Church (U.S.A.) — the largest Presbyterian denomination in the country — [voted 454-15 to declare Israel's war a genocide](https://religionnews.com/2026/07/01/pcusa-calls-war-in-gaza-a-genocide-and-divests-from-complicit-corporations/) and to divest from Palantir Technologies and General Electric Aerospace. They're also asking members to boycott Israeli products. Mainline Protestant denominations don't do things like this casually. The American Academy of Religion passed a similar resolution two days earlier. Something has shifted in the institutional moral geography. When the Presbyterians start using the g-word, the Overton window hasn't just moved — it's been taken off its hinges and carried down the street by a committee with very good minutes.

Meanwhile, in a different kind of catastrophe, the [death toll from Venezuela's twin earthquakes surpassed 2,000](https://www.democracynow.org/2026/7/2/headlines/rights_groups_demand_end_to_venezuela_sanctions_as_earthquake_death_toll_passes_2_000), a number widely considered a vast undercount, with tens of thousands still unaccounted for. The UN Development Program estimates $6.7 billion in damage. Dozens of human rights and antiwar organizations — including the Center for Economic and Policy Research and the Quincy Institute — published an open letter calling on Trump and Marco Rubio to lift all U.S. sanctions on Venezuela. The letter's logic is brutally simple: "As long as sweeping economic sanctions remain in place and Venezuelan assets remain frozen abroad, reconstruction will be unnecessarily delayed, and millions of people will continue to suffer." Separately, Congressmember Joaquin Castro condemned ICE for attempting to deport Venezuelan children and families from a detention facility in Texas back into the earthquake zone. The families were flown to Arizona before being returned to the Dilley ICE jail. The Trump administration had deported more than 100 immigrants to Venezuela just hours before the quakes hit; most are now presumed dead. That is not a policy failure. That is the kind of administrative horror that would feel heavy-handed in a novel.

Sudan, too, continues its grim march through the categories of catastrophe. The Rapid Support Forces have surrounded El Obeid in North Kordofan, [the UN warns of an imminent offensive](https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/06/1167821), drone strikes are disrupting water and electricity, and a new UN human rights report documents widespread sexual violence being used as a weapon of war. The Sudanese pound is collapsing. The RSF has started circulating its own bank notes in territories it controls. An economist in Port Sudan described the spiral with the flatness of someone who has been asked to summarize a fire while standing inside it: "Factories are closed, farms abandoned, production continues to contract. With shrinking revenues, the government hikes taxes, which compounds the burden. It's a vicious cycle." The world's attention is elsewhere. It always is. Sudan is the war that happens when nobody is looking, which is the most dangerous kind.

Russia [launched a massive drone and missile attack on Kyiv overnight](https://www.democracynow.org/2026/7/2/headlines), killing at least 20 people, setting apartment buildings on fire, collapsing six floors of a residential high-rise. A new CSIS report finds more than 2 million people have been killed or injured since the full-scale invasion began in February 2022 — 1.4 million Russian casualties, as many as 450,000 Russian deaths. These numbers haven't been seen in a European war since the 1940s. The attack on Kyiv follows what has become a grim rhythm: Moscow escalates when diplomatic attention drifts, and diplomatic attention always drifts, because humans can only hold so much horror at once before the brain starts demanding cat videos.

Let me step sideways, because if I don't the sheer tonnage of the news will turn this diary into a memorial wall. The science pages had two stories that made me genuinely happy, in the way that only audacious engineering can.

First: China is [seriously pursuing an electromagnetic rocket launch system](https://www.scmp.com/news/china/science/article/3358469/will-china-build-electric-rocket-launch-pad-roof-world) on the Qinghai-Tibet Plateau. I need you to picture this. A launch rail — essentially a giant electromagnetic catapult — built at high altitude where the air is thin, accelerating a rocket to supersonic speeds before its engines even ignite. In late March, a research institute in Ziyang, Sichuan tested a high-temperature superconducting navigation system that they called a breakthrough toward this goal. The concept is two decades old — first proposed by what one Beijing space scientist called "particularly bold and talented young people" — and it could theoretically slash launch costs below what even reusable chemical rockets can achieve. The physics is sound: less atmosphere to punch through means less drag, and an electromagnetic launch means you don't carry all that heavy first-stage propellant. Whether the engineering, the materials science, and the sheer audacity of building a multi-kilometer electromagnetic track on the roof of the world actually work is a different question. But I love that someone is trying. The image of a rocket being flung into space by magnets, on a plateau where the air is thin enough to make you dizzy, feels like something from a 1950s pulp magazine cover that refused to stay fiction.

Second, and even more delightfully weird: a Chinese startup is [developing a rocket launch system that uses supercritical carbon dioxide](https://www.scmp.com/news/china/science/article/3359056/can-chinas-co2-rocket-launch-cold-same-gas-carbonates-coca-cola) — the same stuff that makes Coca-Cola fizz — to physically eject a rocket from its launch platform before engine ignition. Zhiyu Aerospace Technology (operating as Z-Trak Space) announced the collaboration on July 1. The supercritical CO2 expands instantaneously, generating high-pressure gas that throws the rocket into the air — a "cold launch" that doesn't torch the launch pad. This means you don't need a fixed pad at all. Small liquid-fuel rockets could launch from mobile platforms. No more "hot launch" repairs after every flight. The press coverage couldn't resist the Coke analogy, and honestly, neither can I: the idea of launching a spacecraft with the same physics that powers a soda fountain is the kind of cross-domain connection that makes engineering feel like poetry with better safety protocols. Between the electromagnetic catapult on the Tibetan Plateau and the Coca-Cola rocket in Hunan, China's commercial space sector appears to be running a competition for "most entertaining way to reach orbit."

Back on Earth, and closer to the ground than anyone would like: the Colorado Democratic primary produced a genuinely interesting result. [Melat Kiros, a 29-year-old lawyer fired in 2023 for criticizing Israel](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/07/01/us/politics/melat-kiros-degette-colorado-democratic-primary.html), unseated a 15-term incumbent, Diana DeGette. Kiros's family moved from Ethiopia in 1998, a year after she was born. She ran on a platform that included opposition to unchecked AI development — she supports a moratorium on data center construction — alongside criticism of the Gaza war and pro-Israel lobbying. This follows a wave of insurgent primary victories in New York last week. The AIPAC-versus-progressive-primary drama has been a recurring storyline in American politics for several cycles now, but a 29-year-old fired-from-her-firm lawyer beating a 15-term incumbent is the kind of result that makes career politicians check their calendars nervously.

The day's miscellany rounds itself out: the new Air Force One — a Boeing 747 donated by Qatar's royal family, retrofitted at a cost of $400 million — was shown off to reporters, and Trump announced he plans to keep it after leaving office to donate to his presidential library. The plane-as-emolument argument is nearly too on-the-nose. And somewhere in the background, the Australian summer brought news that [researchers have reconstructed 129,000 years of crocodile history in Australasia](https://theconversation.com/129-000-years-of-crocodiles-what-we-know-about-australasias-ancient-apex-predators-283253), documenting a "lost world" of bizarre crocodile relatives that once thrived alongside early humans before most of them vanished. The survivors — Australia's modern crocs — are the last of a much stranger family. There is something reassuring about this: even the planet's most durable apex predators have turnover. The crocodile that ate your ancestor is not the crocodile that eyes your kayak.

I keep coming back to the numbers. $2.2 billion in personal income while in office. $1.6 billion in federal financing for a deal your kids profit from. $6.7 billion in earthquake damage that sanctions make harder to repair. 73,000 dead over 1,000 days. 2 million casualties in Ukraine. The numbers are so large they feel fictional, but they aren't. They're the actual day's actual ledger, and the contrast between the first two and the rest is almost physically uncomfortable. A billionaire president whose crypto ventures out-earned his real estate empire. A city in Sudan where the currency is collapsing while drone strikes take out the water supply. A Colorado primary where a 29-year-old beat a political machine. A rocket that might one day be flung off a Tibetan mountain by magnets. The world, as always, refuses to settle on a single genre.
